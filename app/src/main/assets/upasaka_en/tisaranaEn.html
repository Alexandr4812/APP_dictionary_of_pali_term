saraṇagamanakathā

Explanation on taking refuge

Idāni tesveva tīsu saraṇagamanesu kosallatthaṃ saraṇaṃ, saraṇagamanaṃ, yo saraṇaṃ gacchati, saraṇagamanappabhedo, saraṇagamanaphalaṃ, saṃkileso, bhedoti ayaṃ vidhi vedi tabbo. so pana idha vuccamāno atibhāriyaṃ vinayanidānaṃ karotīti na vutto.
atthikehi pana papañcasūdaniyaṃ vā majjhimaṭṭhakathāyaṃ bhayabheravasuttavaṇṇanato (ma. ni. aṭṭha. 1.56)
sumaṅgalavilāsiniyaṃ vā dīghanikāyaṭṭhakathāyaṃ (dī. ni. aṭṭha. 1.250)
saraṇavaṇṇanato gahetabboti.

saraṇagamanakathā niṭṭhitā.

 

"Explanation of Taking Refuge
(saraṇagamanakathā):

Now, regarding these three refuges 1, one should understand the following order for gaining skill [in this matter]:

1. what is refuge (saraṇaṃ)

2. what is taking refuge (saraṇagamanaṃ)

3. who takes refuge (yo saraṇaṃ gacchati)

4. different types of taking refuge (saraṇagamanappabhedo)

5. fruits of taking refuge (saraṇagamanaphalaṃ)

6. pollutions [of refuge] (saṃkileso)

7. destruction [of refuge] (bhedo)

However, this is not explained in detail here, as it would make the introduction to the Vinaya too cumbersome. Those who are interested may refer to Papáncasudāni's commentary on the Majjhima Nikaya, the explanation of the Bhayabherava Sutta 2, or to Sumanagalavilasini's commentary on the Digha Nikaya 3, where the explanation of refuge is given.

The explanation of taking refuge is finished.

 

Let us turn to the commentary of the Majjhima-nikaya to the Samanñaphala Sutta: The Fruits of Recluse (DN 2), or more precisely to the last chapter of this sutta.

Excerpt from the sutta:
theravada.ru

“...When this was said, the king of Magadha, Ajatasattu Vedehiputta, said to the Blessed One: “Excellent, lord! Excellent, lord! Just as, lord, one picks up what has fallen, or reveals what is hidden, or shows the way to the lost, or sets up a butter lamp in the darkness so that those endowed with sight may discern images, so has the Blessed One taught the truth with many instructions. And so, lord, I go as a refuge to the Blessed One, and to the dharma, and to the sangha of monks. May the Blessed One accept me as a devoted layman, having taken refuge here from now on and for life…”

 

Commentary on the passage from the sutta:

Saraṇagamanakathā idāni tesu saraṇagamanesu kosallatthaṃ saraṇaṃ, saraṇagamanaṃ, yo ca saraṇaṃ gacchati, saraṇagamanappabhedo, saraṇagamanaphalaṃ, saṅkileso, bhedoti, ayaṃ vidhi veditabbo. seyyathidaṃ – saraṇatthato tāva hiṃsatīti saraṇaṃ. saraṇagatānaṃ teneva saraṇagamanena bhayaṃ santāsaṃ dukkhaṃ duggatiparikilesaṃ hanati vināsetīti attho, ratanattayassevetaṃ adhivacanaṃ. vā hite pavattanena ahitā ca nivattanena sattānaṃ bhayaṃ hiṃsati buddho. bhavakantārā uttāraṇena assāsadānena ca dhammo, appakānampi kārānaṃ vipulaphalapaṭilābhakaraṇena saṅgho. tasmā imināpi pariyāyena ratanattayaṃ saraṇaṃ. tappasādataggarutāhi vihatakileso tapparāyaṇatākārappavatto cittuppādo saraṇagamanaṃ. taṃ samaṅgīsatto saraṇaṃ gacchati. vuttappakārena cittuppādena etāni me tīṇi ratanāni saraṇaṃ, etāni parāyaṇanti evaṃ upetīti attho. evaṃ tāva saraṇaṃ, saraṇagamanaṃ, yo ca saraṇaṃ gacchati, idaṃ tayaṃ veditabbaṃ.atha saraṇagamanappabhede pana duvidhaṃ saraṇagamanaṃ – lokuttaraṃ lokiyañca. tattha lokuttaraṃ diṭṭhasaccānaṃ maggakkhaṇe saraṇagamanupakkilesasamucchedena ārammaṇato nibbānārammaṇaṃ hutvā kiccato sakalepi ratanattaye ijjhati. lokiyaṃ puthujjanānaṃ saraṇagamanupakkilesavikkhambhanena ārammaṇato buddhādiguṇārammaṇaṃ hutvā ijjhati. taṃ atthato buddhādīsu vatthūsu saddhāpaṭilābho saddhāmūlikā ca sammādiṭṭhi dasasu puññakiriyavatthūsu diṭṭhijukammanti vuccati. tayidaṃ catudhā vattati – attasanniyyātanena, tapparāyaṇatāya, sissabhāvūpagamanena, paṇipātenāti.

 

Now one should understand this order regarding taking refuge:

1. what is refuge (saraṇaṃ),

2. what is taking refuge (saraṇagamanaṃ),

3. who takes refuge (yo saraṇaṃ gacchati),

4. various types of taking refuge (saraṇagamanappabhedo),

5. the fruits of taking refuge (saraṇagamanaphalaṃ),

6. pollution (saṅkileso) and

7. destruction (bhedo).

First of all, regarding the meaning of the word "refuge"

It is so called because it eliminates (hiṃsati) 4[dangers]. The meaning is that by taking refuge it destroys and dispels fear, anxiety, suffering and misfortunes of the bad states for those who have taken refuge. This is the designation of the Three Jewels.

The Buddha removes the fear of beings by directing them toward what is good and away from what is bad. The Dhamma [removes fear] by crossing the desert of existence by giving consolation. The Sangha [removes fear] by obtaining great fruits even from small actions [in relation to the Sangha].
Therefore, in this sense, the Three Jewels are a refuge.

Taking refuge

It is the state of mind that arises from faith in them and reverence for them, free from defilements, and [the mind] turns to them as a refuge.

 

Who takes refuge?


A being endowed with this [state of mind] takes refuge. The meaning is that through this state of mind he comes [to understand]: "These three jewels are my refuge, they are my support."

 

As for the types of taking refuge, there are two types:



Supra-mundane (lokuttaraṃ)
Mundane (lokiyañca)

Of these, the supra-mundane occurs at the moment of the path in those who have seen the truth, through the complete eradication of defilements by taking refuge, having Nibbana as the object, and in function is carried out in relation to all the Three Jewels.

The mundane 5is accomplished in ordinary people through suppressing the defilements by taking refuge, having the qualities of the Buddha and other [Jewels] as the object. In essence, this is called gaining faith in the objects - the Buddha and others, the right view based on faith, and correcting the views among the ten foundations of wholesome actions.

This happens in four ways:


through self-denial (attasanniyyātanena)
through taking refuge (tapparāyaṇatāya)
through becoming a disciple (sissabhāvūpagamanena)
through offering respects (paṇipātenāti) 6.

 

tattha attasanniyyātanaṃ nāma – "ajjādiṃ katvā ahaṃ attānaṃ buddhassa niyyātemi, dhammassa, saṅghassā"ti evaṃ buddhādīnaṃ attapariccajanaṃ. tapparāyaṇatā nāma "ajjādiṃ katvā 'ahaṃ buddhaparāyaṇo, dhammaparāyaṇo, saṅghaparāyaṇo'ti. maṃ dhārethā"ti evaṃ tapparāyaṇabhāvo. sissabhāvūpagamanaṃ nāma – "ajjādiṃ katvā – 'ahaṃ buddhassa antevāsiko, dhammassa, saṅghassa antevāsiko'ti maṃ dhārethā"ti evaṃ sissabhāvūpagamo. paṇipāto nāma – "ajjādiṃ katvā ahaṃ abhivādanapaccuṭṭhānaañjalikammasāmīcikammaṃ buddhādīnaṃyeva tiṇṇaṃ vatthūnaṃ karomī'ti maṃ dhārethā"ti evaṃ buddhādīsu paramanipaccākāro. imesañhi catunnaṃ ākārānaṃ aññatarampi karontena gahitaṃyeva hoti saraṇaṃ. api ca bhagavato attānaṃ pariccajāmi, dhammassa, saṅghassa, attānaṃ pariccajāmi, jīvitaṃ pariccajāmi, pariccattoyeva me attā, pariccattaṃyeva jīvitaṃ, jīvitapariyantikaṃ buddhaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi, buddho me saraṇaṃ leṇaṃ

 

Of these, self-entrustment (attasanniyyātanaṃ) means giving oneself to the Buddha and the other [Jewels] in this way: “Starting from today, I entrust myself to the Buddha, Dhamma, and Sangha.”
idāni vācāya attasanniyyātanaṃ karoti. maggavasena vā sanniyyātanasaraṇataṃ acalasaraṇataṃ patto, taṃ paresaṃ vācāya pākaṭaṃ karonto paṇipātasaraṇagamanaṃ gacchati. cakkhumāti bhagavā pakatidibbapaññāsamantabuddhacakkhūhi pañcahi cakkhūhi cakkhumā. taṃ ālapanto āha – "saraṇaṃ taṃ upema cakkhumā"ti. "satthā no hohi tuvaṃ mahāmunī"ti idaṃ pana vacanaṃ sissabhāvūpagamanenāpi saraṇagamanaṃ pūretuṃ bhaṇati, gopī ca ahañca assavā, brahmacariyaṃ sugate carāmaseti idaṃ samādānavasena.
"To you we go for refuge," with these words he expresses his complete surrender (attasanniyyātanaṃ). Although refuge has already been achieved through understanding the path, here he asserts his devotion through words. In this way he makes it clear to others, emphasizing his refuge in reverential adoration.
The Blessed One, who has five types of sight (pañcahi cakkhūhi): ordinary sight (pakati), divine sight (dibba), wisdom sight (paññā), omniscience (samantabuddhacakkhu), is referred to as the "sighted one." Addressing him, Dhaniya says,
"To you we go for refuge, O sighted one!"
Then he adds,
"Be our Teacher, O Great Sage!"
With these words he establishes himself as the Blessed One's disciple, completing his taking of refuge. He also says:
"We, both the cowherd girl and I, will follow the precepts of noble life under your guidance, O Blessed One."

 

Taking refuge (tapparāyaṇatā) means the state of taking them for refuge in this way: “From today onwards, know me as one who has taken refuge in the Buddha, taken refuge in the Dhamma, taken refuge in the Sangha.”

Becoming a disciple (sissabhāvūpagamanaṃ) means taking the position of a disciple in this way: “From today onwards, know me as a disciple of the Buddha, a disciple of the Dhamma, a disciple of the Sangha.”

Prostration (paṇipāto) means the highest expression of humility towards the Buddha and the other [Jewels] in this way: “From today onwards, know me as one who performs salutation, standing up, respectfully joining the palms and other appropriate actions only towards these three objects – the Buddha and the other [Jewels].”

Refuge is considered taken even if any one of these four methods is performed.

Moreover [one can say]: “I give myself to the Blessed One, to the Dhamma and the Sangha, I give myself, I give my life. My self is completely given, my life is completely given. I take refuge in the Buddha until the end of my life, the Buddha is my refuge and shelter.

 

tāṇanti, evampi attasanniyyātanaṃ veditabbaṃ. "satthārañca vatāhaṃ passeyyaṃ, bhagavantameva passeyyaṃ, sugatañca vatāhaṃ passeyyaṃ, bhagavantameva passeyyaṃ, sammāsambuddhañca vatāhaṃ passeyyaṃ, bhagavantameva passeyya"nti (saṃ. ni. 2.154). evampi mahākassapassa saraṇagamanaṃ viya sissabhāvūpagamanaṃ veditabbaṃ.

"so ahaṃ vicarissāmi, gāmā gāmaṃ purā puraṃ,

namassamāno sambuddhaṃ, dhammassa ca sudhammata "nti.
(su. ni. 194),

evampi āḷavakādīnaṃ saraṇagamanaṃ viya tapparāyaṇatā veditabbā. atha kho brahmāyu brāhmaṇo uṭṭhāyāsanā ekaṃsaṃ uttarāsaṅgaṃ karitvā bhagavato pādesu sirasā nipatitvā bhagavato pādāni mukhena ca paricumbati, pāṇīhi ca parisambāhati, nāmañca sāveti – "brahmāyu ahaṃ, bho gotama brāhmaṇo, brahmāyu ahaṃ, bho gotama brāhmaṇo"ti (ma. ni. 2.394) evampi paṇipāto daṭṭhabbo.

 

[Buddha is my] refuge (tāṇanti) - this is how one should understand self-devotion (attasanniyyātanaṃ).

“Verily, if I were to see the Teacher, I would see the Blessed One; if I were to see the Well-Gone One, I would see the Blessed One; if I were to see the Fully Enlightened One, I would see the Blessed One”
(from Samyutta Nikaya 2.154) – this is how becoming a disciple (sissabhāvūpagamanaṃ) should be understood, as in the case of taking refuge with Mahākassapa.

“I will wander from village to village, from town to town, worshipping the Fully Enlightened One and the excellent Dhamma”
(from Sutta-nipata 194) - this is how taking as a refuge (tapparāyaṇatā) should be understood, as in the case of the taking of refuge by Alavaka and others.

“Then the brahmin Brahmayu, rising from his seat, throwing his upper robe over one shoulder, fell with his head at the feet of the Blessed One, kissed his feet, stroked them with his hands and declared his name: ‘I am the brahmin Brahmayu, the venerable Gotama, I am the brahmin Brahmayu, the venerable Gotama’
(from Majjhima-nikaya 2.394) - thus should worship (paṇipāto) be regarded.

 

So panesa ñātibhayācariyadakkhiṇeyyavasena catubbidho hoti. tattha dakkhiṇeyyapaṇipātena saraṇagamanaṃ hoti, na itarehi. seṭṭhavaseneva hi saraṇaṃ gaṇhāti, seṭṭhavasena ca bhijjati. tasmā yo sākiyo vā koliyo vā – “buddho amhākaṃ ñātako”ti vandati, aggahitameva hoti saraṇaṃ. yo vā – “samaṇo gotamo rājapūjito mahānubhāvo avandīyamāno anatthampi kareyyā”ti bhayena vandati, aggahitameva hoti saraṇaṃ. yo vā bodhisattakāle bhagavato santike kiñci uggahitaṃ saramāno buddhakāle vā – "catudhā vibhaje bhoge, paṇḍito gharamāvasaṃ, ekena bhogaṃ bhuñjeyya, dvīhi kammaṃ payojaye, catutthañca nidhāpeyya, āpadāsu bhavissatī"ti.
(dī. ni. 3.265),
evarūpaṃ anusāsaniṃ uggahetvā – “ācariyo me”ti vandati, aggahitameva hoti saraṇaṃ. yo pana – "ayaṃ loke aggadakkhiṇeyyo"ti vandati, teneva gahitaṃ hoti saraṇaṃ.

 

This [worship] is of four kinds:

out of kinship
out of fear
as to the teacher
as worthy of gifts

Of these, only worship as worthy of gifts (dakkhiṇeyyapaṇipātena) becomes [true] taking of refuge, but not the others.
For refuge is taken only because of [recognizing someone as] the highest and is destroyed for the same reason .

Therefore, when any Sakya or Koliya prostrates [saying], “The Buddha is our relative,” refuge is not considered to have been taken.

Or when someone worships out of fear, [thinking]: “The hermit Gotama is revered by kings, has great power, and if he is not worshiped, harm may come to us” - refuge is not considered to be taken.

Or when someone, remembering something learned from the Blessed One during his time as a Bodhisattva, or during the time of the Buddha, such as: “A wise householder should divide wealth into four parts: one part to use for pleasure, two parts to invest in business, and the fourth to put aside for misfortune” (from Digha Nikaya 3.265), having learned such an instruction, prostrates [saying]: “He is my teacher” - refuge is not considered to be taken.

But when someone worships [thinking], “He is the highest in the world worthy of gifts,” only then is the refuge considered to be accepted.

Evaṃ gahitasaraṇassa ca upāsakassa vā upāsikāya vā aññatitthiyesu pabbajitampi ñātiṃ – “ñātako me aya”nti vandato saraṇagamanaṃ 208na bhijjati, pageva apabbajitaṃ. tathā rājānaṃ bhayavasena vandato. so hi raṭṭhapūjitattā avandīyamāno anatthampi kareyyāti. tathā yaṃ kiñci sippaṃ sikkhāpakaṃ titthiyampi – “ācariyo me aya”nti vandatopi na bhijjati, evaṃ saraṇagamanappabhedo veditabbo.

For a layman or laywoman who has thus taken refuge, the taking of refuge is not broken even if they pay respects to a relative who has become an ascetic in another religion by saying, “This is my relative,” let alone [paying respects] to one who has not become an ascetic. .

Likewise [the refuge is not violated] if they pay respect to the king out of fear. For the king, who is revered in the land, may cause harm if respect is not paid to him.

Similarly, [the refuge is not broken] if they pay respect to a follower of another religion who teaches some craft by saying, “This is my teacher.”7

This is how the different types of taking refuge should be understood.

ettha ca lokuttarassa saraṇagamanassa cattāri sāmaññaphalāni vipākaphalaṃ, sabbadukkhakkhayo ānisaṃsaphalaṃ. vuttañhetaṃ – "yo ca buddhañca dhammañca, saṅghañca saraṇaṃ gato, cattāri ariyasaccāni, sammappaññāya passati. dukkhaṃ dukkhasamuppādaṃ, dukkhassa ca atikkamaṃ, ariyaṃ aṭṭhaṅgikaṃ maggaṃ, dukkhūpasamagāminaṃ. pamuccatī"ti.
(dha. pa. 192),

 

And here, for supramundane refuge-taking, the four fruits of asceticism are the fruit of good ripening, and the cessation of all suffering is the fruit of benefit. For it is said:

Whoever has come to the Buddha, Dhamma and Sangha as refuge,
sees with right wisdom the four noble truths:
suffering, the origin of suffering and the overcoming of suffering,
the noble eightfold path leading to the cessation of suffering. This, verily, is the safe refuge,
this is the supreme refuge.
Having come to this refuge,
one is freed from all suffering.
(dha. pa. 192)

(a. ni. 1.290).

lokiyassa pana saraṇagamanassa bhavasampadāpi bhogasampadāpi phalameva. vuttañhetaṃ –

"ye keci buddhaṃ saraṇaṃ gatāse, na te gamissanti apāyabhūmiṃ,

pahāya mānusaṃ dehaṃ, devakāyaṃ paripūressantī"ti.
(saṃ. ni. 1.37),

 

Moreover, the fruit of good ripening for this (or for him) should be understood in the sense of not approaching [the view of] constancy and so on:

 

“It is impossible and inconceivable that a person who has attained right view should regard any conditioned phenomenon as permanent... regard any conditioned phenomenon as happiness... any phenomenon as the self... should take the life of a mother... a father... an arhat... with an evil mind should shed the blood of a Tathagata... should split the Sangha... should point to another teacher—such a possibility does not exist.”

 

However, for worldly taking refuge, both the perfection of existence and the perfection of wealth are also fruit 8. For it is said:

 

“Whoever comes to the Buddha for refuge, they will not go to the lower realms, leaving the human body, [they] will join the ranks of the gods.”

It is further said:

“Then Sakka, the lord of the devas, together with eighty thousand devatas, approached the venerable Mahamoggallana. The venerable Mahamoggallana said to Sakka, the lord of the devas, standing to one side: “Good, O lord of the devas, taking refuge in the Buddha. Due to the cause of taking refuge in the Buddha, O lord of the devas, some beings here after the destruction of the body, after death, are reborn in a good destination, in the heavenly world. They surpass other devas in ten aspects: divine longevity, divine beauty, divine happiness, divine glory, divine lordship, divine forms, sounds, smells, tastes, tangible objects.”
(Samyutta Nikaya 4.341)

This method [applies] also to the Dhamma and Sangha. Moreover, the special fruit of taking refuge should also be understood on the basis of the Velama Sutta and others. This is how the fruit of taking refuge should be understood.

 

 

Tattha ca lokiyasaraṇagamanaṃ tīsu vatthūsu aññāṇasaṃsayamicchāñāṇādīhi saṃkilissati, na mahājutikaṃ hoti, na mahāvipphāraṃ. lokuttarassa natthi saṃkileso. lokiyassa ca saraṇagamanassa duvidho bhedo – sāvajjo ca anavajjo ca. tattha sāvajjo aññasatthārādīsu attasanniyyātanādīhi hoti, so ca aniṭṭhaphalo hoti. anavajjo kālakiriyāya hoti, so avipākattā aphalo. lokuttarassa pana nevatthi bhedo. bhavantarepi hi ariyasāvako aññaṃ satthāraṃ na uddisatīti. evaṃ saraṇagamanassa saṃkileso ca bhedo ca veditabboti.

Pollution of the refuge

“Here the mundane taking of refuge (lokiyassa saraṇagamanassa) in the three objects is defiled by ignorance (aññāṇa), doubt (saṃsaya), false knowledge (micchāñāṇa), and the like. It does not have great brightness (mahājutika) and great breadth (mahāvipphāra). The supramundane taking of refuge (lokuttarassa) has no defilements (saṃkilesa).



Destruction of the refuge

 

The worldly refuge is subject to two kinds of destruction (bhedo):

with blemish (sāvajjo)
and without blemish (anavajjo).

Destruction with blemish occurs through entrusting oneself (attasanniyyātana) to another teacher (añña-satthāra) or through similar actions. This destruction results in undesirable consequences (aniṭṭhaphala).

Destruction without blemish occurs through death (kālakiriyā), and is fruitless (aphala), since it does not bring about kamic consequences (avipāka).

There is no destruction (bheda) of the supramundane refuge, for even in another existence (bhavantare) the disciple of the Noble One (ariyasāvako) does not turn to another teacher. Thus should the defilement (saṃkilesa) and destruction (bheda) of the refuge be understood.

“May the Blessed One regard me as an upasaka 9!” means: “May the Blessed One regard me as an upasaka.”

To understand the role of an upāsaka here we should study:

1. Who is an upāsaka?
2. Why is he called so?
3. What are his rules of training?
4. What is his way of life?
5. What are his failures?
6. What are his successes?

 

So who is an upasaka?



This is any householder who has taken refuge. As it is said:
“From that moment, Mahanama, when anyone goes to the Buddha for refuge, goes to the Dhamma for refuge, goes to the Sangha for refuge, from that moment he becomes an upasaka” (SN 5.1033).

Why is he called an upasaka?

Because he venerates (upāsana) the Three Jewels (ratanattaya). He venerates the Buddha (Buddhaṃ upāsati), therefore he is an upasaka; also he venerates the Dhamma and the Sangha.

What are his training rules?

These are the five abstinences (pañca veramaṇiyo). As it is said: “From that moment, Mahānama, when an upāsaka abstains from killing living beings (pāṇātipātā paṭivirato hoti), from taking what is not given (adinnādānā paṭivirato hoti), from sexual misconduct (kāmesumicchācārā paṭivirato hoti), from lying (musāvādā paṭivirato hoti), from taking intoxicants and substances that cause heedlessness (surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā paṭivirato hoti), from that moment he becomes a moral upāsaka (sīlavā hotī)’ (SN 5.1033).”

Ko ājīvoti pañca micchāvaṇijjā pahāya dhammena samena jīvitakappanaṃ. vuttañhetaṃ – "pañcimā, bhikkhave, vaṇijjā upāsakena akaraṇīyā. katamā pañca? satthavaṇijjā, sattavaṇijjā, maṃsavaṇijjā, majjavaṇijjā, visavaṇijjā. imā kho, bhikkhave, pañca vaṇijjā upāsakena akaraṇīyā"ti
(a. ni. 5.177).

 

What is the lifestyle like?

 

By giving up the five wrong kinds of trade, and maintaining one's life in a righteous and honest [manner]. For it is said:

“Monks, there are five kinds of trade that should not be carried on by a lay follower. What are the five? Trade in weapons, trade in living beings, trade in meat, trade in intoxicating drinks, trade in poisons. These, monks, are the five kinds of trade that should not be carried on by a lay follower.”

(AN 5.177).

 

Kā vipattīti yā tasseva sīlassa ca ājīvassa ca vipatti, ayamassa vipatti. api ca yāya esa caṇḍālo ceva hoti, malañca patikuṭṭho ca, sāpissa vipattīti veditabbā. te ca atthato assaddhiyādayo pañca dhammā honti. yathāha – "pañcahi, bhikkhave, dhammehi samannāgato upāsako upāsakacaṇḍālo ca hoti, upāsakamalañca, upāsakapatikuṭṭho ca. katamehi pañcahi? assaddho hoti, dussīlo hoti, kotūhalamaṅgaliko hoti, maṅgalaṃ pacceti, no kammaṃ, ito ca bahiddhā dakkhiṇeyyaṃ pariyesati, tattha ca pubbakāraṃ karotī"ti
(a. ni. 5.175).

 

What does failure mean?

 

This means failure both in morality (sīla) and right livelihood (ājīva). This is his downfall (vipatti). Furthermore, what makes him candala ( 10caṇḍāla), impure (mala) and outcast (patikuṭṭha) is also to be understood as his failure.

 

These causes are essentially five qualities, beginning with lack of faith (assaddhiya). As it is said:

 

"O monks, a layman possessing five qualities becomes a layman (upāsakacaṇḍāla), impure (upāsakamala), and rejected (upāsakapatikuṭṭha). Which five? He is unbelieving (assaddha), he is immoral (dussīla), he resorts to superstitious rites (kotūhalamaṅgalika), he believes in omens (maṅgalaṃ pacceti) rather than in kamma, and, instead of seeking worthy offerings (dakkhiṇeyya) within the Dhamma, he seeks them outside and renders assistance there"
(Aṅguttara Nikāya 5.175).

 

Kā sampattīti yā cassa sīlasampadā ceva ājīvasampadā ca, sā sampatti, ye cassa ratanabhāvādikarā saddhādayo pañca dhammaā. yathāha – "pañcahi, bhikkhave, dhammehi samannāgato upāsako upāsakaratanañca hoti, upāsakapadumañca, upāsakapuṇḍarīkañca. What katamehi pañcahi? saddho hoti, sīlavā hoti, on kotūhalamaṅgaliko hoti, kammaṃ pacceti, but maṅgalaṃ, on it bahiddhā dakkhiṇeyyaṃ gavesati, idha ca pubbakāraṃ karotī"ti
(a . ni. 5.175).


What is his progress?

It means the attainment of perfection in morality (sīla) and right livelihood (ājīva). This is his success (sampatti). These causes of success are the five qualities that make him a jewel (ratana), etc., beginning with faith (saddhā). As it is said:

 

"O monks, a layman possessing five qualities becomes a precious layman (upāsakaratana), like a lotus (upāsakapaduma) or a white lotus (upāsakapuṇḍarīka). Which five? He has faith (saddha), morality (sīlavā), does not resort to superstitious rituals (na kotūhalamaṅgalika), believes in kamma (kammaṃ pacceti) and not in omens (maṅgalaṃ), and does not seek meritorious offerings (dakkhiṇeyya) outside the Dhamma, but gives help (pubbakāra) here"
(Aṅguttara Nikāya 5.175).

"teneva aṅgulaggena taṃ aṅgulaggaṃ parāmaseyya. ucchaggaṃ veḷagga"ntiādīsu (kathā. 281) koṭiyaṃ. "ambilaggaṃ vā madhuraggaṃ vā tittakaggaṃ vā vihāraggena vā pariveṇaggena vā bhājetu"ntiādīsu (cūḷava. 317) koṭṭhāse. "yāvatā, bhikkhave, sattā apadā vā…pe… tathāgato tesaṃ aggamakkhāyatī"tiādīsu (a. ni. 4.34) seṭṭhe. idha panāyaṃ ādimhi daṭṭhabbo. tasmā ajjataggeti ajjataṃ ādiṃ katvāti

evametthattho veditabbo. ajjatanti ajjabhāvaṃ. ajjadaggeti vā pāṭho, dakāro padasandhikaro. ajja agganti attho.

 

What does ajjatagge mean

The word 'agga' in this context is used in the meanings of beginning (ādi), end (koṭi), part (koṭṭhāsa) and supreme (seṭṭha).

 

In the meaning of the beginning:
"Ajjatagge, samma dovārika, āvarāmi dvāraṃ nigaṇṭhānaṃ nigaṇṭhīnaṃ"
("From this day on, guardian, I close the doors to the nigganthas and nigganthinis") (Majjhima Nikāya 2.70).

In the sense of the end:
"Teneva aṅgulaggena taṃ aṅgulaggaṃ parāmaseyya. Ucchaggaṃ veḷaggaṃ" ("With that fingertip he would touch that fingertip. The top of the sugarcane, the top of the bamboo")
(Kathāvatthu 281).

In the meaning of the part:
"Ambilaggaṃ vā madhuraggaṃ vā tittakaggaṃ vā vihāraggena vā pariveṇaggena vā bhājetu"
("Let him distribute sour, or sweet, or bitter to the monasteries or cells")
(Cūḷavagga 317).

In the meaning of the highest:
"Yāvatā, bhikkhave, sattā apadā vā...pe... tathāgato tesaṃ aggamakkhāyatī"
(“O monks, as far as beings are encompassed, including the footless… the Tathagata is considered to be the supreme”)
(Aṅguttara Nikāya 4.34).

In this context the word should be understood in the sense of beginning (ādi). Therefore, "ajjatagge" means "starting from today".

 

"Ajjataṃ" is the state of "today".

An alternative reading is "ajjadagge," where the "d" is added to connect the words, meaning "today is the highest."

 

pāṇupetanti pāṇehi upetaṃ.

pāṇupeta means "filled with life" or "animated".

 

yāva me jīvitaṃ pavattati, tāva upetaṃ anaññasatthukaṃ tīhi saraṇagamanehi saraṇaṃ gataṃ upāsakaṃ kappiyakārakaṃ maṃ bhagavā dhāretu jānātu.

As long as my life lasts, may the Blessed One regard me and know me as an upasaka who has taken refuge in the Three Jewels without another teacher and who performs his due duties.

Even if I were beheaded with a sharp sword, I would never say that Buddha is not Buddha, Dhamma is not Dhamma, Sangha is not Sangha.

 



Now let us turn to the commentary on the passage of the Bhayabherava Sutta that deals with taking refuge.

 

 

evaṃ desanaṃ thometvā –
Having glorified the preaching in this way,

imāya desanāya ratanattayapasannacitto –
having gained confidence in the Triple Gem through this preaching,

pasannākāraṃ karonto esāhantiādimāha –
expressing his faith, he said: “esāhaṃ” and so on.

 

tattha esāhanti eso ahaṃ –
Here esāhaṃ means: “It is I.”

bhavantaṃ gotamaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmīti –
“I go to you, Gotama, as a refuge” means: “You are for me, Gotama, a refuge, a support, a protector from suffering, a bestower of good.”

iminā adhippāyena bhavantaṃ gotamaṃ gacchāmi, bhajāmi, sevāmi, payirupāsāmi –
In this sense: “I take refuge in you, I worship you, I serve you, I follow you.”

evaṃ vā jānāmi, bujjhāmīti –
Or: “I thus understand, comprehend.”

 

yesañhi dhātūnaṃ gatiattho, buddhipi tesaṃ attho –
Since the root gacchati has the meaning of “to go” as well as “to understand”,

tasmā gacchāmīti imassa jānāmi, bujjhāmīti ayamattho vutto –
then the word gacchāmi implies the meaning: “I understand, comprehend.”

 

dhammañca bhikkhusaṅghañcāti –
Further, when it is said: “Dhamma and Sangha,”

 

ettha pana adhigatamagge sacchikatanirodhe –
Here, by “Dhamma” is meant the mastered path (adhigatamagga) and the realized cessation of suffering (sacchikatanirodha),

 

yathānusiṭṭhaṃ paṭipajjamāne ca apāyesu apatamāne dhāretīti dhammo –
and also that which protects those who practice as indicated and protects them from falling into the lower realms (apāya).

 

so atthato ariyamaggo ceva nibbānañca –
In essence, this is the Noble Eightfold Path and Nibbana.

 

vuttañhetaṃ – It is said:

yāvatā, bhikkhave, dhammā saṅkhatā, ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo tesaṃ aggamakkhāyatī ti –
“Of all conditioned phenomena, the Eightfold Noble Path is called the highest.”

 

na kevalañca ariyamaggo ceva nibbānañca –
And not only the Eightfold Path and Nibbana,

apica kho ariyaphalehi saddhiṃ pariyattidhammopi –
but also the Dhamma in the form of canonical texts associated with the Noble Fruits (ariyaphala).

 

vuttañhetaṃ chattamāṇavakavimāne –
Thus it is said in the Chatta-Manavaka-vimana:

"rāgavirāgamanejamasokaṃ, dhammamasaṅkhatamappaṭikūlaṃ, madhuramimaṃ paguṇaṃ suvibhattaṃ, dhammamimaṃ saraṇatthamupehī"ti –

“The extinction of thirst, free from worry and sorrow, the Dhamma unconditioned, devoid of contradictions, sweet, clearly divided - this Dhamma you should take as refuge.”

 

ettha rāgavirāgoti maggo kathito –
Here the extinction of thirst signifies the Path (magga).

anejamasokanti phalaṃ –
Free from worries and sorrow means Fruit (phala).

dhammamasaṅkhatanti nibbānaṃ –
Unconditioned Dhamma means Nibbana.

 

appaṭikūlaṃ madhuramimaṃ paguṇaṃ suvibhattanti piṭakattayena vibhattā sabbadhammakkhandhāti –
Uncontradictory, sweet, skillfully divided – this is the Dhamma explained in the Three Pitakas, embracing all groups of phenomena (sabbadhammakkhandha).

 

diṭṭhisīlasaṅghātena saṃhatoti saṅgho –
Sangha are those who are united in the perfection of view and morality.

so atthato aṭṭha ariyapuggalasamūho –
Essentially, these are the eight groups of Noble personalities (ariyapuggala).

 

vuttañhetaṃ tasmiṃyeva vimāne –
It is said in the same Chatta-Manavaka-vimana:

"yattha ca dinnamahapphalamāhu, catūsu sucīsu purisayugesu, aṭṭha ca puggala dhammadasā te, saṅghamimaṃ saraṇatthamupehī"ti –

“Where offerings bear great fruit, where there are four pure pairs of people, eight individuals who know the Dhamma, that Sangha you should take as refuge.”

 

bhikkhūnaṃ saṅgho bhikkhusaṅgho –
The Sangha of monks is the monastic community (bhikkhusaṅgha).

 

ettāvatā brāhmaṇo tīṇi saraṇagamanāni paṭivedesi –
Thus the brahmin declared his turning to the Threefold Refuge.

Further, much the same as with the commentaries on the Samññaphala Sutta.

 

The Special Fruits of Taking Refuge

 

Furthermore, the fruit of taking refuge can be understood through the teaching presented in the Velāma Sutta (Aṅguttara Nikāya 9.20), which describes how taking refuge brings unique blessings.

 

Thus, one should understand the fruits of taking refuge.

 

 

Excerpt from the Velama Sutta:

theravada.ru

 

 

In the past, householder, there lived a Brahmin named Velama. He gave away the following great gifts:

(1) eighty-four thousand gold bowls filled with silver;
(2) eighty-four thousand silver bowls filled with gold; (3) eighty-four thousand bronze bowls filled with ingots; (4) eighty-four thousand elephants with gold ornaments, gold banners, covered with nets of gold thread;
(5) eighty-four thousand chariots covered with the skins of lions, tigers, leopards, with saffron blankets, with gold ornaments and gold banners, covered with nets of gold thread;
(6) eighty-four thousand milch cows with jute tethers and bronze tubs;
(7) eighty-four thousand maidens adorned with jeweled earrings; (8) eighty-four thousand divans made of ivory, heartwood, gold and silver, covered with long-pile coverings, decorated with flowers, the best antelope skins, with canopies and red cushions on both sides;
(9) eighty-four thousand kotis of clothes made of the best linen, the best silk, the best wool, the best cotton. And not to mention food, drink, snacks, dinners, tonics and drinks. It all seemed to flow like a river.

And you may think, householder, "It was somebody else, this brahmana Velama, who was then distributing such great gifts." But you should not think like that. It was I, the brahmana Velama, who was then distributing such great gifts.

And at the time when these alms were being made, householder, there was no one who was worthy of the offering, no one who would purify the offering. And far more fruitful than that great offering made by the brahmin Velama would be to feed one person perfect in views.

Even more fruitful than the great offering made by the brahmin Velama, and than feeding one person perfect in views and a hundred people perfect in views, would be to feed one once-returner.

Even more fruitful than the great offering made by the brahmin Velama, and than feeding one… one hundred persons accomplished in views, and one hundred once-returners, would be to feed one non-returner.

Even more fruitful… and than feeding a hundred non-returners – would be to feed one arahant.

Even more fruitful… and than feeding a hundred arahants – would be to feed one pacceka-buddha.

Even more fruitful… and than feeding a hundred pacceka-buddhas – would be to feed one Tathagata, Arahant, Fully Enlightened One.

Even more fruitful… and than feeding the Tathagata, the Arahant, the Fully Enlightened One – would be to feed the Sangha of monks headed by the Buddha.

Even more fruitful… and than feeding the Sangha of monks headed by the Buddha – would be to build a dwelling and dedicate it to the Sangha of the four directions 11.

Even more fruitful… and than building a dwelling dedicated to the Sangha of the four directions – would be to take
refuge with a believing mind in the Buddha, Dhamma, Sangha.12

Even more fruitful… and than taking refuge with a believing mind in the Buddha, Dhamma, Sangha –
would be the adoption of five rules of conduct: 13
abstinence from the destruction of life, abstinence from taking what is not given, abstinence from unwholesome sexual behavior, abstinence from lying, abstinence from alcoholic drinks, wine, intoxicants, which are the basis for carelessness.

Even more fruitful… and than the adoption of the five rules of conduct… – would be the development of goodwill 14even for [that short] time during which one can [only] pull a cow by the udder 15.

Even more fruitful than the great offering made by the brahmin Velama, and than feeding one man accomplished in views, and one hundred men accomplished in views, and one once-returner, and one hundred once-returners… …and developing goodwill even for [that brief] time in which one can [merely] tug a cow by the udder – would be developing the perception of impermanence even for [that brief] time in which one can [merely] snap one’s fingers 16.”
