Commentary

When classified by object type: In relation to each type of immaterial sphere consciousness, there are two kinds of objects (ālambana) that need to be understood: one is the object that must be taken directly by the citta (ālambitabba); the other is the object that must be transcended (atikkamitabba).

 

The arūpa-jhānas differ from the rūpa-jhānas in several important respects. While the rūpa-jhānas can take various objects, such as different kasinas, etc., each arūpa-jhāna takes only one specific object for itself. Also, the rūpa-jhānas differ from one another in terms of their jhānic factors: the first has five factors, the second four, and so on. A meditator who wants to attain higher jhānas maintains the same

object and sequentially removes the subtler factors until they reach the fifth jhānas. But for the transition from the fifth rūpa-jhāna to the first arūpa-jhāna and from one arūpa-jhāna to the next, there are no more jhānic factors

that could be transcended. Instead, the meditator progresses by transcending each successive, more subtle object. All arūpa-jhāna cittas have the same two jhānic factors as the fifth rūpa-jhāna, namely equanimity and one-pointedness. For this reason, the four arūpa-jhānas are spoken of as being included in the fifth rūpa-jhāna. As cittas, they are different because they belong to different spheres and have different types of objects than the fifth jhāna. But because as jhānas they consist of the same jhānic factors, Abhidhamma teachers sometimes consider them as different forms of the fifth jhānas. All together, the fifteen cittas of the fine-material sphere and the twelve cittas of the immaterial sphere are called mahaggatacitta — exalted or great consciousnesses — because they are free from hindrances, being pure, lofty, and great states of mind. All consciousnesses discussed so far — eighty-one types — are called lokiyacitta, mundane consciousness,

because they pertain to the three worlds — the sensual world (kāmaloka), the fine-material world (rūpaloka), and the immaterial world (arūpaloka).
