The five jhānas are divided into fifteen types, arising as wholesome, resultant, and functional cittas. Each jhāna citta of the same level is defined by the same set of factors, regardless of whether the citta is wholesome, resultant, or functional. All fine-material sphere cittas are associated with knowledge (ñāṇasampayutta), although knowledge, not being a specific jhāna factor, is not mentioned in the formulas. Thus, all fine-material sphere cittas have three roots: non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion.

 

It should be noted that, unlike the wholesome and unwholesome cittas of the sensory sphere, the cittas of the fine-material sphere are not distinguished by the type arising with prompting and without prompting (sasaṅkhārika-asaṅkhārika). This distinction is also omitted from the description of the immaterial sphere cittas and the supramundane cittas. This omission is made because, when someone practices meditation to achieve the jhāna of the path or fruit, as long as they depend on encouragement from others or their own prompting, the mind is not yet ready to attain the achievement. The distinction by type with prompting and without prompting is suitable for the preliminary phase of practice leading to attainment, but the cittas with which actual attainment occurs cannot include prompting or urging. Therefore, in the absence of the real possibility of prompting jhāna and supramundane attainment, the distinction between arising with prompting or without prompting becomes irrelevant concerning these types of cittas.

 

The view presented here differs from the conventional opinion of the "Vibhāvinī-ṭīkā," which states that since any attainment of jhāna requires preliminary effort (pubbābhisaṅkhāra), jhāna cittas can never be called arising without prompting, only with prompting. This view seems untenable because the preliminary effort leading to jhāna should not be identified with the "prompting" accompanying the jhāna cittas themselves. Thus, despite the prestigious authority of the "Vibhāvinī," it still seems preferable to consider the distinction "with prompting—without prompting" as inappropriate concerning the higher classes of consciousness. However, Ledi Sayadaw asserts that this distinction can still be applicable to jhānas and supramundane states because of the distinction made in the texts regarding the kinds of progress (paṭipadā) through which they are attained.

 

The "Dhammasaṅgaṇī" makes a distinction between attainments achieved through difficult progress (dukkhapaṭipadā), where defilements can be suppressed only by intense effort and great exertion, and easy progress (sukhapaṭipadā), where defilements can be suppressed easily and pleasantly. Ledi Sayadaw believes that the jhāna cittas or supramundane cittas of those who attained through difficult progress are similar to the cittas with prompting at the sensory level, while the jhāna cittas or supramundane cittas of those who progressed easily are similar to the cittas without prompting.

 

However, although Ledi Sayadaw's view is noteworthy, the fact remains that:

 

1) The "Dhammasaṅgaṇī" originally classifies the jhāna cittas and supramundane cittas without reference to the type of progress, and

 

2) In the section where this classification by type of progress is given, such a distinction is not used as a basis for numbering the different types of jhāna cittas and supramundane cittas. Therefore, it seems preferable to exclude the distinction "with prompting—without prompting" from jhāna cittas, as well as from the path and fruit cittas altogether.
