**MORA PARITTA**

 

**Udet’ayañ-cakkhumā eka-rājā**

Behold, the All-Seeing Ruler [i.e., the sun] rises,

 

**Harissa-vaṇṇo paṭhavippabhāso**

Illuminating the Earth with golden light.

 

**Taṃ taṃ namassāmi harissa-vaṇṇaṃ paṭhavippabhāsaṃ**

I bow to you, who illuminate the Earth with golden light.

 

**Tay`ajja guttā viharemu divasaṃ**

May I live this day under your protection.

 

**Ye brāhmaṇā vedagu sabba-dhamme**

To the Brahmins who know all Dhamma,

 

**Te me namo te ca maṃ pālayantu**

I offer my respect; may they protect me.

 

**Nam’atthu Buddhānaṃ nam’atthu bodhiyā**

Homage to the Buddhas, homage to Awakening.

 

**Namo vimuttānaṃ namo vimuttiyā**

Homage to the liberated ones, homage to liberation.

 

**Imaṃ so parittaṃ katvā**

Reciting these protective verses,

 

**Moro carati esanā’ti**

The peacock sets out in search of food.

 

**Apet’ayañ-cakkhumā eka-rājā**

Behold, the All-Seeing Ruler sets,

 

**Harissa-vaṇṇo paṭhavippabhāso**

Illuminating the Earth with golden light.

 

**Taṃ taṃ namassāmi harissa-vaṇṇaṃ paṭhavippabhāsaṃ**

I bow to you, who illuminate the Earth with golden light.

 

**Tay`ajja guttā viharemu rattiṃ**

May I live this night under your protection.

 

**Ye brāhmaṇā vedagu sabba-dhamme**

To the Brahmins who know all Dhamma,

 

**Te me namo te ca maṃ pālayantu**

I offer my respect; may they protect me.

 

**Nam’atthu Buddhānaṃ nam’atthu bodhiyā**

Homage to the Buddhas, homage to Awakening.

 

**Namo vimuttānaṃ namo vimuttiyā**

Homage to the liberated ones, homage to liberation.

 

**Imaṃ so parittaṃ katvā**

Reciting these protective verses,

 

**Moro vāsam-akappayī’ti**

The peacock returns to its abode.

 

---

 

The story of the Buddha's birth as a golden peacock was narrated at the Jetavana monastery when it was reported that one of the disciple monks had been captivated by a woman.

 

The future Buddha was once born as a golden peacock living on the golden hill of Dandaka in the Himalayas. At dawn, the golden peacock would sit on the summit of the hill, watch the sunrise, and recite a protective paritta to safeguard himself throughout the day. Then, he would pay homage to the past Buddhas and extol their virtues. After reciting this protective paritta, he would go in search of food.

 

In the evening, when the sun set, the peacock would return to the hilltop to watch the sunset and recite another paritta to protect himself during the night. After this, he would sleep.

 

At that time, a hunter saw the peacock and told his son about the bird's marvels. Meanwhile, Queen Khema of Benares had a dream that compelled her to demand the king bring the golden peacock to the palace so she could hear its teachings. The king sent the hunter to catch the bird, but thanks to the power of the paritta, the trap could not capture him. After seven years of failure, the hunter died, followed by the queen.

 

The enraged king left an inscription stating that whoever eats the flesh of the golden peacock would remain forever young and immortal. Six successive rulers of the kingdom attempted to capture the bird, but all in vain.

 

The seventh successor sent a clever hunter with a charming peahen capable of singing very sweetly. Early in the morning, the hunter set the trap with the peahen, who sang enchantingly before the golden peacock could recite his usual paritta. The bird was tempted, approached her, and was caught in the trap. The delighted hunter captured the golden peacock and hurried back to the palace to present it to the king.

 

The king was amazed by the bird's golden beauty and placed it on a royal seat to converse with him. The golden peacock recounted the story of his past life when he was a pious king in the same kingdom and explained the power of his paritta. He also advised the king to excavate the golden chariot from the royal lake to prove his words. When everything was confirmed, the bird was released and returned to the golden hill of Dandaka.

 

Thus, the Mora Sutta was accepted as a paritta to protect people from traps or to safely release them if they were captured by enemies. This paritta is typically recited by Theravāda Buddhists to ensure the safety of the entire family throughout the day and night.

 

According to the Burmese version of the Mahāparitta text, this sutta consists of only six stanzas.
