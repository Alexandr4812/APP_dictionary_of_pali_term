SAṂVEGA VATTHU
Eight Grounds for a Sense of Urgency

BHĀVETVĀ CATURĀRAKKHĀ ĀVAJJEYYA ANANTARAṂ MAHĀSAṂVEGAVATTHŪNI, AṬṬHA AṬṬHITAVĪRIYO.
Having developed the four protections of the mind (Buddhānussati, mettā, asubha, maraṇasati), one should then turn to the great grounds for saṃvega (deep spiritual urgency), the eight bases for aroused energy.

JĀTIJARĀVYĀDHICUTĪ APĀYĀ, ATĪTA-APPATTAKAVAṬṬADUKKHAṂ, IDĀNI ĀHĀRAGAVEṬṬHIDUKKHAṂ SAṂVEGAVATTHŪNI IMĀNI AṬṬHA.
Birth, aging, illness, death, the woeful realms, suffering rooted in the past and in the future, and present suffering in the search for food — these are the eight grounds for saṃvega.

PĀTO CA SĀYAM-API CEVA IMAṂ VIDHIÑÑO, ĀSEVATE SATATAM-ATTAHITĀBHILĀSI,
In the morning and in the evening as well, one who understands this method reflects upon it,
practising it constantly, aspiring to one’s own welfare,

PAPPOTI SO TI VIPULAṂ HATAPĀRIPANTHO,
he attains the vast goal, with obstacles destroyed,

SEṬṬHAṂ SUKHAṂ MUNI VISIṬṬHAMATAṂ SUKHENA CĀ TI.
the supreme happiness, the excellent deathless happiness, which the sage attains with ease.

There are eight grounds for saṃvega (Pāli: saṃvega vatthu):

1. birth,

2. aging,

3. illness,

4. death,

5. suffering in the woeful realms,

6. the cycle of suffering rooted in the past,

7. the cycle of suffering rooted in the future,

8. the cycle of suffering involved in the present search for food in the world.

For saṃvega to be an effective motivation for practice, it must be accompanied by another mental quality called pasāda, “clarity and serene confidence.” Pasāda prevents saṃvega from degenerating into nihilistic despair by fostering confidence that there is a way out — namely, Nibbāna.

The sense of saṃvega is also connected with the development of energy (viriya) and right effort, according to Buddhaghosa’s Atthasālinī.

Saṃvega (Pāli: saṃvega) is a Buddhist term denoting a feeling of shock, agitation, and spiritual urgency to escape the suffering of saṃsāra and to attain liberation. According to Ṭhānissaro Bhikkhu, saṃvega is “the first emotion to be brought into the practice,” and it may be defined as:

A chastening sense of shock, dismay, and alienation that comes with realizing the futility and meaninglessness of life as it is normally lived; a nagging sense of one’s own complacency and foolishness in allowing oneself to live so carelessly; and an anxious sense of urgency in trying to find a way out of this meaningless cycle.
