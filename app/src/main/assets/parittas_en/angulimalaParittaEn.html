ANGULIMALA PARITTA

 

Parittaṁ yaṁ bhaṇantassa – nisinnaṭṭhāna dhovanaṁ
Whoever recites the paritta—
the seat [on which the paritta was recited] is washed with water.

 

Udaka’mpi vināseti – sabbameva parissayaṁ
With that water, even all dangers are destroyed.

 

Sotthinā gabbha vuṭṭhānaṁ
The well-being of childbirth is achieved,

 

yañ ca sadheti taṁ khaṇe
whatever may be in that moment.

 

Therassaṅgulimālassa – lokanāthena bhāsitaṁ
Spoken by the Elder Angulimala, the protector of the world,

 

Kappaṭṭhāyiṁ mahātejaṁ – parittaṁ
With the longevity of a great aeon and great power—

 

taṁ bhaṇāmahe.
this paritta we shall recite.

 

Yato’haṁ bhagini,
ariyāya jātiya jāto
Since the time, sister,
when I was born into the noble birth,

 

nā’bhijānāmi saṁcicca pāṇaṁ
jīvitā voropetā,
I am not aware of intentionally depriving any living beings of life.

 

tena saccena sotthi te hotu,
sotthi gabbhassā’ti.
By this truth, may you be safe,
may there be safety for your womb.

 

The Angulimala Paritta originates from the story described in the Angulimala Sutta, which is part of the Pali Canon. According to this sutta, Angulimala was a former serial killer who became a monk after encountering the Buddha. One day, while passing by a woman suffering from difficult labor, Angulimala was moved by her suffering and wanted to help. He asked the Buddha how he could offer assistance, and the Buddha advised him to recite a blessing affirming his own virtue:

 

These words are now used in the Theravada Buddhist tradition as a blessing for pregnant women to ensure their safety and the well-being of childbirth.
