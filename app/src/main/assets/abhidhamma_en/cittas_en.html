89 and 121 Cittas
Sense Sphere (54)
Description ▼

The word kāma denotes both subjective sensuality — that is, the passionate desire for sensual pleasures — and objective sensuousness — that is, the five external sense objects: visible forms, sounds, smells, tastes, and tangible objects.

Kāmabhūmi — the sense plane of existence, which includes eleven realms: the four realms of misery, the human world, and the six sensory heavens.

Sense-sphere consciousness includes all cittas whose corresponding domains belong to the sense plane of existence, even though they may also arise in other planes.

Unwholesome (12)
Description ▼

The Abhidhamma classifies unwholesome consciousness by the type of root (mūla, hetu): greed (lobha), hatred (dosa), or delusion (moha). Greed and hatred are mutually exclusive — they cannot arise in the same citta.

Cittas rooted in greed number eight; those rooted in hatred number two. Delusion is present in every unwholesome consciousness. Cittas in which delusion arises without being accompanied by greed or hatred are called "cittas rooted in delusion" — there are two of them.

Rooted in Greed (1–8)
Description ▼

Lobha covers all variations of greed — from intense passion to mild fondness. The eight cittas differ according to three principles: feeling (joy or equanimity), view (with or without wrong view), and prompting (unprompted or prompted).

Joy (somanassa) — a pleasant mental feeling inseparably blended with consciousness. Equanimity (upekkhā) — a neutral feeling, "neither painful nor pleasant." Wrong view supplies greed with a rational justification or is itself the object of attachment. Prompting can be external (from others) or internal (a volitional effort).

Rooted in Hatred (9–10)
Description ▼

Dosa is distinguished only by prompting. Unlike greed, it arises with only one feeling — displeasure (domanassa, "bad mental state") — and is never accompanied by wrong view.

Domanassa (feeling aggregate) — the experience of an unpleasant mental feeling. Aversion / paṭigha (formations aggregate) — a mental attitude of resistance, literally "striking against," ranging from mild irritation to furious rage. They always arise together, but are distinct phenomena.

9
With displeasure · with aversion · unprompted
Example: A person kills another in a spontaneous fit of rage.
10
With displeasure · with aversion · prompted
Example: A person kills another after prior deliberation.
Rooted in Delusion (11–12)
Description ▼

Cittas in which moha operates without greed or hatred ("pure delusion" — momuha). Neither a desirable nor an undesirable object is perceived as such — therefore the feeling is always neutral (equanimity). Both cittas arise exclusively without prompting.

Doubt (vicikicchā) — bewilderment, scepticism, indecisiveness arising from the predominance of delusion. Restlessness (uddhacca) is present in all 12 unwholesome cittas, but only here does it become the primary factor — anxiety and mental agitation unable to focus on an object.

11
With equanimity · with doubt
Example: A person doubts the enlightenment of the Buddha or the efficacy of the Dhamma.
12
With equanimity · with restlessness
Example: A person is so distracted that they cannot focus the mind on any object.
Rootless (18)
Description ▼

Ahetuka — "without roots." Eighteen types of consciousness containing none of the three unwholesome roots (greed, hatred, delusion) and none of the three beautiful roots (non-greed, non-hatred, non-delusion). Because a root helps establish stability in a citta, rootless cittas are weaker than those that possess roots.

They are divided into three groups: unwholesome resultant (7) — fruits of unwholesome actions; wholesome resultant (8) — fruits of wholesome actions; functional (3) — perform auxiliary cognitive functions.

Unwholesome Resultant Consciousness (7)
Description ▼

Akusalavipākacittāni (unwholesome resultant consciousness) — the first category of rootless consciousness comprises seven types of consciousness that are results of unwholesome kamma. These seven types of consciousness are not themselves unwholesome; they are kammically indeterminate (abyākata). The word "unwholesome" (akusala) here means that they are results produced by unwholesome kamma. In this case, akusala characterises not these states of consciousness themselves, but the kamma from which they originated.

Wholesome Resultant Rootless Consciousness (8)
Description ▼

Kusalavipāka-ahetukacittāni (wholesome resultant rootless consciousness) — the eight types of consciousness in this category are results of wholesome kamma. The word ahetuka was not included in the designation of the previous class because all unwholesome resultants are rootless — there are no unwholesome resultants accompanied by roots. However, as will be seen later, wholesome resultants can be accompanied by roots, namely the beautiful roots, which are kammically indeterminate (abyākata).

In order to distinguish wholesome resultants that have no roots from those that do, the word ahetuka is included in the designation of their class.

Seven of all the rootless types of consciousness correspond to the unwholesome resultants. But whereas the unwholesome resultants arise in relation to an undesired object, the wholesome resultants arise in relation to an object that is desired (iṭṭha) or very desirable (ati-iṭṭha).

The first four sense-consciousnesses here, like their unwholesome counterparts, are associated with equanimity, i.e. neutral feeling; however, because the object exerts a [comparatively stronger] impact on the body, the feeling associated with the wholesome resultant body-consciousness is bodily pleasure (sukha).

The wholesome resultant rootless consciousnesses include one type that has no counterpart among the unwholesome resultants. This is the investigating consciousness accompanied by joy (somanassa). While the investigating consciousness originating from unwholesome kamma is always accompanied by neutral feeling, the investigating consciousness originating from wholesome kamma is twofold — one is accompanied by neutral feeling and arises in relation to a moderately desirable object, the other is accompanied by joy and arises in the case of a particularly desirable object. Thus there are eight types of consciousness in this class, unlike the previous class, which contains seven types.

Rootless Functional Consciousness (3)
Description ▼

Ahetukakiriiyacittāni (rootless functional consciousness) — the remaining three types of consciousness among the ahetukas are not kamma results. They belong to the category called kiriya, translated here as "functional," to indicate that they perform tasks that have no kamma potential. Such types of consciousness are neither kamma-producing nor kamma results. Three types of consciousness in this category are rootless; the rest (described below) have roots.

Beautiful Sense-Sphere (24)
Description ▼

Beautiful (sobhana) is broader than wholesome (kusala): it includes all wholesome cittas as well as resultant and functional ones accompanied by beautiful mental factors. The latter are kammically indeterminate (abyākata), but not unwholesome.

The twenty-four beautiful sense-sphere cittas are: eight wholesome (kusala) — produce kamma; eight resultant (vipāka) — fruits of past kamma; eight functional (kiriyā) — in Arahants, without accumulation of kamma.

Wholesome (8)
Description ▼

These eight types of consciousness are called wholesome (kusala) or meritorious (puñña) because they suppress defilements and produce good results. They arise in worldlings (puthujjana) [people absorbed in worldly concerns] and in trainees (sekha) — noble disciples at the three lower stages of Enlightenment: stream-enterer, once-returner, and non-returner — when they perform noble bodily and verbal actions and when they generate wholesome mental states belonging to the sense sphere. These cittas do not arise in Arahants, whose actions have no kamma potential.

Wholesome sense-sphere consciousness (kāmāvacarakusalacittāni): This class of consciousness is subdivided into eight types on the basis of three principles of dichotomisation. The first principle is the accompanying feeling, which in four cases is joy (somanassa) — a pleasant mental feeling — and in four cases equanimity (upekkhā) — a neutral mental feeling; the second principle is the presence or absence of knowledge; and the third is the pair unprompted or prompted.

Associated with knowledge (ñāṇasampayutta): Knowledge comprehends things as they really are (yathāsabhāvaṃ). In consciousness associated with knowledge, the word ñāṇa refers to the mental factor wisdom (paññā-cetasika), also known as the "root of non-delusion" (amoha). Consciousness dissociated from knowledge (ñāṇavippayutta) lacks this wisdom factor, but it does not contain ignorance (avijjā) or delusion (moha), which belong only to unwholesome consciousnesses.

Unprompted: According to the commentary, a person does a good deed without prompting owing to good physical and mental condition, suitable food, climate, etc., as well as as a result of performing similar deeds in the past. Prompting occurs either through motivation by another or after one's own deliberation.

With roots (sahetuka): The four wholesome cittas associated with knowledge possess all three wholesome roots; the four dissociated from knowledge possess non-greed, or generosity, and non-hatred, or loving-kindness, but are devoid of non-delusion.

Functional with Roots (8)
Description ▼

Rooted functional sense-sphere consciousness (sahetukakāmāvacarakiriyācittāni): Whereas the eight wholesome sense-sphere cittas arise in worldlings and trainees [who have attained the initial stages of Enlightenment], they do not arise in Buddhas and Arahants — those who have transcended the cycle of kamma and future becoming in the realms of rebirth. However, in Buddhas and Arahants there arise eight types of consciousness that are their exact counterparts. These are called kiriyā (kiriya), or "functional cittas," because they simply perform their function without leaving any kamma contribution. Since a Buddha or Arahant has eradicated all traces of ignorance and craving, which are the causes of rebirth, their wholesome deeds cannot generate future results. They simply arise, perform some function, and then disappear without remainder.

Fine-Material Jhānas (15)
Description ▼

This sphere of consciousness includes all cittas that "move in" or belong to the fine-material plane of existence (rūpabhūmi) — realms in which coarse matter is absent and only its refined residue remains. Rebirth in these realms occurs through the attainment of meditative states called jhānas — high achievements in the development of concentration (samādhi). States of consciousness that "frequently visit" this plane, being qualitatively connected to it, are called "fine-material sphere consciousness."

Fifteen cittas fall under this category — five wholesome, five resultant, and five functional. The wholesome cittas of the fine-material sphere are experienced by worldlings and trainees (sekha) who have developed the jhānas in the present life. Their corresponding results (vipāka) arise only in the fine-material world, in beings who have been reborn there as a consequence of developing the jhānas. The five functional (kiriyā) jhāna cittas are experienced only by Arahants who have attained the jhānas.

Commentators derive the Pali word jhāna from a root meaning "to contemplate," and also from a root meaning "to burn." Thus the jhānas are so called because they closely contemplate the object and because they burn away various states that oppose concentration. These states are the five hindrances (nīvaraṇa): sensual desire, ill-will, sloth and torpor, restlessness and worry, and doubt.

The jhānas are attained through the method of meditation called "the development of calm, or tranquillity" (samathabhāvanā). This type of meditation leads to the strengthening of the faculty of concentration (samādhi). By fixing the mind on a single object, all mental distractions are removed. The hindrances are suppressed and the mind becomes fully gathered upon the object.

The object of jhāna consciousness is a mental image called the "counterpart sign" (paṭibhāganimitta). This image is considered a conceptual object (paññatti), but it usually arises on the basis of visible form, and therefore these jhānas belong to the fine-material sphere. A meditator aiming at the jhānas may choose as an initial object of concentration a device for contemplation called a kasiṇa, such as a coloured disk upon which the attention is fixed. When concentration matures, this physical device gives rise to its visualised copy, called the "learning sign" (uggahanimitta), and from it in turn the counterpart sign forms, which is taken as the object of jhāna.

The jhānas are numbered in this order for two reasons: 1) because when someone meditates to attain jhāna, they attain them in this order, and 2) because the Buddha taught them in this order. The wholesome cittas of the fine-material sphere are experienced by worldlings and trainees (sekha) who have developed the jhānas in the present life.

Note on Jhānas

The five jhānas are divided into fifteen types, arising as wholesome, resultant, and functional cittas.

Each jhāna citta of the same level is defined by the same set of factors, regardless of the type of citta, which may be wholesome, resultant, or functional. All cittas of the fine-material sphere are associated with knowledge (ñāṇasampayutta), although knowledge, not being a specific jhāna factor, is not mentioned in the formulas. Thus all fine-material sphere cittas have three roots: non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion.

It should be noted that, unlike the wholesome and unwholesome cittas of the sense sphere, the cittas of the fine-material sphere are not distinguished by the type arising with prompting or without prompting (sasaṅkhārika-asaṅkhārika). This distinction is also omitted from the description of immaterial sphere cittas and supramundane cittas. This omission is made because when someone practises meditation to attain jhāna, path, or fruit, as long as they depend on motivation from others or on their own prompting, the mind is not yet ready for the attainment. The distinction between prompted and unprompted is appropriate for the preliminary phase of practice leading to attainment, but the cittas with which actual attainment occurs cannot include prompting or urging. Thus, in the absence of any real possibility of prompting in jhāna and supramundane attainment, the very distinction between arising prompted or unprompted becomes inapplicable with regard to these types of cittas.

The view presented here differs from the generally accepted opinion of the Vibhāvinī-ṭīkā, which states that since every jhāna attainment requires preliminary effort (pubbābhisaṅkhāra), jhāna cittas can never be called unprompted, but only prompted. This view seems untenable because the preliminary effort leading to jhāna should not be identified with the "prompting" accompanying the jhāna cittas themselves. Thus, despite the prestigious authority of the Vibhāvinī, it still seems preferable to regard the prompted–unprompted distinction as inapplicable to the higher classes of consciousness. Nevertheless, Ledi Sayādaw maintains that this distinction may still be applicable to jhānas and supramundane states by reason of the distinction made in the texts among the kinds of progress (paṭipadā) through which they are attained. The Dhammasaṅgaṇī distinguishes between attainments obtained through difficult progress (dukkhāpaṭipadā), when defilements can be suppressed only through intense striving and great effort, and easy progress (sukhāpaṭipadā), when defilements can be suppressed easily and pleasantly. Ledi Sayādaw holds that the jhāna cittas or supramundane cittas of one who attains through difficult progress are analogous to prompted cittas at the sense sphere level, while the jhāna cittas or supramundane cittas of one who progresses easily are analogous to unprompted cittas.

However, although the view of Ledi Sayādaw is worthy of consideration, the fact remains that:

  1. The Dhammasaṅgaṇī originally classifies jhāna cittas and supramundane cittas without reference to the kind of progress, and
  2. in the section where this classification by kind of progress is given, such a distinction is not used as a basis for numbering the various types of jhāna cittas and supramundane cittas. For this reason it seems preferable to exclude the prompted–unprompted distinction altogether from jhāna cittas, as well as from path and fruit cittas.

Explanation of jhāna factors:

INITIAL APPLICATION (VITAKKA): In the Suttas the word vitakka is often used loosely, but in the Abhidhamma it is used in a precise technical sense, denoting the mental factor that places, or directs, the mind upon the object. Just as a king's favourite may bring a villager to the palace, so vitakka directs the mind to the object. In the practice of attaining jhāna, vitakka has the special task of suppressing the hindrance of sloth and torpor (thīnamiddha).

SUSTAINED APPLICATION (VICĀRA): The word vicāra ordinarily means "investigation," but here it expresses the sustained application of the mind to the object. While vitakka is the directing of the mind and its accompanying states [mental factors] to the object, vicāra is the continued engagement of the mind with the object. The commentaries offer various similes to illustrate the difference between the two jhāna factors. Vitakka is like a bird spreading its wings to fly; vicāra is like the bird soaring through the air with spread wings. Vitakka is like a bee diving towards a flower; vicāra is like the bee hovering over the flower. Vitakka is like a hand grasping a tarnished metal dish; vicāra is like the hand rubbing the dish. Vicāra in the jhānas serves to temporarily suppress the hindrance of doubt (vicikicchā).

RAPTURE (PĪTI): Pīti derives from the verb pīnayati, meaning "to refresh," and may be explained as delight or pleasant rapture in the object. This term is often translated as "joy," which is quite fitting for conveying the sense of its role as a jhāna factor, but may not be broad enough to encompass all its nuances. Commentators distinguish five levels of pīti that arise in the development of concentration: minor rapture, momentary rapture, showering rapture, uplifting rapture, and pervading rapture. Minor rapture is able to raise the hairs on the body. Momentary rapture is like flashes of lightning. Showering rapture strikes the body again and again, like waves upon a seashore. Uplifting rapture can cause the body to levitate. And pervading rapture suffuses the entire body, as a flood fills a cave. The last variety is identified as pīti in the jhāna. As a jhāna factor, pīti suppresses the hindrance of ill-will (vyāpāda).

HAPPINESS (SUKHA): This jhāna factor is a pleasant mental feeling. It is identical with somanassa, joy, and not with sukha as a pleasant bodily feeling accompanying the wholesome resultant body-consciousness. Sukha, also translated as "bliss," is born of seclusion from sensual pleasures; and therefore it is explained as nirāmisasukha — unworldly, or spiritual happiness. It counteracts the hindrance of restlessness and worry (uddhaccakukkucca). Although pīti and sukha are closely related, they are nevertheless distinct in that pīti is a volitional factor belonging to the aggregate of mental formations (saṅkhārakkhandha), while sukha is a feeling belonging to the aggregate of feelings (vedanākkhandha). Pīti may be compared to the delight a weary traveller would feel upon encountering an oasis, and sukha to his pleasure after bathing and drinking.

ONE-POINTEDNESS (EKAGGATĀ): This Pali term literally means "one-" (eka) "pointed" (agga) "state" (tā). This mental factor is the primary component in all five jhānas and the essence of concentration (samādhi). One-pointedness temporarily suppresses sensual desire, which is an indispensable condition for any meditative attainment. Ekaggatā performs the function of close contemplation of the object, the chief characteristic of jhāna, but this mental factor cannot perform this function alone. It requires the joint action of the other four jhāna factors, each performing its special function: vitakka applying the associated states to the object, vicāra keeping them there, pīti bringing rapture in the object, and sukha experiencing happiness in the jhāna.

Immaterial Jhānas (12)
Description ▼

This sphere of consciousness encompasses cittas belonging to the immaterial plane of existence (arūpabhūmi) — four realms in which matter has been entirely transcended and only consciousness and mental factors remain. Rebirth in these four realms occurs through the attainment of arūpajjhānas, the four immaterial, or formless absorptions, which are attained through the development of concentration beyond the five jhānas of the fine-material sphere.

The immaterial sphere consists of twelve cittas: four wholesome cittas with which the immaterial attainments are experienced by worldlings and trainees [at the initial stages of Enlightenment]; four resultants, which arise through rebirth in the immaterial realms; and four functional, which arise in Arahants who undertake the immaterial attainments.

Note on Immaterial Jhānas

When classified by type of object: Regarding each type of immaterial sphere consciousness, there are two kinds of object (ālambaṇa) to be understood: one is the object to be taken directly by the citta (ālambitabba); the other is the object to be transcended (atikkamitabba).

The arūpajjhānas differ from the rūpajjhānas in several important respects. While the rūpajjhānas can take various objects, such as different kasiṇas and so on, each arūpajjhāna takes only one object specific to itself. Also, the rūpajjhānas differ from each other in respect of their jhāna factors: the first has five factors, the second four, and so on. A meditator who wishes to attain higher jhānas keeps the same object and progressively removes ever subtler factors until reaching the fifth jhāna. But for the transition from the fifth rūpajjhāna to the first arūpajjhāna and from one arūpajjhāna to the next, there are no more jhāna factors to transcend. Instead the meditator progresses by transcending each successive, more refined object. All arūpajjhāna cittas have the same two jhāna factors as the fifth rūpajjhāna, namely equanimity and one-pointedness. For this reason the four arūpajjhānas are said to be included in the fifth rūpajjhāna. As cittas they are different because they belong to different spheres and have different types of objects than the fifth jhāna. But because as jhānas they consist of the same jhāna factors, they are sometimes regarded by Abhidhamma teachers as different forms of the fifth jhāna. Taken together, the fifteen cittas of the fine-material sphere and the twelve cittas of the immaterial sphere are called mahaggatacittas — elevated, or sublime consciousness — because they are free from hindrances, being pure, elevated, great states of mind. All the consciousnesses discussed up to this point — eighty-one types — are called lokiyacittas, mundane consciousness, because they belong to the three worlds: the sense world (kāmaloka), the fine-material world (rūpaloka), and the immaterial world (arūpaloka).

Supramundane (8 or 40)
Description ▼

Supramundane consciousness (lokuttaracittāni) is consciousness that pertains to the process of transcending (uttara) the world (loka), which consists of the five aggregates of clinging. This type of consciousness leads to liberation from saṃsāra, the cycle of birth and death, and to the attainment of Nibbāna — the cessation of suffering.

There are eight supramundane cittas. These cittas belong to the four stages of Enlightenment — stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, and arahantship. Each stage includes two types of consciousness: path consciousness (maggacitta) and fruit consciousness (phalacitta). All supramundane cittas take as their object the unconditioned reality, Nibbāna, but differ as path and fruit according to their functions.

Path consciousness has the function of eradicating (or irreversibly weakening) defilements; fruit consciousness has the function of experiencing the degree of liberation achieved through the corresponding path. Path consciousness is a kusalacittas, a wholesome state; fruit consciousness is a vipākacittas, a resultant.

Each path consciousness arises only once and lasts for only one mind-moment; it never recurs in the mental continuum of one who has attained it. The corresponding fruit consciousness arises immediately after the path moment and lasts for two or three mind-moments. Subsequently it can recur, and with practice, in the supramundane absorption called "attainment of fruition" (phalasamāpatti), it can last for many mind-moments.

Paths and fruits are attained through the method of meditation called "the development of insight" (vipassanābhāvanā). This type of meditation involves the strengthening of the faculty of wisdom (paññā). Through sustained attention to the changing phenomena of mind and matter, the meditator learns to discern their true qualities of impermanence, suffering, and non-self. When these insights reach full maturity, they resolve into path and fruit.

Note: 8 or 40 Supramundane Cittas

How does consciousness analysed into eighty-nine types become one hundred and twenty-one types?

1. The path consciousness of the first jhāna of stream-entry together with initial application, sustained application, rapture, happiness, and one-pointedness.
2. The path consciousness of the second jhāna of stream-entry together with sustained application, rapture, happiness, and one-pointedness.
3. The path consciousness of the third jhāna of stream-entry together with rapture, happiness, and one-pointedness.
4. The path consciousness of the fourth jhāna of stream-entry together with happiness and one-pointedness.
5. The path consciousness of the fifth jhāna of stream-entry together with equanimity and one-pointedness.

These are the five types of stream-entry path consciousness. Similarly for the once-returning, non-returning, and arahantship path consciousness, making twenty types of path consciousness. In the same way there are twenty types of fruit consciousness. Thus there are forty types of supramundane consciousness.

Commentary

All meditators attain the supramundane paths and fruits through the development of wisdom (paññā) — the comprehension of the three characteristics: impermanence, suffering, and non-self. However, they differ from one another in the degree of development of concentration (samādhi). Those who have developed insight without a foundation in jhāna are called practitioners of pure insight (sukkhavipassaka). When they attain the path and fruit, their path and fruit cittas occur at the level corresponding to the first jhāna.

Those who have developed insight on the basis of jhāna attain path and fruit corresponding to the level of jhāna they had attained before reaching the path. Ancient teachers hold different views on what factors determine the jhāna level of the path and fruit. One school of thought holds that it is the basis jhāna (pādakajjhāna) — the jhāna used as the foundation for concentration of mind before developing insight, which reaches its highest degree in the attainment of the supramundane path. A second theory holds that the jhāna level of the path is determined by the jhāna used as the object of investigation in insight meditation, called the comprehended or investigated jhāna (sammasitajjhāna). A third school of thought says that when the meditator has mastered the range of jhānas, they can control the jhāna level of the path according to their own wish or inclination (ajjhāsaya).

Nevertheless, whatever explanation is adopted, both for the pure insight meditator and for the jhāna meditator, all path and fruit cittas are regarded as types of jhāna consciousness. This consideration is connected with the fact that these cittas, like mundane jhānas, occur as close contemplation of the object, with full absorption in it, and also because they possess jhāna factors whose intensity corresponds to their counterparts in the mundane jhānas.

The supramundane jhānas of paths and fruits differ from mundane jhānas in several important respects:

First: whereas the mundane jhānas take as their object a concept, such as a kasiṇa image, the supramundane jhānas take as their object Nibbāna — the unconditioned reality.

Second: whereas the mundane jhānas merely suppress defilements, leaving the underlying sources intact, the supramundane path jhānas eradicate defilements so that they can never arise again.

Third: whereas the mundane jhānas lead to rebirth in the fine-material world and thus sustain existence in the cycle of rebirths, the path jhānas sever the fetters binding the being to the cycle and thus resolve into liberation from the round of birth and death.

Finally, whereas the role of wisdom in mundane jhānas is subordinate to concentration, in supramundane jhānas wisdom and concentration are perfectly balanced: concentration fixes the mind on the unconditioned element, and wisdom comprehends the profound significance of the Four Noble Truths.

According to the totality of their jhāna factors, the path and fruit cittas are distributed across the scale of five jhānas. Thus, instead of a simple eightfold enumeration of supramundane consciousnesses by type of path and fruit, each path and fruit consciousness can be enumerated as comprising five — according to the jhāna level at which it can occur. By this means the eight supramundane cittas, each taken at all five jhāna levels, become equal to forty.
