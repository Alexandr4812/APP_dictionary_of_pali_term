28 Rūpa — Material Phenomena
Introduction ▼
Matter is of two kinds: the Abhidhamma enumerates twenty‑eight types of material phenomena, which are briefly comprised in two main categories: the four great primaries and the material phenomena derived from the four great primaries. The four great primaries (mahābhūta) are the fundamental material elements: earth, water, fire, and air. They are the inseparable constituents of matter and, in diverse combinations, enter into the composition of all material substances, from the minutest particle to the most massive mountain.

Derived material phenomena (upādāya rūpa) are material phenomena derived from or dependent on the four great primaries. There are twenty‑four of them. The great primaries may be compared to the earth, and the derived phenomena to the trees and bushes that grow dependent on the earth.

All these twenty‑eight types of material phenomena are distributed into eleven main classes. Seven of these are called concretely produced matter (nipphanna rūpa) because they possess real nature and are therefore suitable for contemplation through insight. The remaining four classes, being more abstract in nature, are called non‑concretely produced matter (anipphanna rūpa).

Classification of Matter (Rūpavibhāga)
§ 6. Singlefold
All this matter [with respect to its classification] is singlefold in the sense that it is all: rootless, with conditions, subject to cankers, conditioned, mundane, belonging to the sense‑sphere, objectless, not to be abandoned. But when considered as internal and external, etc., matter becomes manifold.
Commentary on § 6
All this matter is singlefold: All matter is rootless because it is not associated with wholesome, unwholesome, or indeterminate roots; association with roots exists only for mental phenomena. All matter is with conditions because it arises dependent on four conditions [kamma, temperature, consciousness, and nutriment]. It is subject to cankers (sāsava) because it can become an object of the four cankers [canker of sense‑desire, attachment to existence, wrong view, and ignorance]. It is all conditioned and mundane because there is no matter that transcends the world of the five aggregates of clinging. All matter belongs to the sense‑sphere: although matter exists also in the fine‑material world, it belongs by its nature to the sense‑sphere because it is the object of sense‑desire. Matter is objectless because, unlike mental phenomena, it cannot know an object; and it is not to be abandoned because it cannot be abandoned like defilements in the four supramundane paths.
§ 7. How Manifold
In what way?
The five kinds of sensory material phenomena are internal, the rest are external.
Six kinds, including the sense organs and the heart, are material phenomena that are bases, the rest are not bases.
Seven kinds, including the sense organs and the (two) means of communication, are material phenomena that are doors; the rest are not doors.
Eight kinds, including the sense organs, the material determining sex, and life, are material phenomena that are controlling faculties, the rest are not faculties.
Twelve kinds, including the five sense organs and (seven) sense objects, are coarse, immediate, and impinging material phenomena; the rest are subtle, remote, and non‑impinging.
Material phenomena born of kamma are obtained by clinging; the rest are not obtained by clinging.
The sphere of visible objects is visible, the rest are invisible.
Eye and ear are non‑reaching (their objects), nose, tongue, and body are reaching (their objects) — they are the five kinds of material phenomena that take an object; the remaining material phenomena do not take an object.
Colour, odour, taste, nutritive essence, and the four primary elements are eight kinds of material phenomena that are inseparable, the rest are separable.
Commentary on § 7
Internal (ajjhattika): Here the term “internal” is used with reference to matter in a technical sense, applying only to the five types of sensitive matter that serve as doors for mental phenomena. Although other types of material phenomena occur within the physical body, only these five sensitive factors are designated as “internal”.
Bases (vatthu): see Chapter III, § 20.
Doors (dvāra): The five kinds of sensitive matter are doors for consciousness, i.e. that through which consciousness and mental factors meet with objects. Bodily and verbal communication are doors of action, i.e. channels for bodily and verbal deeds.
Faculties (indriya): Sensitives are so called because they exercise controlling power (indra) in their respective spheres. Each of them controls the coexistent material phenomena in the performance of its specific function such as seeing, hearing, etc. The controlling faculty of sex controls the manifestation of masculine and feminine features and characteristics. The life faculty controls the coexistent material types as a captain controls the ship.
Coarse, immediate, and impinging material phenomena: These three terms are used here in a technical sense which should not be confused with their ordinary meaning. They apply only to material phenomena that play an important role in the arising of sense consciousness, and say nothing about relative size or distance. There are twelve such phenomena: five sense organs and seven sense objects — tangibility counts as three objects because it consists of three great primaries. Those material phenomena which do not directly participate in the arising of sense consciousness are called subtle, remote, and non‑impinging, again regardless of their size and distance.
Obtained by clinging (upādinna): The eighteen kinds of matter produced by kamma are known as “obtained by clinging” because they have been obtained as the fruits of kamma motivated by craving and wrong view. Matter produced by other than kamma causes is known as not obtained by clinging. However, in a general, less technical sense, all organic matter in the body is considered obtained by clinging, while inorganic matter is spoken of as not obtained by clinging. It should be noted that, unlike other pairs of terms used for classification, the pair “obtained by clinging” and “not obtained by clinging” does not set up a mutually exclusive dichotomy, since nine types of material phenomena produced by kamma may also originate from other causes (see below, § 17).
Eye and ear as non‑reaching (their object): According to the Abhidhamma, the eye and ear are regarded as sense organs that do not reach or touch (asampatta) their respective objects. In order for the eye or ear to serve as basis for consciousness, their objects must not be in contact with them. Conversely, the remaining three organs are considered to directly touch (sampatta) their objects.
Material phenomena that take an object: The Pāli expression gocaraggāhikā is used figuratively to emphasise that the five sense organs serve as bases for the consciousnesses that arise with their support. But sense organs, being matter, cannot literally apprehend objects. Rather, the sense consciousnesses that depend on them are what actually cognise objects.
Material phenomena that are inseparable: The four great primaries and the four derivatives — colour, odour, taste, and nutritive essence — are known as inseparable matter (avinibbhogarūpa) because they are always bound together and present in all material objects, from the simplest to the most complex. Other types of material phenomena may or may not be present and are thus considered separable. The material group (kalāpa) that consists exclusively of these eight elements is known as the “pure octad” (suddhaṭṭhaka) or “group with nutritive essence as the eighth (constituent)” (ojatthamaka).
Origin of Matter (Rūpasamuṭṭhāna)
§ 9. Four Ways of Origination
Kammaṃ cittaṃ utu āhāro cāti cattāri rūpasamuṭṭhānāni nāma. Material phenomena arise in four ways: from kamma, from consciousness, from temperature, and from nutriment.
§ 10. Kamma as a Mode of Origination
Twenty‑five types of wholesome and unwholesome kamma pertaining to the sense‑sphere and the fine‑material sphere produce, from moment to moment, beginning with rebirth‑consciousness, in the internal continuum of a being material phenomena born of volition, which originate from kamma.
Commentary on § 10
Material phenomena originating from kamma (kammasamuṭṭhānarūpa): By kamma here is meant volition (cetanā) in past wholesome and unwholesome states of consciousness. The twenty‑five types of kamma that produce material phenomena are the volitions of the twelve unwholesome consciousnesses, of the eight great wholesome consciousnesses, and of the five fine‑material wholesome consciousnesses. The volitions of wholesome consciousnesses of the immaterial sphere generate rebirth in the immaterial world and thus cannot produce material phenomena originating from kamma.
Kamma produces material phenomena in each of the three submoments of consciousness — arising, presence, and dissolution — starting from the submoment of arising in rebirth‑consciousness; it continues to do so throughout existence, up to the seventeenth mind‑moment preceding death‑consciousness. Eighteen types of material phenomena are produced by kamma: the eight inseparable ones in the nine groups produced by kamma; the five sensitivities; the two controlling faculties of sex; the life‑controlling faculty, the heart‑base, and space. Among these, nine — the eight controlling faculties and the heart‑base — arise exclusively from kamma.
§ 11. Consciousness as a Mode of Origination
Seventy‑five types of consciousness, excluding the resultant immaterial‑sphere consciousnesses and the two sets of five‑fold sense consciousnesses, produce material phenomena originated by consciousness, starting from the first moment of life‑continuum, but they produce them only in the submoment of arising. Here the absorption javanas also maintain bodily postures. But the determining consciousness, sense‑sphere javanas, and direct‑knowledge consciousness also produce (bodily and verbal communication). Moreover, thirteen javanas accompanied by joy also produce a smile.
Commentary on § 11
Material phenomena originated by consciousness (cittasamuṭṭhānarūpa): Material phenomena produced by consciousness appear from the arising moment of the first life‑continuum citta immediately after rebirth‑consciousness. Rebirth‑consciousness does not produce matter originated by consciousness because at the moment of rebirth the arising matter is produced by kamma, and also because rebirth‑consciousness is just arriving in the new existence. The five‑fold sense consciousnesses lack the power to produce matter, and the four immaterial resultant consciousnesses cannot do so because they arise only in immaterial worlds. According to the commentaries, mental phenomena are strongest at the moment of arising, while material phenomena are strongest at the moment of presence. Therefore consciousness produces matter only at its arising moment, when it is strongest, not at the moments of presence or dissolution.
Absorption javanas, etc.: The maintenance of bodily posture is a function of states of consciousness. The twenty‑six absorption javanas perform this function minimally, keeping the body in a sitting, standing, or lying posture. The other thirty‑two cittas — the determining consciousness, sense‑sphere javanas, and direct‑knowledge consciousness — not only sustain postures but also activate bodily and verbal communication.
Thirteen ... also produce a smile: An ordinary worldling may smile or laugh with any of the four consciousnesses rooted in greed accompanied by joy, or with any of the four great wholesome consciousnesses accompanied by joy. Practitioners smile with six of these consciousnesses — the two associated with wrong view are excluded. Arahants may smile with one of five consciousnesses — these are the four great functional consciousnesses associated with joy and the rootless consciousness producing a smile.
§ 12. Temperature as a Mode of Origination
The fire element, which comprises both cold and heat, on reaching the stage of presence produces, according to circumstances, both internal and external material phenomena originating from temperature.
Commentary on § 12
Material phenomena originating from temperature (utusamuṭṭhānarūpa): Beginning with the stage of presence at the moment of rebirth, the internal fire element present in the material groups produced by kamma, combining with the external fire element, starts to produce organic material phenomena originating from temperature. Thereafter, the fire element in material groups born from all four causes produces organic material phenomena born from temperature throughout existence. Externally, temperature, or the fire element, also produces inorganic material phenomena such as climatic and geological transformations.
§ 13. Nutriment as a Mode of Origination
Nutriment, known as nutritive essence, on reaching the stage of presence produces material phenomena originating from nutriment at the time of its ingestion.
Commentary on § 13
Material phenomena originating from nutriment (āhārasamuṭṭhānarūpa): Internal nutritive essence, supported by external nutriment, produces material phenomena at the moment of presence, starting from the time of ingestion. The nutritive essence that has reached presence in material groups originating from nutriment produces a further pure octad, and the nutritive essence in that octad produces yet another octad; thus the arising of octads continues one after another for ten or twelve times. Food taken by the mother during pregnancy permeates the foetus's body, causing matter to arise in the child. It is said that even food smeared on the body produces matter. The nutritive essence in internal groups born from the other three causes also produces several rounds of pure octads in succession. Food taken in one day can sustain the body for seven days.
§ 14. Analysis by Way of Origination
The heart‑base and the eight controlling faculties are produced by kamma. The two means of communication are produced exclusively by consciousness. Sound is produced by consciousness and temperature. The triple qualities of lightness, malleability, and wieldiness arise from temperature, consciousness, and nutriment. The inseparable material phenomena and the space‑element arise from all four causes. The material characteristics do not arise from any cause.
Commentary on § 14
Articulate [intelligently uttered] sounds are caused by consciousness, inarticulate [all other sounds] by temperature. The triple qualities of lightness, malleability, and wieldiness arise from favourable climatic conditions, a brisk state of mind, and suitable nutriment, while unfavourable climate depresses mental states and unsuitable nutriment causes heaviness, stiffness, and unwieldiness in the physical body. The space‑element arises as the intervals between material groups produced by the four causes, and is therefore considered derivatively born from all four causes. The reason why the material characteristics do not arise from any cause is explained in the next section.
Grouping of Material Phenomena (Kalāpayojanā)
§ 16. In Brief
Twenty‑one material groups exist because they arise together, cease together, share a common base, and occur together.
Commentary on § 16
Material phenomena do not occur singly but arise in combinations or groups known as rūpakalāpas, of which there are twenty‑one. Just as all mental factors possess four characteristics, so too do material phenomena in a group. All material phenomena in a group arise together and cease together. They share a common base, namely the coexisting great primaries, which are the direct cause both for the derived phenomena and for each other. And they all occur together, from arising up to cessation.
§ 17. Groups Produced by Kamma
Life and the eight inseparable material phenomena together with the eye are called the eye‑decad. Similarly (combining the first nine) with the ear we obtain the ear‑decad, and likewise the nose‑decad, tongue‑decad, body‑decad, femininity‑decad, masculinity‑decad, heart‑base‑decad are obtained accordingly. The inseparable material phenomena together with life are called the vital nonad. These nine groups originate from kamma.
§ 18. Groups Produced by Consciousness
The inseparable material phenomena constitute the pure octad. These together with bodily communication form the bodily‑communication nonad; together with verbal communication and sound — the verbal‑communication decad; together with the material phenomena of the triple lightness — the lightness‑triple undecad [group of 11]; the bodily‑communication and lightness‑triple dodecad [group of 12]; the verbal‑communication, sound and lightness‑triple tridecad [group of 13]. These six material groups originate from consciousness.
§ 19. Groups Produced by Temperature
Pure octad, sound‑nonad, lightness‑triple undecad, sound‑and‑lightness‑triple dodecad — these four groups originate from temperature.
§ 20. Groups Originating from Nutriment
Pure octad and lightness‑triple undecad — two material groups originate from nutriment.
§ 21. Internal and External
Among them, two material groups produced by temperature — the pure octad and the sound‑nonad — can also be external. All the others are strictly internal.
Patterns of Occurrence of Material Phenomena (Rūpappavattikkama)
§ 23. In the Sense‑Sphere World
All these material phenomena are present in the required amount, according to circumstances, throughout existence in the sense‑sphere world. But at rebirth‑consciousness for moisture‑born beings and spontaneously‑born beings, a maximum of seven decads arise — the eye‑decad, ear‑decad, nose‑decad, tongue‑decad, body‑decad, sex‑decad, and heart‑base‑decad. At a minimum, sometimes the eye‑decad, ear‑decad, nose‑decad, and sex‑decad are not obtained.
For womb‑born creatures, three decads arise (at the moment of rebirth) — the body‑decad, sex‑decad, and heart‑base‑decad. However, sometimes the sex‑decad is not obtained. Afterwards, in the course of existence, the eye‑decad, etc., gradually arise.
Commentary on § 23
According to Buddhism, there are four modes of birth: egg‑born (aṇḍaja) beings, womb‑born (jalābuja), moisture‑born (saṃsedaja), and spontaneously‑born (opapātika). Moisture‑born beings include certain lower forms of animal life. Spontaneously‑born beings are usually not visible to the physical eye — petas and devas generally belong to this class. Under the term “womb‑born” in this text, egg‑born beings are also included.
§ 24. Continuity of Occurrence
Thus the arising of material groups produced by the four modes — namely, those born of kamma from the moment of rebirth‑consciousness, those born of consciousness from the second moment of consciousness, those born of temperature from the stage of presence (of the first moment), those born of nutriment from the moment of mixing of nutritive essence — flows on continuously in the sense‑sphere world up to the very end of life, like the flame of a lamp or the flow of a river.
§ 25. At the Time of Death
But at the time of death, starting from the stage of presence of the seventeenth consciousness preceding death‑consciousness, material phenomena born of kamma no longer arise. The material phenomena born of kamma that were produced earlier occur up to the moment of death and then cease. Next, material phenomena born of consciousness and born of nutriment cease. After that, the material qualities produced by temperature continue to exist in the form of a corpse.
Thus for beings who have died, material phenomena arise again in the next life, beginning with rebirth‑consciousness, in the same way.
§ 27. In the Fine‑Material World
In the fine‑material world, the nose‑decad, tongue‑decad, body‑decad, sex‑decad, and material groups produced by nutriment are not found. Therefore for these beings, at the time of rebirth‑consciousness, four material groups produced by kamma arise — the three decads of eye, ear, and heart‑base, and the vital nonad. Material phenomena produced by consciousness and temperature are not found in the course of [fine‑material] existence.
Commentary on § 27
Beings in the fine‑material worlds, being asexual, lack the two sex‑decads, and although they possess the physical forms of nose, tongue, and body, these organs lack sensory sensitivity.
§ 28. Among Non‑Percipient Beings
Among non‑percipient beings, the eye, ear, heart‑base, and sound are also not found. And material phenomena born of consciousness are not found there either. Therefore at their rebirth‑moment only the vital nonad arises. In the course of existence, with the exception of sound, material phenomena produced by temperature continue to arise.
