NIBBĀNA

 

Definition


 Nibbānaṃ pana lokuttarasaṅkhātaṃ
catu-magga-ñāṇena sacchikātabbaṃ magga-phalānaṃ ālambana-bhūtaṃ vāna-saṅkhātāya taṇhāya nikkhantattā
“Nibbāna”nti pavuccati.

 

Nibbāna is called supramundane and is to be realized by the knowledge of the four paths. It becomes the object of the paths and fruits and is called “Nibbāna” because it is the departure from craving, which is termed entanglement.

 

Commentary
 Nibbāna is called supramundane: Etymologically the word Nibbāna—the Pāli form of the better-known Sanskrit nirvāṇa—is derived from the verb nibbāti, meaning “to be extinguished” or “to be blown out.” Thus it denotes the extinction of the worldly “fires” of greed, hatred, and delusion. But the Pāli commentators prefer to treat the term as the negation or “departure from” (nikkhantatta) entanglement (vāna) of craving. This derivation is the one proposed here. For as long as a being is entangled in craving, it remains bound in saṃsāra, the cycle of birth and death; but when all craving is eradicated, Nibbāna is attained—the liberation from the cycle of birth and death.

 

 

Analysis

 

Tadetaṃ sabhāvato ekavidhaṃ pi
sa-upādisesa-nibbāna-dhātu anupādisesa-nibbāna-dhātu cā ti duvidhaṃ hoti kāraṇa-pariyāyena. Tathā suññataṃ animittaṃ appaṇihitañ cā ti tividhaṃ hoti ākāra-bhedena.

 

Although Nibbāna is single in its real nature, in regard to the basis for distinction it is twofold, namely: the Nibbāna-element with residue and the Nibbāna-element without residue.
And it is threefold according to its different aspects, which are: voidness, the signless, and the desireless.

 

Commentary

Although Nibbāna is single in its real nature, etc.: Nibbāna is a single, undifferentiated, ultimate reality. It is exclusively supramundane and has one intrinsic nature (sabhāva), namely that it is the unconditioned deathless element which has completely transcended the conditioned world. Nevertheless, in relation to the basis for distinction it is said to be twofold. The basis for distinction is the presence or absence of the five aggregates. The Nibbāna-element experienced by arahants is called “with residue” (sa-upādisesa), because although all defilements have been extinguished, the “residue” of the five aggregates acquired by past clinging remains throughout the life of the arahant. The Nibbāna-element attained at the death of the arahant is called “without residue” (anupādisesa), because the five aggregates are abandoned and will never again be acquired. These two Nibbāna-elements are also called in the commentaries the extinction of the defilements (kilesa-parinibbāna) and the extinction of the aggregates (khandha-parinibbāna).

And it is threefold according to its different aspects: Nibbāna is called void (suññata) because it is devoid of greed, hatred, and delusion, and because it is devoid of all conditioned things. It is called signless (animitta) because it is free from the signs of greed, hatred, and delusion, and free from the signs of all conditioned things. It is called desireless (appaṇihita) because it is free from the striving of greed, [hatred and delusion] and because it is not an object of craving.

 

 

Summary

 

Padaṃ accutaṃ accantaṃ asaṅkhataṃ anuttaraṃ
Nibbānaṃ iti bhāsanti vānamuttā mahesayo.
Iti cittaṃ cetasikaṃ rūpaṃ Nibbānaṃ icc’api
Paramatthaṃ pakāsenti catudhāva tathāgatā.

 

The great sages who are freed from craving declare Nibbāna to be an objective state that is deathless, absolutely endless, unconditioned, and unsurpassed.
Thus the Tathāgatas make known the ultimate reality as fourfold—consciousness, mental factors, matter, and Nibbāna.
