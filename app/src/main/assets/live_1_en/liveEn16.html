VICTORY OVER THE ARMY OF MARA 1



When the Bodhisatta sat on the unsurpassed (aparājita) throne at the tree of Great Awakening (Mahā Bodhi) in order to attain omniscience (sabbaññutā-ñāṇa), Sakka 2came to pay homage and stood blowing the Vijayuttara conch shell, 120 cubits long, the sound of which ceased only after four months. Deva Pancasikha paid homage playing the harp made of beluva wood, Deva Suyama stood waving a yak tail fan, Deva Santusita stood with a fan in his hands, and Brahma Sahampati held a white umbrella three yojanas long.

 

The Naga King Kala arrived with 80,000 Nagas and paid homage by reciting hundreds of verses in praise of the Bodhisatta. All the devas and Brahmas of the ten thousand world elements gathered to pay homage, bringing garlands of fragrant flowers and incense, and singing songs of praise.

 

Vasavatti Mara, having given up his heavenly pleasures, followed the Bodhisatta all this time during six years of severe austerities, waiting for the moment when the Bodhisatta might have wrong or evil thoughts (micchā-vitakka), such as thoughts about sensual pleasures (kāma-vitakka). But not finding the slightest deviation from right thoughts in the Bodhisatta, Mara thought: “Now Siddhattha has attained the Bodhi tree to become a Buddha. At present he is striving to escape from my power. I cannot allow him to leave the three worlds under my control. 3".

 

With this thought he went to the abode of Vasavatti and, gathering his warrior forces, he commanded: "Maras, assume fearsome forms and proceed towards Prince Siddhattha, like a great torrent of water falling with incredible force." He himself followed them, riding the elephant Girimehala, measuring 150 yojanas. Having created a thousand hands, he held a different weapon in each of them.

 

Mara, mentioned here, was not the legitimate ruler of the kingdom of Vasavatti. The legitimate ruler of Vasavatti was another deva. Just as a rebellious leader with many followers in the human world actively rebels against the state by plundering and committing robberies, Mara was actually only a powerful inhabitant of the abode of Paranimmitavasavatti, leading a rebellion there with a large retinue of evil devas. They created great obstacles for the men, devas and brahmas in their performance of pious activities.

 

The Bodhisattva was surrounded by such a huge army of Maras that their depth was twelve yojanas, their width on the right side was twelve yojanas, on the left side twelve yojanas, and their height above him was nine yojanas. Behind them they extended to the very edge of the ten thousand world system 4. The terrifying cries and noise of the numerous armies of the Maras could be heard at a distance of a thousand yojanas, resembling the sounds of a huge landslide. Mara himself, holding a thousand different weapons in his thousand created hands, and his numerous troops, each with a different weapon, transformed themselves into terrifying figures with diverse faces and advanced on the Bodhisatta to crush him.

 

When the vast armies of the Maras thus approached the tree of Great Awakening (Mahā Bodhi), none of the devas 5, including Sakka, who had previously paid homage to the Bodhisatta, could not bear it. They fled in different directions. There was not a single deva or brahma who dared to remain near the Bodhisatta and the tree of Great Awakening. At this time, the Bodhisatta remained sitting alone, like the Great Brahma in a secluded abode.

 

Clear omens of evil foreshadowed Mara's approach. These omens included: the fall of meteors; the onset of total darkness with the appearance of fog; a violent shaking of the oceans and the great earth; the appearance of darkness over the oceans; the flow of some rivers going in the opposite direction; rocks falling from the mountains; trees being overturned; violent storms and winds; the appearance of terrifying sounds caused by these winds; the disappearance of the sun in the darkness and the appearance in the sky and in the darkness of headless bodies. When Mara appeared with these clear omens, the Bodhisatta remained seated without the slightest fear, like Garuda among birds or the lion-king Kesaraja among beasts.

 

Even with the appearance of the above-mentioned ominous omens, Mara remained at a distance, not daring to approach the immediate vicinity of the Great Awakening Tree. Not daring to approach, Mara's great armies surrounded the Bodhisatta on all sides. Surveying his hordes, Mara could only give orders, but he himself could not approach the Great Awakening Tree, just as a fly cannot approach a white-hot iron. He said to his troops: "There is no one who can compare with Siddhattha, the son of Rajah Suddhodana. We cannot attack him head-on; we will attack him from the rear."

 

Looking around him to the front, left and right, the Bodhisatta saw that all the devas and brahmas had fled. Then, seeing Mara's troops advancing to attack him from the north, he thought: 'Such a huge number of Mara's troops are making great efforts for the sole purpose of attacking me. Here in this place, there is neither my mother, nor my father, nor my brother, nor my other relatives. Only the ten perfections (pairs) that I have developed and cultivated for so long will serve as my companions and retinue. Therefore, relying only on these companions of mine, the ten perfections, it is right to destroy these hordes of Maras by attacking them with the weapons of my perfections.' He then concentrated on the merit of the ten perfections. 6.

Compassion of the Bodhisattva

 

Sapāda-mūle kīḷantaṁ passanto taruṇaṁ sutaṁ,

pitā vudikkhi taṁ Māraṁ mettāyanto dayaparo.

 

A very compassionate father, to whom his children are indebted, will not show the slightest anger if his little son, playing at his feet, hits him with his hands and feet or hurts him in any way. Instead of getting angry, he will embrace him and press him to his chest to let him sleep with fatherly love and care.

 

In the same way, the noble Bodhisatta showed tolerance towards all the evil deeds of the wicked Mara and did not feel the least bit sad. He looked at Mara without fear, but with kindness and compassion.

 

Tadā so asambhivācaṁ sīha-nādaṁ nadī muni,

na jānāti sayaṁ mayhaṁ, dāsa-bhāva-piyaṁ khaḷo.

Yena kenaci kammena jāto Deva-pure vare,

sakaṁ gatiṁ ajānanto loka-jeṭṭho ti maññati.

 

When Mara with his great army approached and began to disturb the Bodhisatta, he spoke these words:

 

"This evil Mara is completely unaware that he himself has fallen into misfortune. Having been born in the abode of Vasavatti merely due to a few good deeds, he does not even know anything about his own duration of life, the time of his death, or the place of suffering to which he will be condemned after death. Yet he thinks, 'I am constant. I am the one who rules the whole world.'

 

He does not reflect and is unaware of his own position and the danger of falling into the lower realms. Due to such ignorance, he dares to commit such atrocities."

 

Ananta-loka-khātumhi sattānaṁ hi kataṁ subhaṁ,

mayheka-pāramiyā pi kalaṁ nagghati soḷasiṁ.

Tiracchāno saso hutvā disvā yācakam-āgataṁ,

pacitvāna sakaṁ maṁsaṁ pati-oggimhi dātave.

 

“If the accumulated good deeds performed by all beings in all the countless worlds were placed on one scale and compared with my accumulated good deeds performed in the form of perfections (pairs) placed on the other scale, the merits of all beings would not reach even one 256th part of those merits arising from one perfection out of ten that I have practiced.”


Even in the form of a small hare in the animal kingdom, showing the greatest courage, he jumped into a blazing fire to have his meat roasted, and with joy and delight he did so, seeing the one who came to ask. 7

 

Ordinary people might take the Bodhisatta's bold words, "If the accumulated merits made by beings in all the countless worlds were compared with the merits arising from just one of my ten perfections, they would not reach even one 256th of the merits of that one perfection," as an exaggeration. But in fact, this was not the case: these words were quite natural and true.

 

People other than the Bodhisatta usually perform good deeds with the desire to attain human or divine happiness. Therefore, their accumulated merits only lead to the continuation of the cycle of suffering. This is similar to those who spend their wealth on personal needs immediately after receiving it and do not save anything for the future. Thus, all beings other than the Bodhisatta, carried away by the enjoyment of the cycle of existences (vattābhirata), remain poor, lacking wealth accumulated from merit.

 

The Bodhisatta, however, aimed exclusively at omniscience (sabbaññutā-ñāṇa) every time he performed a virtuous action to fulfill the perfections. As a result, all the merits the Bodhisatta had performed remained intact, without losing their power, in his stream of consciousness until they bore the fruit of omniscience. Thus, just as one who does not squander but saves his means, daily accumulating more and more wealth, the Bodhisatta, through life after life acquiring an ever-increasing wealth of merit, became extremely rich in terms of his virtuous actions.

 

One rich man, possessing a billion-dollar fortune, cannot be compared with many poor people in terms of wealth. They will inevitably lose in this comparison. Likewise, a Bodhisatta, who is incredibly rich, possessing incalculable wealth of merit accumulated through perfections, cannot be compared with beings from countless world systems. They are doomed to defeat, since they have become impoverished in terms of merit, having squandered it immediately after receiving it for the sake of acquiring well-being (bhava-sampatti) and enjoying pleasures (bhoga-sampatti). Therefore, the Bodhisatta’s bold statement is not an exaggeration; it is natural and true.

 

Evaṁ ananta-kālesu kataṁ dukkara-kārikaṁ,

ko hi nāma kareyyañño anummatto sacetano.

 

"For so many countless ages I have performed deeds requiring great effort. Who else, without being mad or possessing a wholesome consciousness, could have done this?"

"Mara does not know who I really am; that I have acquired this identity of mine in this life due to the mentioned innumerable meritorious deeds. Therefore he thinks that I am an ordinary person."

 

Nāhaṁ manussomanusso na Brahmā na ca devatā,

jarā-maraṇaṁ lokassa dassetuṁ panidhāgato.

 

"In fact, I am not an ordinary person who has just been born; I am not a yakkha, nor a Brahma, nor a deva. I was born in a human womb in order to reveal the Truths of the Noble Ones to all living beings."

 

It cannot be said with certainty that the Bodhisatta was a human being, a Yakkha, a Deva, a Mara, or a Brahma, since none of these beings perform tasks similar to those performed by the Bodhisatta.

 

The question may then arise, why did the Bodhisatta have to be born of a human woman, Queen Maya? Therefore, it was said that he accepted conception in the womb of a human woman in order to show all sentient beings - humans, devas and brahmas - the sufferings of old age, illness and death in the cycle of existence.

 

There is no Deva, no Mara, no Brahma, no self (atta) that can create or give birth to any sentient being. In reality, it is only the thirst (tanha) that arises in the mental continuum of every being that is responsible for the repetition of rebirths (patisandhi). It is the force of thirst that leads to the commission of good and bad deeds.

 

Action (kamma) is like a field, consciousness (viññana) is like a seed, and thirst (tanha) or greed (lobha) is like water. Just as a tree sprout appears due to the combination of soil, seed, and water, so a sentient being appears due to the combination of the soil of action (kamma), the seed of consciousness (viññana), and the moisture of thirst or greed. In the absence of the moisture of thirst or greed, even though the soil of action and the seed of consciousness are present, the sprout of the tree of existence will not appear. Therefore, noble arhats who have uprooted the moisture of thirst or greed are not reborn in a new existence.

 

Thus, sentient beings, who arise from the combination of the three causes, are overwhelmed by a stream of various sufferings, such as birth, etc. A Bodhisatta is one who wants to eliminate all these sufferings that afflict sentient beings.

 

The root cause of all sufferings such as birth, etc., is craving (tanha). If craving is eradicated, birth does not occur. If birth does not occur, old age, death, etc., do not occur. Therefore, craving should be eradicated first of all; and from delusion (moha) this craving arises in sentient beings who seek happiness by perceiving the six sense objects (arammana), such as visible objects (ruparammana), sounds, tastes, smell, touch, thoughts, as permanent (nicca-sanya), pleasant (sukha-sanya), beautiful (subha-sanya), or unchanging (atta-sanya). And craving can be abandoned only when the defects of the six sense objects, which are the source of craving, are manifested.

 

Again, the faults of the six sense objects mean that they are characterized by a state of impermanence; the state of impermanence, in turn, can be recognized only when the nature of old age and death is seen from the apparently primitive, simple level; only when a being's old age and death are seen can one begin to know the teaching, straighten the views, and move along the Eightfold Path and finally see the true impermanence and faults of the six sense objects and the six sense organs at the deep level of the arising and passing away of mind and matter in each moment; and only when these faults are seen can one abandon the craving and greed that cling to the six sense objects. Only when craving and greed can be abandoned will the suffering of Samsara, such as birth, old age, and so on, cease. In this case, the initial vision and understanding of old age and death is the fundamental and most essential factor in the cessation of the suffering of Samsara. And so the Bodhisatta came into this human world and was conceived in the womb of Queen Maya.

 

If a Bodhisatta, having been born as a Deva or Brahma, were to teach about the nature of old age and death and perform miracles, others would not believe him, thinking, "This Deva or Brahma, although he himself enjoys happiness, is constant and unchanging, comes and teaches us the doctrine of impermanence, suffering and not-self, what kind of teaching is this?" They would not listen to him with respect. They would simply think, "Is there anything that Brahma cannot teach us? There is nothing that he cannot teach. And are there any miracles that he cannot perform? Indeed, he can perform all miracles. So his teaching a doctrine or performing a miracle is not something wonderful."

 

As is attested by many, the Bodhisatta was born of Queen Maya; when he reached maturity, he enjoyed sensual pleasures; when a son was born to him, he abandoned the son, renounced the world, and became a recluse; after he had practiced severe austerities (dukkara-cariya), he eventually attained knowledge of the path and omniscience and became a Buddha. When, therefore, he began to teach the Dhamma, or to make manifest the nature of impermanence, or to explain the three characteristics (anicca, dukkha, anatta 8), all the people listened to his teaching with respect, thinking: “Even this noble man, endowed with great power and wisdom and knowing all aspects of the Dhamma, cannot overcome old age, illness and death, what to speak of us?”

 

Our Buddha, who teaches us to get rid of sufferings such as birth, old age, etc., truly understands the true nature of all things! Indeed, Nibbana, where there is no suffering such as birth, old age, etc., is blissful!" With this faith they follow the Buddha's teaching, applying reason to it, and come to understand that this individuality (atta-bhava), which is actually the five aggregates of clinging (upadanakkhandha), is suffering (dukkha) and the source of suffering.

 

They also perfectly discern the faults of craving and greed which give rise to this body, which is in reality the five aggregates of clinging. Having discerned this, sentient beings experience fear and aversion toward craving, which is the truth of the origin of suffering (samudaya-sacca); and toward the five groups of clinging, which are the truth of suffering (dukkha-sacca) arising through craving. When they do this, they are prepared to attain final Nibbana (anupada-parinibbana), the complete cessation of suffering. Therefore the Bodhisatta said, "I have come to this human world and have been conceived in the lotus-like womb of Queen Maya in order to show all sentient beings the suffering of birth, old age, and death in the cycle of existence.

 

 

Anupalitto lokena jātonanta-jino ahaṁ,

Buddho bodhi-tale hutvā, tāremi janataṁ bahuṁ

 

Unstained by the world, born a conqueror, I, having become a Buddha at the Bodhi tree, will save many beings.

 

 

Samantā dhajinaṁ disvā, yuddhaṁ Māraṁ savāhanaṁ,

yuddhāya paccugacchāmi, mā maṁ ṭhānā acāvayi

 

Seeing the banners of Mara and her army from all sides, I go out to battle - you will not move me from my place, Mara.

 

Yan-te taṁ nappasahati senaṁ loko sadevako,

tan-te paññāya gacchāmi, āmaṁ pattaṁ va asmanā.

 

Your army, which the world and the devas cannot defeat, I will smash with wisdom, just as an unbaked cup is smashed with a stone.

 

Icchanto sāsape gabbhe caṅkamāmi ito cito,

icchanto loka-dhātumhi, attabhāvena chādayi.

 

If I wish, I walk back and forth inside the mustard seed, If I wish, I fill the world system with my being. 9

 

Ete sabbe gahetvāna, cuṇṇetuṁ accharāya pi,

atthi thāmaṁ balaṁ mayhaṁ pāṇa-ghāto na vaṭṭati.

 

Taking all of them [Mara's army], in the blink of an eye, grind them [into powder] I have strength and power, [but] killing living beings does not befit [me].

 

 

Imassa gaṇḍuppādassa āyudhena balena kiṁ,

mayhaṁ hi tena pāpena, sallāpo pi na yujjati.

 

What do I care about your weapons and strength? Even [to hold] conversation with such a vicious [being] is not fitting for me.

Before the Bodhisatta had spoken these words, Mara had asked, "Siddhartha, why do you occupy this unconquered throne that belongs to me?" The Bodhisatta replied, "Who is the witness who can prove that the unconquered throne belongs to you?" Mara spread out his hands, saying, "What is the use of looking for other witnesses, all my troops who are now before you are witnesses." At that moment, millions of his hordes appeared simultaneously, creating a noise like a landslide, shouting, "I am the witness, I am the witness." Then the Bodhisatta, holding back Mara's hordes, recited the following verse with the intention of presenting his witness.

 

Pallaṅkaṁ mama bhāvāya, kim-atthaññena sakkhinā,

kampitā maddiyā dānā, sakkhi hoti ayaṁ mahī.



[This is] the throne for my becoming - what is the point of another witness?

Shaking with my [power], this earth is now [my] witness.

 

At that very moment, the great earth shook violently. The sound emanating from the earth caused the entire expanse of the sky to resonate loudly, like thunder. The seven mountain ranges, as well as the Himalayas, emitted a deep, continuous sound. The entire 10,000 world system shook. The entire cloudless sky rumbled frighteningly, as if rice grains were cracking loudly in a red-hot frying pan.

 

Mara, finding himself caught between earth and sky amidst the incessant noise and din, greatly frightened, without shelter or help, lowered his battle-standard and, abandoning his weapons on the spot, fled in confusion, not even being able to look back at his elephant Girmekhalu. As Mara fled, his great armies disintegrated, scattering in confusion in all directions, like ashes blown away by a storm; at last they returned to the world of Vasavatti.

 

At that moment, seeing Mara's great armies disintegrating, the Devas and Brahmas who had fled in fear at Mara's appearance and who were watching to see, "Who will emerge victorious? Who will be the vanquished?" simultaneously proclaimed in deep praise the joyful news, "Mara has been vanquished. Prince Siddhartha has emerged victorious. We will celebrate and honor his victory" (jayo hi buddhassa sirimato ayaṁ - "Truly, this is the victory of the blessed Buddha"). This news was passed from one Naga to another Naga, from one Garuda to another Garuda, from one Deva to another Deva, from one Brahma to another Brahma. Carrying incense and fragrant flowers in their hands, they gathered at the throne of Maha Bodhi, where the Bodhisatta resided.

 

Thus, with the victory over Mara Vasavatti before sunset on the full moon day of the month of Vesakha, in 528 B.C., the Bodhisatta became the conqueror of the entire world of living beings and attained a secure, flawless, fearless state.

In describing the path taken by the Bodhisatta during his journey and his activities on the full moon of the month of Vesakha in 528 BCE, the commentaries on the Chronicles of the Buddhas (Buddha-vaṁsa), the Introduction to the Birth Histories (Jātaka-nidāna) and the Ornaments of the Conqueror (Jinālaṅkāra) generally agree in the substance of their narratives. They differ slightly only in their presentation, some presenting things briefly and others in greater detail.

 

However, in describing the Bodhisatta's midday rest in the Sala forest, the commentaries to the Chronicles of the Buddhas and the Introduction to the Jataka Birth Stories make no mention of the attainment of jhanas (jhāna-samāpatti) and super-knowledge (abhiññā). On the other hand, the Jinālaṅkāra states that the Bodhisatta attained the eight mundane jhanas and the five mundane super-knowledges while spending the day in the Sala forest.

 

It is also stated that by the time he arrived at the Maha Bodhi tree, the noble Bodhisatta already possessed physical strength (majjhima-purisa) and the power of super-knowledge (abhiññā). This statement by the author of the Jinālaṅkāra is consistent with his other statements, namely: "When Mara advanced on him to fight, the noble Bodhisatta said: 'If I wish, I can cover the entire world system with my body', and also: 'Pointing as evidence to the great benefits accomplished in his existence as Vessantara'," which could only be known through knowledge of past existences (pubbe-nivāsānussati-abhiññā).

 

According to the author of the Jinālaṅkāra, the Bodhisatta had already acquired the eight mundane jhānas and the five mundane superknowledges by the time he met the leaders, Alara and Udaka. Without using them during the six years of ascetic practice (dukkara-cariyā), they had become as if stained, like golden bowls that become dull without use; the Bodhisatta purified them anew while in the Sala forest, as if polishing stained bowls. The implication is evidently that it was only after his victory over Mara that the Bodhisatta made full use of the knowledge of past existences (pubbe-nivāsa-abhiññā) and divine vision (dibba-cakkhu-abhiññā) he had already acquired to attain the stage of insight into truths. These observations are made here because the view of the author of the Jinālaṅkāra seems worthy of attention.

 

1  1. Mara skandhas (khandha-māra) are the five aggregates of clinging (form, feelings, perception, formations, consciousness), which are the basis of suffering and an obstacle to liberation. They "kill" the possibility of attaining nibbana through attachment to the "I".

 

2. The māra of defilements (kilesa-māra) is the mental obscurations: greed (lobha), hatred (dosa), delusion (moha) and their derivatives. They "pollute" the mind and prevent one from seeing the true nature of reality.

 

3. The mara of kammic formations (abhisaṅkhāra-māra) are the wholesome and unwholesome volitional actions that lead to rebirth and continued existence in samsara.

 

4. Mara as a deity (devaputta-māra) - personified Mara, a deity who represents all obstacles to Awakening. Actively opposes the spiritual progress of beings.

 

5. The Mara of Death (maccu-māra) is death itself, interrupting life and the possibility of practice. It symbolizes impermanence and the finitude of existence in samsara.

 

Vasavatti-māra is devaputta-māra, that is, Māra as a deity/deva, the fourth type listed.

He is the ruler of the highest sky of the world of sensual desires (Paranimmita-vasavatti) and is therefore called Vasavatti. In the texts he is described as a powerful deity who tries to prevent beings from attaining liberation.

In the Samyutta Nikaya (SN 4) there is an entire section (Māra-saṃyutta) devoted to the meetings of this Mara with the Buddha and his disciples. There Mara-devaputta is described as a being with supernatural powers and the ability to take various forms.

 

Although Vasavatti-mara is a specific being (deva), he also symbolizes all the obstacles on the path to Awakening, especially those related to sensual desires and attachments.

 

 

2  Sakka (Sakka) - in Buddhist cosmology is the ruler of the heavenly kingdom of Tavatimsa (Heaven of the Thirty-Three). In the Pali Canon he is also known as Indra or Maghava.

3  Mara is considered the ruler of the kama-loka (the world of sensual desires), but his influence to a certain extent extends to all three worlds of samsara, since beings in them are still subject to ignorance and attachments. Only the attainment of nibbana allows one to completely escape from the power of Mara.

4  The ten-thousand world system is a cosmological concept described in Buddhist texts.

The Anguttara Nikaya (AN 3.80) explains the hierarchy of world systems:

1,000 worlds = small thousandth world system

1,000 small thousandth systems = average thousandth system (or millionth)

1,000 average systems = ten thousandth world system

Thus, the ten-thousand world system includes one billion (1,000,000,000) individual worlds.

The suttas often mention that the light from important events (such as the appearance of the Buddha) illuminates the ten-thousand world system, symbolizing the scale of the influence of the Dhamma.

 

5  Devas (Pali: deva) are celestial beings or deities in Buddhist cosmology. They reside in the higher realms and have subtler bodies and longer lifespans than humans. However, despite their favorable birth, devas are still in samsara and are subject to rebirth. According to the Buddha's teaching, being born as a human is even more favorable for achieving enlightenment than being born as a deva, since devas, living in pleasure and comfort, often lack the motivation to practice the Dhamma.

6  1. Dāna-pārāmi (dāna) - the perfection of generosity. The practice of giving material things, Dhamma and fearlessness (non-harm towards other beings).

2. Sīla-parāmi (sīla) - perfection of morality. Observance of ethical principles and purity of behavior.

3. Nekkhamma-parami (nekkhamma) - the perfection of renunciation. Renunciation of sensual pleasures and worldly life.

4. Paññā - perfection of wisdom. Penetration into the true nature of phenomena.

5. Viriya-parami (viriya) - perfection of diligence. Untiring efforts in developing the mind and helping others.

6. Khanti-parami (khanti) - perfection of patience. Fortitude in difficulties and acceptance of the unpleasant.

7. Sacca-parami (sacca) - perfection of truthfulness. Honesty in words and actions.

8. Adhitthana-parami (adhiṭṭhāna) - perfection of determination. Unwaveringness in following the path and any other good goal.

9. Metta-parami (mettā) - the perfection of goodwill. Unconditional goodwill towards all beings.

10. Upekkha-parami (upekkhā) - perfection of equanimity. Peace of mind in all circumstances.

7  This is a reference to the Jataka of the Hare, one of the stories about the Buddha's past lives.

In this story, the Bodhisatta was born as a hare and lived in the forest with other animals. One day, on a full moon day, he decided to observe uposatha (a day of observing special vows) and practice giving. Shakra (the king of the devas) came to him in the form of a brahmin, asking for alms. Since the hare had nothing but grass, which the brahmin could not eat, the hare decided to give the only thing he had - his own body. He jumped into a fire that the brahmin had made.

Shakra, touched by such self-sacrifice, revealed his true nature and in memory of this act placed an image of a hare on the moon (according to Asian tradition, the silhouette of a hare is visible on the moon).

This story illustrates the paramita of giving (dana-paramita) in its highest form – the willingness to give even one’s own life for the benefit of others.

8  Anicca - indicates that all constituent phenomena are continually changing, arising and passing away. Nothing remains the same even for a moment.
Dukkha - covers the entire spectrum of unsatisfactoriness: from gross physical suffering to subtle existential anxiety. It is the fundamental characteristic of unenlightened existence.

Anatta is the teaching that nowhere in the five aggregates (khandhas) can a permanent, independent self be found. This is in direct opposition to the popular idea of the existence of an unchanging soul or atman.
