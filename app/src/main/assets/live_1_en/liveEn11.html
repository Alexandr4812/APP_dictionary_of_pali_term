Namo tassa bhagavato arahato sammāsambuddhassa

Homage to the Blessed One, the Worthy One, the Fully Enlightened One.

 

 

Sabbapāpassa akaraṇaṃ,

To abstain from all evil;

Kusalassa upasampadā;

To cultivate good;

Sacitta pariyo dapanaṃ,

To purify one's mind;

Etaṃ buddhāna sāsanaṃ

This is the teaching of the Buddhas.

Atha Rājagahaṁ vara-Rājagahaṁ

Then to Rājagaha, the glorious Rājagaha,

nara-rāja-vare nagaraṁ tu gate

The city to which the noble ruler of the people (Bimbisāra) returned,

giri-rāja-varo muni-rāja-varo

Like the king of mountains (Mount Meru), the best among the kings of sages,

migi-rāja-gato sugato pi gato

With the grace of the king of beasts (the lion), the Well-Gone One (Sugata) also went.

 

According to this verse, which appears in the commentaries to the Chronicles of the Buddhas (Buddhavaṁsa, PTS 286) and in the Ornaments of the Victor (Jinālaṅkāra), when the righteous King Bimbisāra returned to the city of Rājagaha, the Bodhisatta set off on a journey in search of the bliss of Nibbāna, known as the supreme peace (santi-vara). Along the way, he reached the place of a great religious teacher named Ālāra, from the Kalāma clan.

As the commentary explains:

In this context, a question might arise: "Why did the noble Bodhisatta go to Ālāra and Uddaka, and not to the famous heretical teachers, such as Pūraṇa Kassapa and others, who claimed to be Buddhas?"

The reason was that the systems of practice of these heretical teachers had no real value. The Bodhisatta had already investigated them 91 aeons ago, personally following their paths to determine whether they had any true merit. Through his own experience, he knew for certain that they did not. Thus, he did not go to these heretical teachers but turned to the well-known teacher Ālāra, who led a religious life and was surrounded by many followers.

When the Bodhisatta came to Ālāra, the head of the community, he sincerely said: "Friend, you are from the Kalāma clan, I wish to lead the holy life in your system." Ālāra gladly gave his consent and said these encouraging words: "Noble friend, practice in our system! Our system allows a wise practitioner to quickly comprehend the teacher's view and dwell in bliss."

The Bodhisatta immediately began studying the new system, receiving permission from the head of the community. Being a person of extraordinary intelligence, he easily mastered Ālāra's doctrine and practice in theory. By simply following his teacher, the Bodhisatta reached a level of understanding where he could say: "I have understood!" He declared, "I have seen the path!" and the head of the community, as well as his students, agreed with this statement.

Reflecting on the confidence with which the head of the community, Ālāra, spoke of the path of practice that led to concentration in the realm of nothingness (ākiñcaññāyatana-jhāna), the Bodhisatta realized: "It seems that the head of the sect, Ālāra, did not merely learn this path as a theory based on faith alone, without personal wisdom. Truly, this teacher himself has achieved and realized the seven worldly attainments."

The Bodhisatta again approached the teacher and asked: "Friend from the Kalāma clan, to what extent do you claim to have realized this teaching yourself?" Since Ālāra had personally realized it, he responded like those who have practical knowledge, not just theoretical. He described in detail the seven attainments, culminating in the concentration in the realm of nothingness.

Then the Bodhisatta had the following thought: "Not only does the head of the community, Ālāra, possess the faith required to attain these concentrations, but I too have this faith. Not only does he have the energy, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom necessary for attaining concentration (appanā-jhāna), but I too have this energy, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom. Ālāra says that he himself has attained the seven worldly concentrations, including the realm of nothingness, with higher knowledge (abhiññā), and dwells in bliss. It would be good if I too strove to attain these seven worldly concentrations."

With this intention, he diligently performed the preliminary duties (kasiṇa-parikamma), and within two to three days, like his teacher Ālāra, he attained the seven worldly attainments, culminating in concentration in the realm of nothingness, and dwelled in bliss.

Then the Bodhisatta approached the head of the community, Ālāra, and asked: "Friend Kalāma, do you say that through this practice you have personally attained the seven worldly concentrations with higher knowledge and dwell in bliss?" When Ālāra affirmed this, the Bodhisatta informed him: "Friend, through this practice I too have personally attained the seven worldly concentrations, culminating in concentration in the realm of nothingness, and dwell in bliss."

Then, as befits a noble person free from mental impurities like envy (issā) and greed (macchariya), the head of the community, Ālāra, joyfully uttered words of altruism: "We have seen with our own eyes another ascetic with a sharp mind like yours, noble friend. This is a great fortune for us, friend!"

 

Issābhibhūta lokamhi

In this vast world where envy reigns,

Āḷāro yo anissukī

Ālāra, truly free from jealousy,

vaṇṇavādī sudhīrassa

Openly praises the virtues of the wisest (the Bodhisatta),

aho uḷārachandata

Oh, what noble and pure aspiration!

 

As previously mentioned, the head of the community, Ālāra, was free from envy and selfishness, which allowed him to express his words of praise with genuine joy. He continued: "Exalted man, what I have realized through higher knowledge and taught, you have also realized through higher knowledge. And what you have realized, I too have realized and taught. Therefore, the teaching that I have realized, you too have realized, and the teaching that you have realized, I too have realized. Thus, we are alike. Come, dear friend, let us lead this community of followers together in harmony."

Then he summoned his students and announced: "Students, I have attained the seven attainments, and this excellent man has also attained them, just like I have. You, students, should receive guidance from this excellent man in the same way you receive it from me." With these words, he entrusted half of his students to the Bodhisatta.

Not only did he fairly divide his followers, but he also urged the men and women who came to serve him with incense and flowers to honor the Bodhisatta, saying: "Go and pay homage to this distinguished man!"

As the head of the community instructed, men and women first paid their respects to the Bodhisatta, and only then to their teacher, using the remaining incense and flowers. When they offered fine beds and seats, they were instructed to give them first to the Bodhisatta, and only after that would Ālāra accept what was left, if anything remained.

Wherever they went, he always gave the best places for sleeping and residing to the Bodhisatta, demonstrating his generosity and respect. In this way, Ālāra treated the Bodhisatta, who was at that time his student, with the greatest honor and respect, even though he himself was the teacher.

The Bodhisatta, through his efforts, attained the seven worldly absorptions. In his past lives, he had already experienced these absorptions and possessed superior intellect, which helped him quickly realize the nature and possibilities of these attainments in his current life, as well as their potential benefit in the future.

He fully understood the nature and prospects of these absorptions, including the absorption into the realm of nothingness (ākiñcaññāyatana), and realized that in his next life, he would be reborn in the Brahma world of the Realm of Nothingness (Ākiñcaññāyatana).

Then he came to the conclusion that these seven worldly absorptions still belonged to the cycle of suffering (vaṭṭa-dukkha). He also reflected: "These attainments cannot lead to weariness of the cycle of suffering, to renunciation, to the cessation of defilements such as craving (rāga) and hatred (dosa), to the extinguishing of all these defilements, to the knowledge of all that must be known, to the realization of the four paths, or to the attainment of Nibbāna."

These seven absorptions may lead at most to the Brahma world of the Realm of Nothingness, where a being resides for 60,000 great aeons (mahā-kappa), but they provide no benefit beyond this. The highest Brahma world of the Realm of Nothingness, where this absorption occurs, is also not free from the dangers of birth, aging, and death. Essentially, it is a domain already ensnared by the traps of the king of death.

Like a person suffering from intense hunger who initially enjoys eating a large plate of delicious food, but later becomes repulsed by it due to a bile or phlegm disorder or because a fly landed in the food, and he leaves it, not thinking of taking another bite.

Similarly, the Bodhisatta, through his efforts, attained the aforementioned seven worldly absorptions within two or three days without much difficulty, and at first, he dwelled in and enjoyed these attainments. But from the moment he realized the aforementioned flaws, such as their belonging to the cycle of suffering, he completely lost interest in practicing these seven attainments, even with one of the five forms of mastery. He repeatedly abandoned these attainments, saying: "These seven attainments are useless! These seven attainments are useless!" And, disappointed in them, he left the community teacher Ālāra.
