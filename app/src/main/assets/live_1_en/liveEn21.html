Buddha's Arrival at Deer Grove

 

The Buddha continued on his way until he met the Group of Five Ascetics at Isipatana, in the Migadāya forest near Varanasi, on a cool evening during the full moon of the month of Āsāḷha (July) in the year 528 BCE.

Seeing the Buddha approaching from afar, the five ascetics agreed among themselves:

"Friends, the recluse Gotama is approaching, he who has abandoned ascetic practices. He has become one who aspires to obtain the four requisites. He has returned to the acquisition of the four requisites. Let us not bow down to the recluse Gotama. Let us not greet him. Let us not take the begging bowl from his hands. However, we will prepare a seat for him if he wishes to sit down."

 

Aware of the mood of the five ascetics, the Buddha developed goodwill (mettā) specifically directed towards them. As the Buddha came closer and closer, under the influence of the power of the Buddha (Buddha-tejānubhāva) and the splendour and power of his goodwill (mettā-tejānubhāva), they found that they could not keep their agreement, and all five came out to greet the Buddha; one took his begging bowl, another prepared a seat, a third brought water, a fourth laid a board, and a fifth brought a vessel for washing his feet.

The Buddha sat down and washed his feet as the ascetics had prepared. Although they paid due respect by physical acts, despite their agreement, they spoke to him as an equal, addressing him by the name Gotama and calling him friend (āvuso):

"Friend Gotama, when you were engaged in meditative practices in the Uruveḷā forest, we carried your begging bowl and clothes and went begging. We offered you water and toothpaste. We swept the grounds of the hermitage. Who looked after you, performing such duties, great and small, after we left? Were you not perplexed when we left you?"

After this Buddha said:

"Do not address me by the name Gotama or by the word 'friend', as if I were your equal. Ascetics, listen carefully, I have realized the deathless Nibbāna (amata-Nibbāna). I will instruct you. I will teach you the Dhamma. If you follow and practice according to the instructions I have given, you will soon realize, even in this life, direct knowledge, the happiness of the fruit of Arahatship, which is sought after by two kinds of noble men: those noble by birth (jāti-kula-putta), and those noble by virtuous conduct (ācārā-kula-putta), who renounce the world, giving up family life, and become ascetics in the service of the Doctrine (Sāsana)."

 

But the Group of Five Ascetics, being skeptical, replied, although with sincere faith:

"Friend Gotama! Even when you practiced and achieved absorptions (appānaka-jhāna) which are very difficult for ordinary people to achieve, while you practiced severe austerities (dukkarā-cariya) for six long years, you were unable to realize the knowledge of the Arhat's path (Arahatta-magga-ñāṇa) and omniscience (sabbaññutā-ñāṇa) which could make you noble (ariya). At that time you began to strive to acquire the four requisites and abandoned the meditative practices; now that you have returned to the acquisition of the four requisites, how could you achieve and realize the knowledge of the Arhat's path and omniscience which can make a person noble and which surpass the ten kinds of virtuous actions (kusalā-kamma-patha) of ordinary people?"

 

When he was thus told, the Buddha addressed the Group of Five:

"Ascetics, I am not one who aspires to acquire the four requisites. I am not one who has abandoned the practice of meditation. I am not one who has returned to the acquisition of the four requisites. Ascetics, I have become Awakened, possessing full, genuine, penetrating and clear knowledge of all knowable phenomena (sacca-ñeyya-dhamma), worthy of special veneration. Ascetics! Listen carefully. I have realized the deathless Nibbāna. I will instruct you. I will teach you the Dhamma. If you follow and practice in accordance with the instructions I have given, you will soon realize, even in this life, through direct knowledge, the happiness of the fruit of Arhatship, which is aspired for by these two kinds of noble men, namely, those noble by birth and those noble by virtue of good conduct, who renounce the world, giving up family life, and become ascetics in the service of the Dhamma. (Sāsana)."

But the five ascetics, still remaining skeptical, answered as before for the second time.

Although the Buddha told them for the third time as before, "I am not one who strives to acquire the four necessary things, and so on," the five ascetics, still remaining skeptical, answered as before for the third time.

After this, Buddha addressed them as follows:

 

"Ascetics, do you remember that when I was formerly engaged in meditative practices in the Uruvela forest, I came and spoke to you, in order to encourage you and save you from boredom, and also to make you form a high opinion of me, these words: 'My friends, do not be embarrassed and do not think of going elsewhere. I have begun to see the nimitta in meditation (kammaṭṭhāna-nimitta).'" The five ascetics then thought: "At the time when this ascetic Gotama was practicing meditation, we would easily have believed him if he had said: 'I have become an Arahant!' But he did not boast and never uttered lies at that time. Now he speaks only of the qualities (guṇa) that he actually possesses." After this single utterance of the Buddha, they became calm, regained their faith, and began to hold him in high esteem. And, fully convinced that he had truly become Awakened, they replied in recognition: No hetaṁ Bhante, “Venerable Buddha, we cannot recall you saying such words.”

 

After the Buddha had convinced the ascetics that he had become Awakened, the five ascetics listened to the Buddha's words with respect. They listened to him with full attention. They directed their minds to attaining the fruit of Arhatship. Having satisfied themselves that the Group of Five Ascetics had completely dispelled any doubt that he had truly become Awakened, the Buddha first delivered the Dhamma-cakka sermon, beginning with the words:

"There are these two extremes, monks..."



And so it went, on a cool Saturday evening, the full moon day of the month of Āsāḷha in 528 BCE. At that time, the sun as the moon, in conjunction with the constellation Uttarāsāḷha, was just rising in the east, conquering the darkness with its radiance.

 

As soon as the Buddha uttered, "Dveme bhikkhave ante...," the sound spread throughout all 10,000 world systems, reaching Bhavagga, the highest of the formless realms, and Avīci, the lowest of the hells below. By then, 180 million Brahmas who possessed the mature roots of merit for realizing the four truths had already gathered around the Buddha.

 

When the Buddha thus delivered the discourse on the Wheel of Dhamma, the venerable Koṇḍañña listened to the teaching, deeply concentrating his mind on the discourse throughout its course, and developed his knowledge (ñāṇa) so that he was established in the fruit of Stream-Entry (Sotāpatti-phala) along with 180 million Brahmas by the time the discourse came to an end.

32 wonderful and extraordinary great signs appeared when the Buddha was delivering a sermon, as well as during his conception, birth and becoming a Buddha.

When the venerable Koṇḍañña was thus established in the fruit of Stream Entry, the Buddha exclaimed with joy:

 

"Aññāsi vata bho Koṇḍañño! Aññāsi vata bho Koṇḍañño!"

("Ah! Koṇḍañña has really understood! Ah! Koṇḍañña has really understood!")

 

So the Devas and people of the 10,000 world systems could hear this. Because the Buddha made such a joyful exclamation beginning with the words "Koṇḍañña has understood (aññā)...", the venerable Koṇḍañña became known by the name "Aññā Koṇḍañña. - (Kondañña who has understood)"

Having thus become a Stream Enterer, the venerable Aññā Koṇḍañña requested initiation from the Buddha, saying:

"Exalted Buddha, may I become a novice (samanera) in your presence and then a fully ordained monk?"

After this, the Buddha extended his right hand and addressed him in a voice like the voice of Brahma:

"Ehi bhikkhu svākkhāto Dhammo, cara brahma-cariyaṁ sammā dukkhassa anta-kiriyāya"

("Come, monk! The Dhamma is well proclaimed. Practice the holy life for the complete cessation of suffering.")

 

The ordination of the Venerable Aññā Koṇḍañña as a monk was successfully completed as soon as the first of the three sentences spoken by the Buddha was finished.

 

When the Buddha called the venerable Koṇḍañña with the words, "Come, monk," Kondanni's former appearance disappeared and he was transformed into a monk with a shaved head, dressed in monk's robes. He was already equipped with all the monk's paraphernalia, each in its place: a single-layer robe (antaravāsako) covering the body, a double robe (saṅghāṭi) lying on his shoulder, and an alms bowl hanging on his left shoulder. His demeanor (iriyā-patha) was worthy of veneration and was like that of an elder of sixty years' standing, who was eighty years old; and his posture was one of paying homage to the Buddha, who was his preceptor (upajjhāya).

The paraphernalia received by ordained monks (ehi-bhikkhu) are known as paraphernalia created by supernormal powers (iddhi-mayā-parikkhāra). If a person had made a gift of the eight paraphernalia such as robes, bowl, etc., in the past to a noble person (ariya-puggala) such as a Stream Enterer, Once Returner, etc., or to an ordinary but virtuous monk, and had sincerely aspired, saying, "May this gift of paraphernalia become a supporting cause (paccaya) for becoming a ordained monk in the future," then the said gift, provided it had exceptional merit, could help that person to obtain paraphernalia created by supernormal powers in the presence of the Buddhas and become a ordained monk.

 

In total, there were 8 ways of the highest ordination to bhikkhus. Nowadays, only one of these ways exists - this is Ñatti-catuttha-kamma upasampadā - ordination by declaration and three proclamations.

The other methods existed only during the time of the Buddha, and one method ceased with the disappearance of the last Theravada nuns:

1. Ehi bhikkhu upasampadā
Some people came to the Tathagata, heard the Dhamma and, having gained faith, asked for initiation. To those of them who had the opportunity to become arhats in the same life and had past merit, the Tathagata granted the initiation “ehi bhikkhu upasampadā”. When the one awaiting initiation asked for initiation, the Tathagata extended his right hand and said: “Ehi bhikkhu, svākkhāto dhammo, caratha brahmacariyaṃ sammā dukkhassa antakiriyāya”. Then the worldly appearance of the one awaiting initiation disappeared, the hair on his head disappeared, and he was already with the bowl and robes obtained due to his past merit.

2. Saraṇāgamana Upasampadā

Upasampada by taking refuge in the triple gem. At the end of the first rainy season, the Tathagata sent sixty arhats for the purpose of spreading the Dhamma. The sons of householders in the places where the various theras were transmitting the Dhamma expressed their willingness to receive initiation after hearing the Dhamma. They were brought to the Tathagata and were granted initiation and the supreme initiation. To bring all those wishing to receive initiation to the Tathagata became a very difficult task for both those awaiting initiation and the monk-teachers. Therefore, the Tathagata granted permission to the monks to confer initiation and the supreme initiation. This was to be done by taking refuge in the triple gem given by the teacher and accepted by those awaiting initiation. The vinaya does not mention the first person to receive initiation and the supreme initiation by this method.

3. Ovāda pratigrahaṇa upasampadā

When the young man Piphalī visited the Tathāgata under the banyan tree named Bahuputtaka and expressed his status as a disciple, the Tathāgata gave the young man three pieces of advice. His supreme empowerment occurred simply by accepting these pieces of advice. Having received the empowerment, the young man Piphalī became known as Mahā Kassapa thera. Kassapa thera's supreme empowerment is called ovāda pratigrahaṇa (acceptance of advice) upasampadā, because it occurred by accepting the Tathāgata's advice. The Buddha gave Kassapa three pieces of advice about having a sense of shame and fear of committing an offense toward his fellow monks regardless of their status, paying close attention to the teachings that lead to the growth of virtue, and not neglecting mindfulness of the body.

4. Paсhabyākaraṇa upasampadā

One day, Sopāka Sāmaṇera was walking behind the Tathāgata in the Pubbārāma monastery. Then the Tathāgata turned to Sopāka Sāmaṇera and asked ten questions. Sopāka Sāmaṇera answered them all very well. Pleased with this, the Tathāgata proclaimed the supreme empowerment of Sopāka. He was only seven years old when he received the supreme empowerment. This is called paсhabyākaraṇa upasampadā because it is achieved by solving questions.

5. Garu dhamma paṭiggahaṇa upasampadā

When Pajāpatī Gotamī, the Tathāgata's stepmother/aunt, asked the Tathāgata for higher ordination, the Aṭṭha garu dhamma (eight basic rules for nuns) were established. The Buddha declared that accepting the Aṭṭha garu dhamma would itself lead to her higher ordination. When Ānanda Thera proclaimed the Aṭṭha garu dhamma, she happily accepted them, and as a result received her higher ordination.

6. Aṭṭhavācikā upasampadā

In the early stages when female monasticism began, the higher ordination of women, except for Pajāpatī Gotami who left home with her, was performed by monks. This was during the Catti catuttha kamma upasampadā. But later the Tathāgata included other factors in this method of higher ordination, and it was declared that in order for a woman to receive higher ordination, her higher ordination must be performed by Catti catuttha kamma among bhikkhunīs and then among bhikkhus. Since Catti catuttha kamma is repeated twice among bhikkhunīs and bhikkhus, the Kammavaca will be recited eight times. Thus the highest ordination of the bhikkhunīs becomes Aṭṭhavācikā upasampadā. The five hundred women who left home with Pajāpatī Gotami were awarded the supreme initiation of Catti catuttha kamma. In this way.

 

7. Dūtena upasampadā

At the time of the Buddha, a prostitute named Aḍḍhakāsī was ordained in this way. After she had received the highest ordination among the bhikkhunīs and was about to go to the bhikkhu to undergo the second part of the ceremony, it was reported that a group of men were waylaying her on the road to rape her. The Tathāgata then approved the sending of another bhikkhunī as an agent in place of Aḍḍhakāsī to perform the highest ordination of Aḍḍhakāsī to pronounce the required kamma vaca. Because this highest ordination is performed using an agent, it was called Dūtena (by means of a messenger) upasampadā.

 

And finally:

8. Catti catuttha kamma upasampadā - which is performed in our days

At the time of the Buddha, the responsibility of conferring the highest initiation of Rādha thera was taken over by the elder Sāriputta. He asked the Tathāgata how the highest initiation of Rādha thera should be performed. The Tathāgata then declared that the hitherto practiced saraṇāgamana upasampadā should be discontinued and catti catuttha kamma upasampadā should be practiced in the future for conferring the higher initiation. If this takes place in the middle part of India, it should be done with ten monks who have received the highest initiation; if the initiation takes place outside the middle part of India, it should be done with five monks. A person who has no faults that would prevent the highest initiation can be initiated into the highest initiation by making the correct pronunciation (kammavaca – proclamation). By correctly reciting the kammavaca among suitable people, in a properly constructed and limited sima, the person awaiting the highest initiation will establish the highest initiation, as it is written in the Vinaya.


Nowadays, the highest initiation of monks is performed by catti catuttha kamma upasampadā.

When a person awaiting the highest initiation arrives in Sima 1,
the first thing that is done is to give him the temporary name Naga, and the temporary name Tissa is given to his preceptor; this is done so that their names can be easily pronounced when reciting the kamavaca - a special formula for upasampada in Pali where maximum precision in pronunciation of Pali words is required and the legitimacy of the initiation may even depend on this pronunciation.

The monk conducting the upasampada (highest initiation) says it in Pali as follows. Here is the translation:

“To facilitate the pronunciation of the empowerment text, I remove the old name of the candidate for the highest empowerment and give him the name Naga, I remove the old name of the preceptor and give him the name Tissa. This should be known by the one who is awaiting the highest empowerment, the preceptor and the Sangha.”

After the assignment of names, the teacher should tell the candidate for higher initiation the following:

“Samanera! You who have come with the expectation of the supreme initiation for the purpose of crossing samsara and attaining Nibbana, should first accept a preceptor, as the Buddha expounded “Paṭhamaṃ upajjhaṃ gāhāpetabbo.”

When this is said, the person awaiting the highest initiation (upasampadāpekkha), sitting down before the preceptor, utters the words of acceptance of the preceptor.

After this, the teacher explains to the applicant.

“For your information I tell you what is used in the sasana to denote the cup and the garments, because the Blessed One, the guide of the world, said so.”

"Ayaṃ te patto",

"Samanera! This black spherical vessel is your cup."

"Ayaṃ saṅghāṭi",

“This garment, cut into pieces, sewn in two layers and dyed, is called saṅghāṭi in the Buddha’s sasana.”

"Ayaṃ uttarāsṅgoa"

“This garment, cut into pieces, stitched together in one layer and dyed, is called uttarāsṅgoa in Sasana.”

"Ayaṃ antaravāsako",

“This robe that you wear is called antaravāsako in Buddha-Sasana.”

"Gaccha amumhi okāse tiṭṭhāhi",

“Samanera! As the Tathagata declared:

“Anujāmi bhikkhave! Ekamantaṃ anusāsitvā saṅghamajjhe antarāyike dhamme pucchituṃ",

That a person awaiting the highest initiation should be interviewed in a place far from the Sangha, it is not appropriate to give you advice among the Sangha. Therefore, go to that place."

Thus, the one awaiting the highest initiation retires to a special place.

There the monk says to the samanera:

Suṇasi, nāga. Ayaṃ te saccakālo, bhūtakālo. Yaṃ jātaṃ taṃ saṅghamajjhe pucchante santaṃ atthī'ti vattabbaṃ, asantaṃ natthi'ti vattabbaṃ. Mā kho vitthāsi, mā kho maṅku ahosi.

Samanera named Naga, you who have sacrificed your worldly life to the Buddha Sasana for the purpose of crossing samsara. This moment when you are about to receive the highest initiation consisting of the highest morality in this extremely rare Buddha Sasana is the time to honestly express the truth by answering the questions asked, without hiding or trying to hide anything.

Then the aspirant is asked special questions.

After this, the one who performs the initiation leaves the person waiting for the highest initiation there and goes to the Sangha for approval of the highest initiation. In the meantime, the candidate changes into a civara.
Then, in front of the Sangha, the candidate is asked 14 more questions and if he answers them appropriately, he is ordained as a bhikkhu and no one has any objections.

Then, the Pali texts on the morality of bhikkhus and so on are read to him. The information about his initiation is read as follows:

Tāvadeva chāyāmetabbā. Uttuppamāṇaṃ ācikkhitabbaṃ divasabhāgo ācikkhitabbo. Saṅghīti ācikkhitabbā. Cattāro nissāya ācikkhitabbā. Cattāri akaraṇīyāni ācikkhitabbāni.

As our omniscient, all-compassionate, blessed Arahant, Sammasambuddha has instructed us to tell you this within the sima itself; the time, season and year when the Maha Sangha, having entered the right path, bestowed upon you the absolutely pure supreme empowerment and judged you by the four supports and the four forbidden things; I do so, please listen to them with appreciation. Venerable person!

On the day...... month.......... B.E. or...... day...... month...... year of our era;

The Sangha has bestowed upon you the absolutely pure supreme initiation and elevated you to the status of a bhikkhu from the realm of samanera. Remember this and at the cost of your life protect the morality of the supreme initiation, without violating even the slightest rules.

Piṇḍiyālopa bhojanaṃ nissāya pabbajjā. Tattha te yāvajīvaṃ ussāho karaṇīyo. Atirekalābho saṅghattaṃ, uddesabhattaṃ, nimaṇtanaṃ, salākabhattaṃ, pakkhikaṃ, uposathikaṃ, pāṭipādikaṃ.

This dedication is supported by food obtained by going from house to house. You should try to live on alms all your life. Alms offered to the whole sangha, alms offered to two or three members of the sangha, alms given privately, periodic alms, alms given once, alms given on the day of Poya, and alms given on the day after the full moon or new moon should be considered as additional benefits due to your past merits.

 

Paṃsakūla cīvaraṃ nissāya pabbajjā, tattha te yāvajīvaṃ ussāho karaṇīyo. Atireka lābho khomaṃ, kappāsikaṃ, koseyyaṃ, kambalaṃ, sānaṃ, bhaṅgaṃ.

Initiation is supported by clothes. You should try to make do with discarded clothes at the cost of your life. If you receive chivara made of goat hair, cotton, silk, wool, and mixed materials due to past merits; these should be used as additional benefits.

 

Rukkhamūlasenāsanaṃ nissāya pabbajjā, tattha te yāvajīvaṃ ussāho karaṇīyo. Atireka lābho vihāro, aḍḍhayogo, pāsādo, hammiyaṃ guhā.

Dedication is supported by sheltering at the roots of trees. You should try to live at the cost of your life at the roots of trees. If houses with two-sided roofs, one-sided roofs, four-sided roofs, caves are obtained as a result of past merits; they can be used.

 

Pūtimuttabhesajjaṃ nissāya pabbajjā, tattha te yāvajīvaṃ ussāho karaṇīyo. Atireka lābho sappi, navanītaṃ, telaṃ, madhu, phānītaṃ.

Dedication is supported by medicines prepared from gall nuts and urine of cattle. You should try to live on this medicine at the cost of your life. However, if you get ghee, butter, spicy butter, honey, jaggery due to past merits; they can be used as additional benefits.

 

This is how the initiation of Catti catuttha kamma upasampadā takes place.

 

Starting the Wheel of Dhamma

 

Before the appearance of the Buddha, there appeared in India some leaders of religious schools who called themselves ascetics (samaṇa). Some of them practiced and taught sensual indulgence as an ascetic's conduct, while others practiced and taught a self-torturing way of life. At a time when the world was enveloped in the darkness of these two extreme teachings of self-indulgence and self-torture, each claiming to be the true good practice, on the full moon sunset of the month of Vesākha in 528 BCE, the Buddha delivered the Discourse on Turning the Wheel of Dhamma (Dhamma-cakkappavattana-sutta, SN 56.11).

The Buddha began the discourse by saying, "Monks, these two extremes should not be followed by one who has renounced the world." And as soon as these words were spoken by the Buddha, by the power of the Buddha, they echoed throughout the 10,000 world system that constituted the Buddha's birth sphere, and filled the entire world, from the Avīci sphere below to the Bhavagga sphere above. By that time, the Brahmas, 180 million in number, who had ripened the roots of merit as a sufficient condition for realizing the four truths, had already assembled in the Deer Park, Isipatana, where the discourse was to be delivered. As this first discourse was being delivered by the Buddha, the sun was setting in the west and the moon was appearing on the eastern horizon.

 

The subject of the Discourse on Turning the Wheel of Dhamma is this: The Buddha exhorted the Group of Five Ascetics to avoid the two extremes of self-indulgence and self-torture, and pointed out to them the Middle Way, consisting of eight factors, as the correct course of practice. He then outlined the Four Truths. After this, he announced the characteristics that require three stages of knowledge regarding each of the Four Truths, and declared that he was a Buddha because he had fulfilled these requirements.

 

As the teaching continued, Koṇḍañña "stream-entered" and became the first Stream-Enter (Sotāpanna), a disciple established on the first path. Thus the Wheel of Dhamma was set in motion and the noble truth was established in the world. This great event was hailed by the earthly Devas, whose loud praises spread among the heavenly Devas and Brahmas. The great earth trembled. The wondrous light emanating from the Buddha, generated by his mind and arising from the element of temperature, was infinitely superior to the personal radiance of the greatest of the Devas or Brahmas, due to his omniscient wisdom.

 

What exactly is called the "Wheel of Dhamma"? The Wheel of Dhamma is a term referring to the two kinds of knowledge of the Buddha: the penetrating knowledge (paṭivedha-ñāṇa) of the four knowledges of the path (magga-ñāṇa) and the knowledge of exposition (desanā-ñāṇa).

The four knowledges of the path, consisting of the twelve aspects of the four truths that arose in the Bodhisattva at the moment of Perfect Awakening, are the Wheel of Dhamma; and the power of exposition of these same twelve aspects of the four truths that was expounded to the Group of Five is also the Wheel of Dhamma. They are called the Wheel of Dhamma or Righteousness because these two kinds of knowledge of the Buddha destroy all defilements, just as a powerful weapon destroys all enemies.

It was when the Venerable Koṇḍañña and the 180 million Brahmas attained the Fruit of Stream-Entry (Sotāpatti-phala) that the Wheel of Dhamma could be said to have been successfully set in motion and the kingdom of Dhamma established in the world. This was a particularly significant event because from the time of the disappearance of the teaching of the previous Buddha Kassapa until this time under the Buddha Gotama, no one had been able to set this Wheel of Dhamma in motion through these two special knowledges of the Buddha.

In regard to the insight into the four truths, the truth of cessation is realized or perceived through Nibbāna as the object of the mind. The other three truths are perceived in their respective functions. This means that the four truths are simultaneously revealed at the moment when knowledge of the path (magga-ñāṇa) dispels the confusion or ignorance that has obscured these four truths.

 

Characteristics of Impersonality (Anatta)

 

After the Buddha had brought Kondanna to the fruit of Stream-Entry (Sotāpatti-phala), he taught further spiritual development to the Group of Five Ascetics. From the first day of the waning moon of the month of Vesakha, he did not go out for alms, but engaged only in teaching. On the first and second days of the waning moon, the venerables Vappa and Bhaddiya attained the fruit of Stream-Entry, and both were ordained as monks by the Buddha himself in the manner: "Ehi bhikkhu" ("Come, monk").

The Buddha then allowed the Venerables Kondanna, Vappa and Bhaddiya to go on alms round, while he himself taught the Dhamma to the Venerables Mahananma and Assaji. The Buddha and his five disciples supported themselves on the alms collected by the three monks. Then on the third and fourth days of the waning moon in the month of Vesakha, the Venerables Mahananma and Assaji became established in Stream Entry, and both were ordained as monks in the manner of 'Ehi bhikkhu'.

After all five ascetics had established themselves in Stream-Entry, the Buddha decided on the fifth day of the waning moon to expound the teaching further in order to lead them to Awakening. And accordingly on that day he taught them the Sutta on the Characteristics of Impersonality (Anatta-lakkhaṇa-sutta, SN22.59).

The Buddha began the sutta by saying, "Monks, form is not the self," and explained this fact through the suffering (dukkha) characteristic of bodily form. He then asked five monks, "Is form permanent or impermanent?" The monks, after pondering the question, replied, "Form is impermanent, Venerable Sir."

Using the same method of question and answer, the Buddha revealed to his listeners the character of impermanence of the five aggregates (khandha), one after the other. In the same way, he revealed to his disciples the fact of suffering (dukkha) and impersonality (anatta). This method of dialogue, in which the disciple comes to his own conclusion about the three characteristics of the five aggregates, is technically called by the commentators the "threefold circle instruction" (teparivaṭṭa-dhamma-desanā). In this question, the Buddha seeks to establish the characteristic of impersonality (anatta), having previously established the characteristic of impermanence (anicca) and the characteristic of suffering (dukkha) of the five aggregates.

In some suttas, the Buddha expounded the impermanent nature of the five aggregates through the characteristic of impermanence. In some suttas, he expounded the characteristic of impersonality through the fact of the suffering nature (dukkha) of the five aggregates. The early part of this sutta is an example of this. In some suttas, he makes the characteristic of impersonality obvious after first establishing the fact of the impermanence and suffering nature of the aggregates. In this sutta, the latter part makes it clear. The Buddha uses this approach because the impermanent and suffering nature of things is more obvious to everyone, whereas the nature of impersonality is not so obvious.

To explain this further: When someone accidentally breaks an object, he or she may notice, "Ah, this is impermanent!" but will not say, "Ah, this is without essence or without self." When someone is tormented by pain or the prick of a thorn, one may say, "Ah, this is suffering," but will not say, "Ah, this is without self." Without self (anatta) is not mentioned in these cases because the nature of without self is somewhat removed from man's ordinary thinking. Therefore the Buddha teaches without self through impermanence, or through suffering, or through a combination of impermanence and suffering. This latter method is used in the final part, the threefold circle instruction (teparivaṭṭa-dhamma-desanā) of this sutta.

The Buddha further explains: “Therefore, monks, whatever bodily form there may be – past, future or present, internal or external, gross or subtle, low or sublime, distant or near – the whole bodily form should be seen as it actually is, with right insight and path-knowledge wisdom: ‘This is not mine,’ ‘This is not I,’ ‘This is not my self.’” Thus, the Buddha points out the falsity of the ego when one gains insight into the five aggregates and when one himself comes to this through the attainment of path-knowledge.

In insight meditation (vipassanā), if one concentrates on the impermanence (anicca) of phenomena, one can dispel the illusion of vanity. If one concentrates on suffering (dukkha), one can let go of craving. If one concentrates on essencelessness (anatta), one can dispel the illusion of wrong views.

Here, regarding the five aggregates as "This is not mine" leads to the destruction of craving (taṇhā) and is equivalent to focusing on suffering in insight meditation. Regarding the five aggregates as "This is not me" leads to the destruction of conceit (māna) and is equivalent to focusing on impermanence. Regarding the five aggregates as "This is not my self" leads to the destruction of wrong views (diṭṭhi) and is equivalent to focusing on impersonality.

 

In the conclusion of the sutta, the Buddha summarizes the result that a person with right view attains, namely the fruit of an Arahant. "Having attained this right view, the well-informed one becomes a noble disciple (ariya)." In the sequence of practices and events following right perception as described above, he attains sufficient insight into the five aggregates to attain path-knowledge and its fruition, as well as overseeing knowledge (paccavekkhaṇa-ñāṇa). The sutta then ends with a brief description of the attainment of Enlightenment by five monks.

From this sutta it is clear that the Group of Five Ascetics attained Awakening through meditation on the impermanence, suffering and impersonality of the five aggregates. Therefore, it would be beneficial for all followers of the Buddha to reflect on the following verses on the five aggregates:

 

The totality of corporeality is short-lived:

It appears and disappears in the blink of an eye.

It is sad, sad, painful to be subjected to

Arising and disappearing, continually again and again.

The corporeality is without essence, without anything really substantial.

To the discerning eye it is fickle by nature,

And therefore there is only suffering and non-self.

 

The aggregates of feeling, perception, volitional impulses and consciousness are also short-lived:

They appear and disappear in the blink of an eye.

It is sad, sad, painful to be subjected to

Arising and disappearing, continually again and again.

Consciousness is without essence, without anything really substantial.

To the discerning eye it is impermanent by nature,

And therefore there is only suffering and non-self.
