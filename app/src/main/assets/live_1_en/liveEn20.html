Homage to the Most Venerable One, the Arhat, the Self-Awakened One

 

Namo tassa bhagavato arahato sammāsambuddhassa

Homage to the Most Venerable One, the Arhat, the Self-Awakened One

 

 

Sabbapāpassa akaraṇaṃ,

Do no evil

Kusalassa upasampadā ;

Accumulate good things

Sacitta pariyo dapanaṃ,

Encourage your mind to comprehend

Etaṃ buddhāna sāsanaṃ

This is Buddha's teaching.

 

 

Buddha reflects on the Dhamma

 

Then, on Thursday, the sixth day of the waxing moon in the month of Asalha, 50 days after he became Buddha on Wednesday, the full moon of the month of Vesak, having spent 49 days (satta-sattāha), the Buddha rose from his seat under the Rajayatana tree and again went to the foot of the Ajapala banyan tree, where he remained sitting in a cross-legged posture.

After this, the Buddha, in solitary tranquility, reflected thus: “This aggregate of Dhamma, the Four Noble Truths, clearly comprehended by me through self-cognized knowledge (sayambhū-ñāṇa), is truly profound, like the body of water that supports the earth’s firmament. Truly, it is difficult to see, like a mustard seed hidden by the great mountain Meru. Truly, it is difficult to know, just as difficult as for the tip of one hair, divided into 100 strands, to touch the tip of another such divided hair. Truly, it is peaceful, truly noble. These two definitions refer to the supramundane Dhamma (Lokutara-Dhamma). It is not a Dhamma into which intellectuals can delve through mere applied reasoning (vitakka). It is a Dhamma to be resorted to and accepted through knowledge and wisdom. (ñāṇa-paññā) It is truly subtle, it is the Dhamma that is understood only by the wise who practice correctly.

All these living beings, however, take pleasure in two forms of attachment, namely, attachment to the five objects of sense pleasure (kāmālaya) and attachment to the enjoyment of the five sense objects (taṇhālaya). These living beings who take pleasure in these two forms of attachment are not actually able to recognize this doctrine of dependent origination (paṭicca-samuppāda), the relationship of cause and effect. It will be even more difficult for them to realize Nibbana, which is the extinction of all the sankharas of conditioned phenomena (saṅkhāra), the complete rejection of all the substrata of sensuality (kāma-upadhi), aggregates (khandha-upadhi), defilements (kilesa-upadhi) and formations (abhisaṅkhāra-upadhi), which is the drying up of the 108 kinds of craving (taṇhā); which is the exhaustion of the 1500 forms of defilements and passions (kilesa-rāga), and which is the cessation of all suffering. Also, if I were to teach the Dhamma of such depth, those devas and persons whose faculties (indriya) are immature, who are not yet fully developed for liberation, will not see or understand the Dhamma. Teaching the Dhamma to such devas and persons would only mean a waste of energy for me."

Moreover, two extremely wonderful verses, which had never been heard before, clearly appeared in the Buddha's stream of consciousness. (Ariyapariesana Sutta: The Noble Search MN 26):

 

Kicchena me adhigataṁ, halaṁ dāni pakāsituṁ,
What I have achieved with such difficulty, there is no point in proclaiming now.

rāga-dosa-paretehi, nāyaṁ Dhammo susambudho.

This Dhamma will not be easily understood by those who are overcome by passion and hatred.

Paṭisotagāmiṁ nipuṇaṁ, gambhīraṁ duddasaṁ aṇuṁ,

Going against the current of the World, the Dhamma, subtle, deep, difficult to see,

rāga-rattā na dakkhanti, tamokhandhena āvuṭā.

Those who are overcome by passion, shrouded in the darkness of ignorance, will not see.

This thought is, in fact, the natural law (Dhammata) that arises in all Buddhas. The Buddha, thinking in this way, was inclined not to make efforts to teach the Dhamma immediately for three reasons:

1) The minds of living beings were full of defilements;
2) The Dhamma was very deep and subtle to understand;
3) The Buddha treated the Dhamma with the greatest respect.

The Buddha's process of reflection can be compared to the thoughts of a doctor who, while treating a patient suffering from various diseases, reflects: "In what way and with what medicine should I treat this patient so that he will recover?" In the same way, the Buddha, realizing on the one hand that all living beings are afflicted with various ailments of defilements (kilesa), and on the other hand that the Dhamma is incredibly profound and difficult to understand, reflected: "What Dhamma should be taught to these beings and what method should be used to teach them."

Another explanation is that there are two reasons (nidāna) for the Buddhas to teach the Dhamma:

1) The feeling of compassion for living beings that arises in the mind-stream of the Buddhas, that is, great compassion (mahā-karuṇā), which is the internal (ajjhattika) cause;

2) The act of requesting the World-Honored Brahma for the Buddha to teach the Dhamma, that is, the request of Brahma (Brahma-yācana), which is the external (bāhira) cause.

At the time when the Buddha was thus reflecting on the depth of the Dhamma and the abundance of defilements (kilesa) in sentient beings, the Buddha's great compassion (mahā-karuṇā), the inner cause, was already there, but the outer cause was still missing, since Brahma had not yet made his request. The Buddha was inclined to teach the Dhamma only after Brahma had made his request, thereby fulfilling the outer cause.

Teaching the Dhamma only after Brahma's request was the natural law (Dhammata) for every Buddha. The reason why the teaching was given only after such a request from Brahma was as follows:

Outside the Buddha's teaching, before the Buddha appeared, those who were considered virtuous - whether laymen, wanderers, ascetics or Brahmins - worshipped and revered Brahma alone. If the Great Brahma, respected by the world, showed reverence to the Buddha by bowing before him, the entire world (including the infinite inhabitants of the divine realms) would follow his example and develop (saddha) faith in the Buddha. For this reason, it was customary for the Buddhas to teach the Dhamma only after Brahma's request. Thus, only when the external cause - Brahma's request - was fulfilled would the Buddha begin to teach the Dhamma.

 

The Request of the Great Brahma Sahampati

 

The great Brahma Sahampati was in a previous birth a noble elder named Sahaka during the time of Buddha Kassapa. In this capacity he attained the first jhana, and when he died without losing this absorption, he was reborn in the first plane of jhana existence and became Mahabrahma with a lifespan of 64 intermediate periods, which is equal to one incalculable kalpa. The Brahmas of the said plane of existence called him Brahma Sahampati.

 

When the Buddha's mind-stream was such that he was still inclined not to make efforts to teach the Dhamma, this thought occurred to Brahma Sahampati:

 

vata bho loko! Vinassati vata bho loko!

"Friends, the world will perish! O friends, the world will perish!"

 

For the Buddha, who is worthy of special veneration by Devas and people because of his complete and true penetration into the knowledge of all things in the world, is inclined not to make efforts to teach the Dhamma!

Then, as instantly as a person of great physical strength might straighten a bent arm or bend a straightened arm, Brahma Sahampati disappeared from the world of the Brahmas along with his 10,000 Mahabrahmas and appeared before the Buddha.

At that time, Brahma Sahampati, with his robe over his left shoulder and his right knee on the ground, sitting as befits a Brahma, paid homage to the Buddha with his palms folded and addressed him:

"O Bhagavan! May the Buddha be pleased to teach the Dhamma to all living beings - humans, Devas and Brahmas. Exalted One of Mercy, may the Buddha be pleased to teach the Dhamma to all living beings - humans, Devas and Brahmas. There are many beings who have only a little dust of defilements in their eyes. If these beings do not have the opportunity to hear the Dhamma of the Buddha, they will suffer great loss, not having attained the extraordinary Dhamma of the paths and fruits that they deserve. Venerable Buddha, there will be those who can comprehend the Dhamma taught by you."

 

Then, addressing the Buddha in prose, Brahma Sahampati also made a request in verse:

 

"Exalted Buddha, in the past, before your appearance, in the country of Magadha there existed an impure, false teaching, supported by six heretical teachers such as Purana Kassapa, who were tainted with the filth of defilements (kilesa)."

Please open the great gate to the deathless Nibbana, which has remained closed since the disappearance of the teaching of Buddha Kassapa. May all sentient beings hear the Dhamma of the four noble truths, realized by you, free from the dust of defilements.

"Noble and wise Buddha, possessor of the vision of wisdom, able to see in all directions! Just as a man with keen eyesight, standing on the top of a rocky hill, surveys all the people around him, so you, venerable sir, already freed from sorrows, look upon all living beings - humans, Devas and Brahmas - who have fallen into the abyss of suffering and are oppressed by birth, old age, illness, death and so on."

 

That Brahma came and made a request to teach the Dhamma at a time when the Buddha was reflecting on the depth of the Dhamma and the abundance of defilements in sentient beings, and was still inclined not to make efforts to teach the Dhamma is the natural law (Dhammata) for every Buddha.

 

Buddha surveys the world

 

When Brahma Sahampati thus asked to survey the world and teach the Dhamma, the two conditions for this - the external cause and the internal cause - were fulfilled; and so he surveyed the world of living beings with the eyes of the Buddha (Buddha-cakkhu): Knowledge of the hidden desires or inclinations of beings (āsayānusaya-ñāṇa) and knowledge of the maturity or immaturity of faculties (indriya-paro-pariyatta-ñāṇa 1).

 

Looking in this way, he clearly saw different types of beings, comparable to the four kinds of lotuses: there are blue lotuses, red lotuses, or white lotuses in a pond. Of these four kinds of lotuses, there are:

1) the kind of lotuses that arise in water, grow and develop in water, but remain submerged;
2) the kind of lotuses that arise in water, grow in water, and stand level with the surface of the water;
3) the kind of lotuses that arise in water, grow in water, and stand elevated above the water, without the water wetting them or sticking to them.

Of the three kinds of lotuses mentioned, the 3rd lotus, which stands raised above the water, will bloom on the same day; the 2nd lotus, which stands level with the surface of the water, will bloom on the next day; and the 1st lotus, which develops but remains submerged, will bloom on the third day.

 

Besides the three kinds of lotus, there is a fourth kind of lotus that will not emerge from the water or bloom at all; this kind of lotus is diseased and will eventually become food for fish and turtles. Like these four kinds of lotus, there are:

 

1. Creatures that have little or no dust pollution.

2. Creatures that have a lot of impurities.

3. Beings who have the five faculties - faith (saddhā), energy (viriya), mindfulness (sati), concentration (samādhi) and wisdom (paññā) - are sharp and mature.

4. Creatures who have these five abilities are dull and immature.

5. Beings whose dispositions, such as faith, energy, etc., are good or bad.

6. Beings who will easily understand the Dhamma, and beings who will not.

7. Beings who regard all worldly phenomena (such as the aggregates, all forms of defilements, unwholesome actions, volitional impulses, and actions that give rise to further existences) as a terrible group of dangers, like an enemy brandishing a sharp sword, ready to attack.

8. And beings that do not have such a vision. Or have a distorted opposite vision.

 

When the Buddha surveyed the world of beings with his Buddha eye, he saw four groups of individuals:

 

1) Individuals who understood the Dhamma of the Four Noble Truths even in its brief exposition (mātikā-uddesa) and grew like lotuses rising above the water that bloom on the same day as the sunrise (ugghāṭitaññū-puggala);

 

2) Individuals who could not blossom simply by hearing a summary of the Dhamma, but understood and blossomed only when the Four Noble Truths were taught and explained in detail (vitthāra-niddesa-pada-bhājanī), like lotuses at the surface of the water that blossom the next day (vipañcitaññū-puggala);

 

3) Individuals who could not blossom by hearing the Dhamma briefly or in detail at one time, but understood the Four Noble Truths and blossomed after a day, a month, or a year with the help of friends and by persistently making efforts to understand the Dhamma, like absorbed lotuses that blossom on the third day (neyya-puggala);

 

4) Individuals who do not realize the path and fruit, no matter how much they hear and practice the Dhamma in this life (pada-parama-puggala), although they try very hard, they gain the benefit of the predisposition (vāsanā) for the Dhamma in future existences in which they can blossom. They end their lives becoming food for the fish and turtles of defilements, like diseased lotuses that do not rise from the water and do not blossom, but end up becoming food for the fish and turtles.

 

Having seen these four types of individuals and thinking about the Dhamma that would benefit them, the Buddha developed the effort to teach the Dhamma. He then divided all beings into two categories: individuals fit for the highest truths and liberation, called capable people (bhabba-puggala), and individuals unfit for the highest truths and liberation, called incapable people (abhabba-puggala). Of these two categories, the incapable people were put aside and not considered, he took only the capable people into the field of his omniscience (sabbaññutā-ñāṇa) and divided them into six groups, saying: “Beings full of passion (rāga), so many. Beings full of hatred (dosa), so many. Beings full of delusion (moha), so many. Beings of thought (vitakka), so many. Beings full of faith (saddhā), so many. Beings full of wisdom (paññā), so many.” And then he expressed his intention, saying: “Indeed, I will teach the Dhamma!

 

After deep reflection, the Buddha gave his consent to Brahma Sahampati in verse (MN 26):

Apārutā tesaṁ amatassa dvārā,

ye sotavanto pamuñcantu saddhaṁ,

vihiṁsa-saññī paguṇaṁ na bhāsiṁ,

Dhammaṁ paṇītaṁ manujesu Brahme.

 

The gates that lead to Immortality are open,

Let those who can hear be filled with faith.

I abstained from teaching before,

O Brahma, this is the highest Dhamma among men.

 

What is meant here is that only those who have the proper skill of listening will be able to listen to the Dhamma taught by the Buddha. And only if they have faith in him will they make efforts to practice the Dhamma with faith, and only then will the gate of Nibbana be opened. If they do not have faith in the Buddha, even if they have good skill of listening, they will not have sufficient faith in the Dhamma either, will not make sufficient efforts to practice it, and in that case the gate of Nibbana will remain closed. Therefore, beings who have good hearing (suvaco) should have faith in the Buddha and listen to the Dhamma.

 

It may also be said that from the time of the disappearance (Sāsana - era) of Kassapa Buddha until the present time, the true Buddha and his true Dhamma could not be found, and therefore in the consciousness-stream of beings there was no true faith - that faith which is a jewel to be kept tightly clenched in the fist. Devotion to heretical teachers at that time was not true faith. It was only the arising of an unwholesome consciousness (akusala-cittuppāda) mistaking the false for the true (micchādhimokkha). Now that the true Buddha has appeared, let the devas and men with good ears unclench their fists and take out the precious bundles of faith which have been clenched in their fists, as it were, since the disappearance (Sāsana) of Kassapa Buddha. Then Brahma Sahampati became filled with joy and exclaimed:

 

"The Buddha has given me his word of consent to proclaim the Dhamma!"

 

Then, having bowed to the Buddha and walked around Him on the right side, he disappeared from that very place and reappeared in the world of Brahma.



Buddha goes to Deer Park

 

After Brahma Sahampatti had passed away, the Buddha reflected:

"To whom shall I first teach the Dhamma? Who will quickly comprehend the Dhamma taught by me?"

 

Then it occurred to Him:

“The chief of the ascetics, Alara of the Kalama clan, is one who is endowed with the three kinds of wisdom (paññā):


1. wisdom about the birth of beings (jāti-paññā)
2. wisdom about meditation (bhāvanā-paññā)
3. wisdom about preservation (pārihārika-paññā)


He is also a person whose wisdom eye (paññā-cakkhu) has long been free from the dust of defilements—apparajakkha-puggala. It would be good if I taught the Dhamma to him first. He will quickly understand the Teaching."

 

At that moment, one of the devas, without making himself visible, addressed the Buddha:

“O Bhagavan, it is now seven days since Alara of the Kalama clan died.”

 

However, the Buddha, not accepting the deva's words without confirmation, with the help of omniscience (sabbaññutā-ñāṇa) himself saw and verified that Alara had indeed died seven days ago, and had been reborn in the realm of nothingness (ākiñcaññāyatana), the third of the four immaterial levels of existence (arūpa-loka).

 

After reflecting: “Indeed, it was a great loss for Alara of the Kalama clan that he missed the opportunity to attain the path and fruit of which he was worthy; if he had been able to hear the Dhamma taught by me, he would quickly have realized the Four Noble Truths,”


The Buddha again continued to think: “To whom shall I now teach the Dhamma? Who will be able to understand it quickly?” Then he thought: “The teacher of the ascetics Udakka, the son of Rama, is also endowed, like Alara, with the three kinds of wisdom. He is also one who has little dust in his eyes; his eye of wisdom has long been free from the dust of defilements. It would be good if I taught the Dhamma to him first – he will quickly understand the Teaching.”

 

At that moment another deva, without revealing himself, addressed the Buddha:

 "The Venerable Buddha, Teacher Udakka, son of Rama, passed away yesterday around midnight."

However, the Buddha, not relying solely on the word of the deva, verified this by means of his omniscience and confirmed that Udakka had indeed died the previous night at midnight and that he had since been reborn in the Realm of Neither-Perception-Non-Perception (Nevasaññā-nāsaññāyatana), the fourth of the four immaterial realms (arūpa-loka), also called Bhavagga.

 

And then, thinking: "Indeed, great was the loss of the ascetic teacher Uddaka, the son of Rama, that he missed the opportunity to attain the paths and fruits of which he was worthy. If he could have heard the Dhamma taught by me, he would quickly have realized the Four Noble Truths."

 

He again continued to reflect: “To whom should I first teach the Dhamma? Who will quickly understand it?” Then the Buddha thought: “The group of five wandering ascetics (pañca-vaggiya) have been of great help to me. They stayed with me and looked after me when I was practising arduous austerities (dukkara-cariyā) for six years in the Uruvela forest. Therefore, it would be good if I first taught the Dhamma to this group of five.”

 

Thinking, “Where are these five ascetics now living?” he saw them, through the super-knowledge of divine vision (dibba-cakkhu-abhiññā), living in the great Deer Park, also called Isipatana, near the city of Varanasi.


Thinking, “I will go to the Deer Park and recite the Dhamma-cakka,” he performed his alms rounds near the Bodhimaṇḍala and remained there until the fourteenth day of the waxing moon in the month of Āsāḷha. Then, on the full moon day of that month, early in the morning, having collected and taken with him his robes and alms bowl, he thought, “I will go to the city of Varanasi,” and began a journey of eighteen yojanas on foot.

 

The distance between Mahā Bodhi and Gaya (Buddhagaya) was three miles. The distance between Mahā Bodhi and Varanasi was eighteen yojanas. The Buddhas of the past went to the Deer Park to preach the Wheel of Dhamma, by means of their meditative absorptions (jhāna). As for our Buddha, he walked these eighteen yojanas, for he foresaw that a hermit named Upaka would soon attain the state of non-returner (Anāgāmi) due to his previous merits.

 

He knew the following: “Upaka is now walking along the same road. He will meet me, converse with me, and continue on his way. Later, tired of the World, he will return to me to listen to the Dhamma and become a noble non-returner (Anāgāmi) in this life, and then attain the state of Arhat in his next life, being reborn in Avihā, the twelfth realm of the Brahmā world.”

 

Buddha meets the hermit Upaka

 

When the Buddha was walking from Mahā Bodhi towards Varanasi, a hermit named Upaka, who was travelling between Mahā Bodhi and Varanasi, approached him. Seeing the Buddha, he asked:

 

- Friend, your eyes, ears, nose, tongue, body and mind are clear and pure. The color of your face is bright and clear. Friend, to whom have you gone as a hermit? Who is your teacher? Whose teaching is to your heart?

 

And then the Blessed One answered him in verse:

 

The Blessed One said:

 

Sabbābhibhū sabba-vidūham-asmi,

I have conquered everything, I know everything,

 

I'm sorry,

In all dhammas, undefiled,

 

sabbañjaho taṇhākkhaye vimutto,

Having abandoned everything, I became liberated with the cessation of taṇhā,

 

sayaṁ abhiññāya kamuddiseyyaṁ.

Having learned everything myself, whom should I point to as a teacher?

 

Na me ācariyo atthi, sadiso me na vijjati,

I have no teacher, no one equal to me.

 

sadevakasmiṁ lokasmiṁ, natthi me paṭipuggalo.

In the whole world, among the devas and people, there is no equal for me.

 

Ahañ-hi Arahā loke, ahaṁ Satthā anuttaro,

I am an Arhat in this world, I am a Teacher without equal,

 

ekomhi Sammā-sambuddho, sīti-bhūtosmi nibbuto.

I am the only Samma-sambuddha, extinguished, having attained Nibbana.

 

Dhamma-cakkaṁ pavattetuṁ, gacchāmi Kāsinaṁ puraṁ,

To proclaim the Wheel of Dhamma, I go to the city of Kasi,

 

andhī-bhūtasmiṁ lokasmiṁ, āhañchaṁ amata-dundubhiṁ.

In this blinded world I will beat the immortal drum.

 

To this the hermit Upaka said:

— Friend, if all this is so, then you must be possessed of infinite knowledge (ananta-ñāṇa) and have conquered Mara in his five forms.

 

The Blessed One replied:

 

Mādisā ve Jinā honti, ye pattā āsavakkhayaṁ,

Such are called Jina, who have achieved the destruction of the Asavas.

 

jitā me pāpakā dhammā, tasmāham-Upaka jino.

I have conquered the unwholesome dhammas, therefore, Upaka, I am Jina.

 

Then the hermit Upaka said:

"Friend, perhaps everything you say is true!"

nodded his head and went another way - to the Vaṅkahāra region.

 

The opportunity to converse and exchange words with the Buddha became for him an auspicious cause (upanissaya) for his subsequent renunciation of worldly life.
Yes, indeed! Upaka had a special merit (adhikāra) different from the ordinary.

It was for this reason that the Buddha walked all the way to meet him.

 

Those who heard the Dhamma from the Buddha before he proclaimed the Discourse of the Wheel of Dhamma (Dhamma-cakka) could not yet attain the paths and fruits (magga-phala). They only developed the inclination (vāsanā) for subsequent penetration into the Dhamma.

 

This is the natural law (dhammatā).

And so Upaka, although he listened to such an exalted Dhamma, revealing the qualities of the Buddha, did not attain either the path or the fruit. He merely acquired a wholesome disposition (kusala-vāsanā), which led him in the future to take monastic vāsanā and further attainments.

 

A Brief History of Upaka

 

Jumping ahead a little, we will tell the story of the ascetic Upaka.

An ascetic named Upaka lived in a small hut in a hunter's village in the Vaṅkahāra region. He was highly respected and was under the care of the chief hunter of that village. Since there were many mosquitoes in that area, Upaka spent his time inside a large clay vessel-like room. When the hunter was about to leave for a remote deer forest, he gave his daughter named Chapa this instruction:

 — “Daughter, take care of and diligently serve our venerable ascetic, who is an arahant. Do not neglect this!”

Having said this, he went into the forest, accompanied by his sons and younger brothers.

 

Chapa, the daughter of the chief hunter, was of a pleasant appearance, with beautiful features. The day after his father's departure, the ascetic Upaka came to the house of the chief hunter. When Chapa came to serve him the food she had prepared herself, he, seeing her up close, was overcome with passion. Therefore, even without being able to eat, he returned to his home, carrying the food in a vessel.

Having placed the vessel with food in a suitable place, he lay down without touching the food, thinking:

 — “I can only live if I live with Chapa! If not, I will die!”

On the seventh day, when the chief hunter returned home, he asked Chapa about the ascetic Upaka.

When Chapa answered:

 - “Father, your ascetic Upaka came to the house only once and never showed up again,”

The hunter, without even changing his clothes, in the same clothes he had on from the forest, immediately went to the ascetic Upaka. Approaching him, he began to feel and knead his legs, asking:

 — “What’s the matter with you, venerable sir?”

Ascetic Upaka, without answering, continued to lie, rolling from side to side, and groaning. When the hunter began to persistently ask:

 — "Tell me, venerable sir. I will do everything I can."

Ascetic Upaka replied:

 — “If there is no way to live next to your daughter Chapa, then it is better for me to die.”

 

When the hunter asked:

 — “Venerable sir, do you have any skill?”

Ascetic Upaka replied:

 — “I don’t have any.”

 

Then the hunter noticed:

 - "Venerable sir, if a person is not knowledgeable in anything, he will not be able to manage household affairs."

 

Ascetic Upaka answered again:

 - "I know nothing. However, I will be able to carry the carcasses of the animals you hunt. I will also be able to sell the meat."

 

The hunter said:

 - "We also like the idea of you carrying and selling meat."

 

He then gave him an upper garment and dressed him in worldly attire. Taking him home, he gave his daughter Chapa to him in marriage. From their marriage a son was born, who was named Subbhuda. When the child began to cry, Chapa would rebuke and mock Upaka, singing a lullaby to hurt and humiliate him:

 

The son of a meat merchant, a former monk and ascetic!

You are the son of a fool,

a hanger-on in our house who fell in love with me!

Mom is trying to persuade you to fall asleep - don't cry!

I wish you sleep.

 

In a cradle decorated with emeralds,

Sleep, my son, as if made of pure gold.

Mom will sing and rock you to sleep.

Stop crying!

I wish you sleep.

 

Your father is the last in a line of men!

Avoid his path;

in the future strive for liberation (vimutti).

Mom is instructing you,

my beautiful son, my wreath of gold!

 

Then Upaka said:

- Wife, do you really think that I have nowhere to go? I have a very good friend named Ananta Jina. I will go to this good friend.

 

Chapa, realizing:

“This Upaka suffers unbearably from my ridicule and humiliation,”

 

Still, she continued to sing her lullaby over and over again.

 

One day Upaka went to the Middle Land (Majjhima-desa) without saying anything to Chapa. At that time the Blessed One was staying at Jetavana Monastery in Savatthi; and he had previously said to the monks:

— Monks, if anyone comes and asks about Ananta Jina, point me out to him.

 

Upaka asked everyone he met along the way:

— Where does Ananta Jina reside?

 

And in due course he reached Savatthi and, standing in the centre of Jetavana Monastery, asked the monks:

— Honorable sirs! Where is Ananta Jina?

 

The monks brought him to the Blessed One. Upon seeing the Buddha, Upaka immediately addressed him:

— Venerable Buddha! Do you remember and recognize me, your disciple?

 

When Buddha said:

- Yes, Upaka, I remember. Where do you live now?

 

Upaka replied:

— Venerable sir! I live in Vaṅkahāra.

 

Then Buddha asked him:

- Upaka, you are already at an advanced age. Will you be able to join the order of hermits?

 

Upaka replied:

- Yes, Venerable One, I can.

 

Then the Buddha permitted him to take the highest initiation and instructed him in the proper way of meditation. Upaka practised this meditation with great diligence and attained the fruition of the Non-Returner (Anāgāmī-phala). After his death he was reborn in the Brahma-loka, in the abode of Aviha, which is the lowest of the five realms of the world of Pure Abodes (Suddhāvāsa); and soon after that he attained the state of an arhat.

 

After Upaka left Cāpa, she lost interest in the world. So, leaving her young son Subhadda in the care of his grandfather, she set out on the same path as Upaka. Arriving at Savatthi, she took ordination in the presence of other nuns. There she practised diligently and developed insight, and, having traversed the four paths in succession, she attained arahantship and became known as the Venerable Cāpa (Bhikkhunī Cāpā), the destroyer of defilements.
