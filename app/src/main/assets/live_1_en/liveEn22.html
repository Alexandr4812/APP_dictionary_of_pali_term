The Story of Satagiri and Hemavata
and the Hemavata Sutta

 

The Buddha delivered the Wheel of Dhamma discourse shortly before sunset on the full moon day of the month of July (Āsāḷha) in 528 BCE. At midnight he delivered the Hemavata Sutta, sometimes called the Sātāgiri Sutta (Snp 1.9).

 

The detailed circumstances of the teaching of this sutta were as follows:

 
In the Good Kappa, when the human lifespan reached 20,000 years, Buddha Kassapa appeared and attained Parinibbana after living 16,000 years, which was four-fifths of the lifespan of that time. The cremation of his remains was performed with great reverence. The Buddha's bodily relics did not fall apart but remained as one solid golden mass. This was the natural law (dhammatā) for all Buddhas who live during the period when human lifespans reach such a long time.

As for short-lived Buddhas, they attain Parinibbana before many people have had the chance to see them. Therefore, out of compassion, wishing, "May many people living in different towns and villages gain merit by honoring the relics even after I attain Parinibbana," they resolve, "May my relics fall apart during cremation." Because of this, the relics of short-lived Buddhas, like the relics of our Buddha, fell apart into pieces that could then be used as relics.

The people built a large stupa one league high and the same in circumference, and placed in it the only relic of the Buddha Kassapa. Each side in one direction was one mile long, and on each side there was a large entrance gate, so that the distance between the gates was one mile.

King Kiki of Varanasi donated one gate; his son, Prince Pathavindhara, donated another; the officials, headed by an army general, donated a third; and the public, headed by a wealthy official, donated the last. The bricks used to build the great stupa were made of precious metals and gems. A special mixture of realgar and special oil was used as cement in the construction of the stupa. After the great stupa was built, two friends from noble families renounced the worldly life and took monastic ordination in the presence of senior disciples who had followed the Buddha during his lifetime.

It is especially noteworthy that in the Teaching (Sasana) of the long-lived Buddhas, only such senior disciples were competent to ordain monks and also to give them instructions. Those who became disciples only after the Buddha's Parinibbana were not qualified to perform such tasks.

This is different from the situation with short-lived Buddhas, like our Buddha Gotama, where the lineage continues after Parinibbana through fully ordained monks. In the case of Buddha Kassapa, who was a long-lived Buddha, only those who personally met him or his direct disciples during his lifetime could perform ordinations.

 

Then these two monk friends from noble families asked the senior disciples: "Venerable sirs, what are the tasks of monks that must be accomplished in the Buddha's Teaching?"

 

To this the senior disciples answered them:

 

"Monks, there are two duties for monks in the Buddha's Teaching:

1) Practice of insight meditation (vāsa-dhura)

2) Study or teaching of scriptures (pariyatti-dhura)

 

Of these two, the monk is from a noble family:

1. Spends five years with his mentors, taking care of their needs

2. Studies and masters the Monastic Rules (Pātimokkha)

3. Studies two or three sections (bhāṇavāra) of the suttas

4. Undergoes proper training in insight meditation (vipassanā)

5. Cuts off attachment to his fellow monks and lay followers.

6. After this, having retired to a large forest, far from people, he practices meditation for the realization of Awakening.

 

This is the monastic duty of practicing insight meditation (vāsa-dhura).

 

Or he must, according to his abilities:

1. To learn and become skilled in one collection (nikāya) of baskets (piṭaka)

2. Or in two, three, four or five collections of baskets

3. To strive to develop correct and pure teaching (pariyatti-sāsana) both in letter and in spirit.

It is the duty of a monk to study or teach (pariyatti-dhura).

 

Having said, "Of the two duties that monks should perform, the practice of insight meditation (vāsa-dhura) is the highest and more meritorious," the two monk-friends nevertheless thought, "We are still young. We should perform the practice of insight meditation only when we become older. Before we become old, we will practice to fulfill and complete the duty of studying and teaching the scriptures (pariyatti-dhura)."

 

Being naturally intelligent, they:

In a short time, we mastered all three baskets (piṭaka),

Became very skilled in making decisions on Vinaya matters,

Due to their knowledge of the scriptures, the two monk friends became very famous,

They always had a large entourage and many gifts and offerings,

Each of them had 500 monk followers,

The elders continued to give instructions (ovāda) to the four assemblies of people who came to them.

 

As a result, the Teaching flourished and shone, as if the Buddha had appeared again.

 

Dhamma and non-Dhamma

 

At that time, in a monastery near a village, there lived two monks: one who taught the Dhamma and the other who taught the opposite Dhamma (adhamma-vādī). Of the two, the monk who taught the opposite Dhamma was rude and harsh in speech. Once, when the monk who taught the Dhamma clearly learned of the other monk’s violation of a certain disciplinary rule, he rebuked the latter thus: “Friend, your behavior is unworthy of the Dhamma!” To this, the monk who taught the opposite Dhamma, in order to interrupt the original course of conversation, retorted, saying: “Friend, what do you see in me? What do you hear about me? Do not make hasty accusations!” The monk who taught the Dhamma replied: “Friend, the noble elders who are the guardians of the Vinaya know better.”

A monk who taught the opposite Dhamma thought, "If the elders, the keepers of the Vinaya, decide this matter according to the Vinaya, I will definitely not have any support for the Teaching," and he immediately went to the two elders, the keepers of the Vinaya. He approached them with certain offerings as gifts in order to deceive them and win them over to his side. He bowed respectfully and presented what he had brought to them and tried to obtain their guidance. He pretended to have due respect for them and to wish to be near them for service.

Once a monk who taught contrary to the Dhamma came to the assembly place of the elders, the guardians of the Vinaya, and after paying respects he continued to stand stubbornly, even when the elders asked him to leave. The elders asked him, "Friend, do you have anything special to tell us?" and he replied, "Yes, venerable sirs, I do. I have had a dispute with another monk about breaking the precept. If he were to come to you and report this matter, please do not decide according to what is considered appropriate." When the elders replied, "In a matter that has been presented to the Sangha, it is wrong and inappropriate not to decide according to what is considered correct," he begged them, saying, "Venerable sirs, if such a decision is made, I will have no support in the Doctrine. Let this offence remain mine. I will bear its consequences in Samsara. Just do not decide this matter, please!"

Being persistent and persistent, he finally got his way, the guardians of the Vinaya relented and said, "Okay, monk!" Having received the consent of the elders, the monk who taught the opposite Dhamma returned to the village monastery; and thinking, "I have done everything I wanted to do with the elders, the guardians of the Vinaya," he became even more overbearing, contemptuous, rude and unyielding in his treatment of the monk who taught the Dhamma.

The monk who taught the Dhamma, thinking, "This monk who teaches the opposite Dhamma is truly not afraid of anything!" immediately left the monastery and went to the monks who were followers of the elders, the guardians of the Vinaya, and addressed them:

"Brothers, should not a case coming to the Sangha be decided according to the Vinaya? Or, without allowing the case to come to the Sangha, should not the accusing monk and the accused monk admit their own mistakes and settle their dispute peacefully? But now these elders, the guardians of the Vinaya, are neither deciding the case themselves, nor are they allowing us to settle it peacefully through compromise. What does this mean?"

Hearing the words of the monk who taught the Dhamma and thinking, “The elders, the keepers of the Vinaya, must already know something wrong,” the thousand monks who were disciples of the elders, the keepers of the Vinaya, made no reply and remained silent.

The monk who taught the opposite of the Dhamma later took advantage of this and one day said in a suppressive tone:

"Friend, you said earlier that the elders, the keepers of the Vinaya, would know. Well, now you had better report this matter to them."

 Then he left, saying rudely:

"From now on you are completely destroyed! Do not return to the village monastery where we live!"

After this, the monk who taught the Dhamma went to the elders, the keepers of the Vinaya, and loudly lamented:

"Venerable sirs, thinking, 'that this monk who teaches something contrary to the Dhamma is taking care of our needs and pleasing us,' you have no respect for the Buddha's Teaching, but only respect for an individual; you do not protect the Teaching, but only protect a shameless immoral person. Sirs, from today on you should not decide any matters pertaining to the Vinaya." Then he left the elders and lamented:

"The Teaching (Sāsana) of Buddha Kassapa has indeed been defiled!"

After this, the two elders, the keepers of the Vinaya, were deeply moved and felt remorse (kukkucca), saying:

"By showing respect and giving protection only to this shameless immoral person, we have thrown the solid jewel of the Teaching (Sāsana) into the abyss of deep waters."

Wounded and with a depressed mind full of regret (kukkucca), they were not reborn in the higher realms after their death.

Of the two elders, one was reborn as a Yaksha named Hemavata on Mount Hemavata in Himavanta, and the other was also reborn as a Yaksha named Satagiri on Mount Sata in the Middle Country (Majjhima-desa). The thousand monks who followed these two elders were also not reborn in the higher heavenly abodes. Because they supported the elders, they were reborn as followers, 500 each of the two Yakshas. However, those who supported the four necessities of the elders, the keepers of the Vinaya, were reborn in the higher planes of existence of the Devas.

Both Hemavata and Satagiri were Yakshas of great power and fame and were included in the list of 28 generals of the Devas. It was the custom of the Devas to hold meetings to decide on legal cases eight times a month in a pavilion called Nagavati, on the flat surface of a realgar rock in the Himavanta forests. These two Yakshas used to participate in the meetings.

Satagiri and Hemavata, seeing each other at the said meetings of the Devas and remembering their past lives in the human world, asked each other about the place of their present existence:

"Friend, in what place were you reborn? And you, friend, what is your place of rebirth?"

And they were struck with great melancholy, telling of their fate:

"Friend, we have truly been irreparably destroyed! Even though we practiced the Dhamma for the entire period of 20,000 years during the Teaching of Buddha Kassapa in the past, we were reborn as Yakshas because of our shameless, immoral friend. However, some of our followers who uphold our four necessities were reborn in the higher realms of sensual pleasures."

 

Mutual Promises

 

After this, Satagiri said to Hemavata:

"My friend Hemavata, it is said that Himavanta, where you live, is a wonderful and unusual place. Therefore, if you see or hear anything strange and unusual, please come and inform me."

Hemavata also said to Satagiri:

"My friend Satagiri, the Middle Country where you live is a region where noble people appear and live, and it is said to be a wonderful and unusual country. If you see or hear anything strange and unusual, please come and tell me."

Thus the two friends, Satagiri and Hemavata, made a mutual promise to each other and continued to live without being able to get rid of their existence as yakshas; during this time even one immeasurable period passed between the appearance of one Buddha and the other (Buddhantara-kappa). During this period the great earth also increased by one league 1and three miles.

At that time, our Bodhisatta practiced and developed the ten perfections (pāramī) for the entire period of four immeasurable periods and 100,000 kalpas, from the time he received the definite prophecy from the Buddha Dīpaṅkara that he would become a Buddha until the time of his life as King Vessantara. He then took rebirth in the Tushita realm and lived there the full span of a Deva's life. At the request of the Devas who had come from all the 10,000 worlds, after conducting the five great examinations, he gave them his consent to become a Buddha. He then accepted conception in the womb of Queen Maha Maya in this human world, causing the 10,000 worlds to tremble while the 32 great signs were manifested.

These two friends, Satagiri and Hemavata, knew about the appearance of 32 great signs at the time of the Bodhisatta's conception, but it so happened that they did not pay attention to them. They did not reflect, "These great signs appear because the Bodhisatta was conceived." The 32 great signs also appeared clearly at the Bodhisatta's birth, at his renunciation of the world, and when he became a Buddha. Although they knew about the appearance of these great signs, they did not reflect or realize, "These great signs appear because of these events."

When the Buddha called the Five Monks and taught the Discourse on the Wheel of Dhamma (Dhamma-chakka), which has three phases and twelve aspects, there was a distinctly strong earthquake, and also the wonderful and unusual 32 great signs appeared. This was first noticed by Satagiri of the two Yakshas, and learning that the Buddha was then teaching the Discourse on the Wheel of Dhamma, the root cause of signs, he went to the Buddha along with his retinue of 500 Yakshas and listened to the teaching of the Wheel of Dhamma. But he could not attain any significant path and fruit.

The reason was that Satagiri, while listening to the discourse of the Wheel of Dhamma, remembered his friend Hemavata and looked around the assembly, thinking:

"Did my friend Hemavata come to this Dhamma meeting? Or not?"

Not finding Hemavata, his mind became scattered:

"How could my friend Hemavata be so late! He may not have time to listen to the Buddha's teaching on the Wheel of Dhamma, which is so wonderful and magnificent both in letter and in spirit!"

It is for this reason that he was unable to realize any important path and fruit.

The sun was already setting, but the Buddha continued preaching the Wheel of Dhamma.

Then, after thinking:

"I will go and bring my friend Hemavata to hear the Wheel of Dhamma preaching."

Satagiri created vehicles and travelled through the air in the direction where Hemavata and his retinue of 500 yakshas were.

The two yakshas met each other in the sky above the city of Rajagaha. When Satagiri asked the reason for his visit, Hemavata said:

"My friend Satagiri, ever since I became a yaksha in the Himavanta forests, this great mountain Hemavata has never looked so delightful, with trees blooming out of season. Therefore, I have come to visit you, intending to enjoy these wonderful flowers of the Himavanta forests with you."

When Satagiri asked Hemavata:

"My friend Hemavata, do you know why these flowers bloom out of season and in such a wonderful way?"

He replied:

"I don't know, my friend Satagiri."

Then Satagiri said to Hemavata:

"My friend Hemavata, this wonderful and extraordinary phenomenon has not only happened in this Himavanta. In fact, the same thing has happened everywhere in the 10,000 world system. My friend Hemavata, the Buddha has appeared in the three worlds of humans, devas and brahmas. The Buddha is now teaching the Wheel of Dhamma in a park called Isipatana near the city of Varanasi. Because the Buddha is teaching the Wheel of Dhamma, 32 wonderful and extraordinary great signs have clearly manifested throughout the world."

 

How Kali Became the First Stream-Entering Lay Woman

 

At that time, like a divine maiden enjoying divine luxury, a woman named Kali, who was pregnant and resided in the city of Rajagaha, where she had returned to give birth to a child in her parents' house, went up to the upper terrace of her parents' mansion and, opening the window decorated with lions, stood and breathed the fresh air to relieve the pains of her approaching birth.

 

Kali was originally from the city of Rajagaha. On reaching adulthood, she was married at Kuraraghara in Avanti. When the time came for her delivery, she returned to her parents' house. As she climbed up to the terrace of the mansion and tried to ease her pain from the approaching birth with some fresh air, she heard two yakshas speaking about the virtues of the Buddha.

 

Having heard the whole conversation between the two Yakshas about the virtues of the Buddha, Kali listened attentively to them and thought:

"Truly, Buddhas are endowed with such amazing and extraordinary qualities!"

At this thought she became filled with joy and rapture. Standing in that very place in a state of joy and having removed the hindrances through that joy, she attained insight knowledge and thereby attained the fruit of stream entry (Sotāpatti).

Kali, being the first stream-enterer (Sotāpanna) and noble disciple (ariya-sāvika) among women, was, one might say, the eldest sister of the entire female race. That same night she gave birth to a son, who later became the venerable Sona Kutikanna.

After staying at her parents' house for as long as she wanted, she returned to her home in Kuraraghara.

 

Thus, without even meeting or seeing the Buddha, but simply hearing about him and fixing her mind on the great qualities of the Buddha, she gained absolute faith in the Buddha's qualities and became established in the fruit of stream entry (Sotāpatti-phala), just as one effortlessly accepts food that has already been prepared and served. That is why later, when the Buddha, sitting among the Sangha, held a meeting to confer titles on outstanding lay women, he declared, "Dear monks, Kali of Kuraraghara is the most noble and excellent of all lay women (upāsikā) who have gained absolute faith in the Three Jewels simply by hearing!" and appointed her first among those who have gained faith by hearing (anussava-pasāda).

 

 



Conversation about Hemavat

Praise to the Buddha :
(Snp 1.9, Hemavata-sutta)

 

Ajja pannaraso uposatho, dibbā ratti upaṭṭhitā,

anomanāmaṁ Satthāraṁ, handa passāma Gotamaṁ.

 

"My friend Hemavata, today is indeed the full moon day of Uposatha on the fifteenth lunar day! Today is a very pleasant night when the whole of Jambudipa appears as if splendidly adorned with the bodily light of the Devas and Brahmas who have come from 10,000 worlds to hear the discourse, for this is the day when the Buddha teaches the Dhamma-cakka Sutta, all illuminated by the radiance of their robes and heavenly abodes, by the light of the moon, stars and lunar constellations, and by the bodily light of the Visuddha Deva and the Buddha himself. My friend Hemavata, do not be troubled by doubt whether this is the Buddha or not. Come! Let us go right now and pay homage to the Buddha Gotama, who is endowed with undiminished qualities, possesses such special epithets as Buddha, Blessed One and others, and who is the teacher of men, Devas and Brahmas."

Hearing the words of Satagiri, Hemavata thought and reasoned thus:

"This Satagiri boldly asserts that the person he has met and seen is a genuine omniscient Buddha, saying, 'Let us go right now and pay homage to Gotama Buddha, who is endowed with undiminished qualities, possessing such special epithets as Buddha, Blessed One, and so on.' Omniscient Buddhas are, in fact, rare and difficult to obtain in the world. Persons like Purana Kassapa and others, who claimed to be omniscient Buddhas, have ruined many people by imparting false knowledge to them. If the monk Gotama, whom Satagiri saw, is a genuine omniscient Buddha, he must be truly endowed with the quality of equanimity and unwaveringness in the face of favorable and unfavorable conditions of the world. Therefore, I will first inquire whether he possesses the steadfastness that only Buddhas possess."

And wishing to ask about the signs of steadfastness (tādi-lakkhaṇa), he recited this verse:

 

Kacci mano supaṇihito, sabba-bhūtesu tādino,

kacci iṭṭhe aniṭṭhe ca, saṅkappassa vasī-katā?

 

"My friend Satagiri, what is he like? Is the mind of the Buddha whom you have seen naturally and completely free from love and hatred for all beings and is it stable as befits one endowed with steadfastness? What is he like? Is the Buddha Gotama whom you have seen able to free himself or overcome the sensual thoughts (kāma-vitakka), ill-will thoughts (vyāpāda-vitakka) and cruel thoughts (vihiṁsā-vitakka) which tend to give rise to love and hatred for desirable and undesirable objects?"

 

After this, Satagiri, being absolutely convinced that the Buddha was certainly the omniscient Buddha, and wishing to answer Hemavata's questions regarding the full range of qualities of the omniscient Buddha, replied by reciting this verse:

 

Mano cassa supaṇihito, sabba-bhūtesu tādino,

atho iṭṭhe aniṭṭhe ca, saṅkappassa vasī-katā.

 

"My friend Hemavata, the mind of the Buddha whom I have seen is naturally and completely free from love and hatred towards all beings, as befits one endowed with steadfastness.

 

Even during the practice and development of the pairs to become a Buddha, the Bodhisatta was endowed with steadfastness, not to mention his steadfastness now that he has become a Buddha!

 

In his life as the elephant king Chaddanta, he had no animosity towards the hunter Sonuttara who intentionally killed him, but instead cut off his own tusks and gave them to Sonuttara as a gift.

 

Also in his life as the monkey king, he did not feel hatred even towards the hostile brahmin who hit him on the head with a stone to kill him, but instead, without anger, showed the brahmin the way out of the forest.

 

In his life as the sage Vidhura, he had no hatred for the yaksha Punnaka, who dragged him by the legs and cruelly threw him headfirst into a ravine at the foot of Mount Kala, 60 yojanas high; he even taught him the Dhamma."

 

That is why Satagiri boldly gave his answer:

"The mind of the Buddha whom I have seen is naturally and completely free from love and hatred towards all beings, as befits one endowed with steadfastness."

 

"My friend Hemavata, the Buddha whom I have seen is able to liberate himself or overcome sensual thoughts (kāma-vitakka), ill-willed thoughts (vyāpāda-vitakka) and cruel thoughts (vihiṁsā-vitakka), which tend to give rise to love and hatred for desired and undesired objects."

 

Thus when Hemavata asked the question, first concerning the doors of the mind or thought (manodvāra), whether the Buddha was fully endowed with steadfastness, he received an affirmative answer from Satagiri. Wanting to ask again in order to be more certain whether the purity of the three doors (dvāra) of actions existed in the Buddha at the present time, or, in other words, having heard Satagiri's affirmative answer to the first brief question as to whether the Buddha was endowed with steadfastness concerning the three doors, and wanting to ask the question again in more detail in order to make the answer more firm, Hemavata asked again, reciting this verse:

 

Kacci adinnaṁ nādiyati, kacci pāṇesu saññato,

kacci ārā pamādamhā, kacci jhānaṁ na riñcati?

 

"My friend Satagiri, what is the Buddha you have seen like? Is he free from appropriating another's property not given by the owner physically or verbally? Is he one who completely abstains from the evil deed of killing beings? Is he free from attachment to the five objects of sensual pleasures and far removed from the unawareness that includes unchastity? Is he one who has abandoned the five hindrances but has not allowed the attainments of absorption (jhāna) to fade away?"

 

The Buddha refrained from taking what is not given (adinnādāna) and other forms of wrong conduct not only in his last life as a Buddha, but throughout the long period in the past he refrained from these evils. Due to the merit of refraining from such evils, he is endowed with the marks of a Great Man (Mahā-purisa-lakkhaṇa).

 

The whole world also praised Buddha with these words:

"Gotama abstains from stealing and so on," so Satagiri, wishing to answer in clear and bold words, spoke further:

 

Na so adinnaṁ ādiyati, atho pāṇesu saññato,

atho ārā pamādamhā, Buddho jhānaṁ na riñcati.

 

"My friend Hemavata, the Buddha whom I have seen is one who is free from appropriating another's property not given by the owner physically or verbally. He is one who completely abstains from the evil deed of killing beings. He is free from attachment to the five objects of sensual pleasures and far from unawareness such as unchastity actions. He is also one who has abandoned the five hindrances and has not allowed the attainments of absorption (jhāna) to fade away."

 

 

Kacci musā na bhaṇati, kacci na khīṇa-byappatho,

kacci vebhūtiyaṁ nāha, kacci samphaṁ na bhāsati?

 

"My friend Satagiri, what is he like? Is the Buddha you have seen one who does not speak lies? Is he one who does not utter harsh words that would upset and depress beings? Is he one who does not utter words that maliciously destroy friendship between two people? Is he one who does not indulge in frivolous talk that is as insignificant and useless as unripe rice?"

 

The Buddha refrained from verbal misconduct not only in his last life as a Buddha, but throughout the long period in the past he refrained from lying and other verbal misconduct. Due to the merit of refraining from misconduct in words, he is endowed with such marks of a Great Man as one hair growing in each pore, hair between the eyebrows (uṇṇa-loma), and others. The whole world also praised the Buddha: "Gotama refrains from misconduct such as lying," and so on.

 

Musā ca so na bhaṇati, atho na khīṇa-byappatho,

atho vebhūtiyaṁ nāha, mantā atthaṁ ca bhāsati.

 

"My friend Hemavata, verily the Buddha whom I have seen does not speak lies. It is also true that he does not utter harsh and unpleasant words. It is also true that he does not speak malicious words. He speaks only wise and beneficial words."

 

Hemavata, having heard the affirmative answer regarding purity of speech and wishing to ask whether the Buddha at present possessed purity of mind, had overcome ignorance and was endowed with five eyes, asked, reciting this verse:

 

Kacci na rajjati kāmesu, kacci cittaṁ anāvilaṁ,

kacci mohaṁ atikkanto, kacci dhammesu cakkhumā?

 

"My friend Sagari, what is he like? Is the Buddha you have seen free from greed (abhijjhā) for the five material objects of sense pleasure? Is the mind of the Buddha you have seen free from the unwholesome mental state aroused by hatred (vyāpāda)? Has the Buddha you have seen really overcome the fourfold delusion (moha), which is the root cause of wrong view (micchā-diṭṭhi)? Is he really endowed with the eye of wisdom that penetrates all phenomena without obstruction?"

 

Even before realizing the fruit of Arhatship and even at the moment of attaining the path of Non-return, the Buddha had become free from the defilement of craving for sense objects and also free from the defilement of ill will, the unwholesome mental state, because he had already abandoned the defilement of sense pleasure (kāma-rāga-kilesa) and the defilement of ill will (vyāpāda-kilesa). Even at the moment of attaining Stream-Entry, he was already one who had overcome ignorance, because he had already abandoned the delusion that conceals the four truths, which is the cause of wrong view (micchā-diṭṭhi).

He had already earned the title of Buddha and had also realized the knowledge of insight, since he had realized the four truths by himself, through his own knowledge. Therefore, Satagiri, wishing to boldly proclaim the fact that the Buddha had purity in regard to his consciousness and, having attained omniscience, was a genuine Buddha, responded by reciting this verse:

 

Na so rajjati kāmesu, atho cittaṁ anāvilaṁ,

sabba-mohaṁ atikkanto, Buddho dhammesu cakkhumā .

 

"My friend Hemavata, the Buddha I have seen is truly free from greed for material objects and sensual pleasures. The mind of the Buddha I have seen is also free from ill will. The Buddha I have seen has truly overcome all the fourfold delusion 2, which is the root cause of wrong views. Because he has penetrated all phenomena with independent knowledge, he has earned the title of Buddha and is also endowed with the fivefold vision."

 

Thus Hemavata was extremely pleased and rejoiced to hear and know that the Buddha had purity in regard to the three sense spheres and was a genuine omniscient Buddha. Being himself endowed with the blessing of great learning (bāhu-sacca-maṅgala) in his previous life at the time of Buddha Kassapa, and therefore an eloquent, learned speaker with very pure knowledge and wisdom, and wishing to hear again about the further wonderful and extraordinary qualities of the Buddha, he asked, reciting this verse:

 

Kacci vijjāya sampanno, kacci saṁsuddha-cāraṇo,

kaccissa āsavā khīṇā, kacci natthi punabbhavo?

 

"My friend Satagiri, is the Buddha you have seen endowed with the eye of understanding (vijjā) that all Buddhas must have? Is he really endowed with the fifteenfold pure conduct, like good legs used for ascending to Nibbāna? Is the Buddha you have seen already free from the four defilements (āsava)? Is the Buddha you have seen free from the possibility of appearing in a new existence or being reborn?"

 

After this, Satagiri, because he had deep and firm faith in the Buddha and a desire to confirm that the Buddha was fully endowed with all the qualities that Hemavata had asked about, replied by reciting this verse:

 

Vijjāya ceva sampanno, atho saṁsuddha-cāraṇo,

sabbassa āsavā khīṇā, natthi tassa punabbhavo.

 

"My friend Hemavata, the Buddha I have seen is truly endowed with the eye of understanding that all Buddhas must be endowed with. He also truly possesses the fifteenfold pure conduct, like good legs used for the path to Nibbana. The Buddha I have seen is already free from the four defilements. The Buddha I have seen is free from the possibility of appearing in a new existence."

 

Then Hemavata became free from doubts about the Buddha, thinking thus:

"The Buddha whom Satagiri has just seen is the true Buddha, the Completely Self-Awakened One and the supreme one in the three worlds."

So, still remaining in the sky, he recited this verse to praise the Buddha and please Satagiri:

 

Sampannaṁ Munino cittaṁ, kammunā byappathena ca,

vijjā-caraṇa-sampannaṁ, dhammato naṁ pasaṁsati.

 

"My friend Satagiri, the mind of the Buddha whom you have seen is endowed with steadfastness. He is endowed with purity of action, word and thought. My friend Satagiri, you have rightly praised the Buddha who is endowed with the three understandings (vijjā), the eight understandings and the fifteen modes of good conduct (caraṇa) 3."

 

Then Satagiri, with the intention of once again pleasing Hemavata, recited this verse, meaning:

"My friend Hemavata, what you have said is quite true. Now you, my friend, fully know the qualities of the Buddha and are very happy":

 

Sampannaṁ Munino cittaṁ, kammunā byappathena ca,

vijjā-caraṇa-sampannaṁ, dhammato anumodasi.

 

"My friend Hemavata, the mind of the Buddha whom I have seen is endowed with steadfastness. He is endowed with purity of deeds, words and thoughts. My friend Hemavata, you have rightly rejoiced in the Buddha who is endowed with the three understandings, the eight understandings and the fifteen modes of good conduct."

 

And having said this and intending to induce Hemavata to go with him to the Buddha, Satagiri asked with this verse:

 

Sampannaṁ Munino cittaṁ, kammunā byappathena ca,

vijjā-caraṇa-sampannaṁ, handa passāma Gotamaṁ.

 

"My friend Hemavata, the mind of the Buddha whom I have seen is endowed with steadfastness. He is endowed with purity of action, word and thought. Let us go right now and respectfully see the Buddha who is endowed with the three understandings, the eight understandings and the fifteen modes of good conduct."

 

Then Hemavata, as one who had acquired the power of great learning in his previous existence, wished to speak in praise of the qualities of the Buddha that had attracted him, and to invite Satagiri to go with him and see the Buddha. Thus he recited the following verses:

 

Eṇi-jaṅghaṁ kisaṁ vīraṁ, appāhāraṁ alolupaṁ,

muniṁ vanasmiṁ jhāyantaṁ, ehi passāma Gotamaṁ.

 

"Come, Satagiri, let us go. Let us behold a Buddha of the Gotama family, whose calves are round like those of an antelope (eṇī) living in the upper reaches of the forest; whose limbs and other parts of the body, large and small, are respectively long in five respects, short in four, small in four, tall in six, and round where they should be round; who is diligent and able to withstand and fight against dangers from within and without the body; who takes only one meal a day just for the maintenance of his body; who has gotten rid of excessive thirst (loluppa-taṇhā) for food; who is in full possession of the knowledge of the four paths; and who habitually dwells in absorption in the secluded forest."

 

Sīhaṁveka-caraṁ nāgaṁ, kāmesu anapekkhinaṁ,

upasaṅkamma pucchāma, maccupāsappa-mocanaṁ.

 

"My friend Satagiri, let us go to the presence of the Buddha who, like the lion king Kesara, cannot be easily approached by ordinary people; who can endure the vicissitudes of the world; who is fearless; who is the only Buddha to appear in the universe; who, like the elephant king Chaddanta, is endowed with great physical and intellectual strength; and who is free from all desire and passion for all objects of sense pleasure. Let us ask him about Nibbana, which will surely free us from the round of suffering in the three planes of existence, from the snare of the King of Death."

 

Thus Hemavata induced Satagiri and a retinue of 1000 yakshas to come with him to worship the Buddha and listen to the sermon.

 

Two Yakshas are going to Buddha

 

Satagiri and Hemavata, accompanied by their 1000 yaksha followers, reached the Deer Park at Isipatana in the city of Baranasi at midnight. And having approached and paid homage to the Buddha, who had not changed his posture but was still sitting cross-legged as when preaching the Wheel of Dhamma, they recited this verse to praise the Buddha and to ask permission to ask him questions:

 

Akkhā-tāraṁ pavattāraṁ, sabba-dhammāna' pāraguṁ,

Buddhaṁ vera-bhayātītaṁ, mayaṁ pucchāma Gotamaṁ.

 

"To the Buddha Gotama, who preaches the four noble truths, both briefly and in detail; who is fully endowed with knowledge of all things (dhamma) in six ways: through superknowledge (abhiññā), analysis (pariññā), abandonment (pahāna), meditation (bhāvanā), the realization of Nibbāna (sacchikiriya), and the attainment of absorptions (jhāna-samāpatti); who has awakened from the slumber of delusion (moha); and who has freed himself from the five wrong paths, such as taking life (pāṇātipātā) and others, may we have your permission to ask you about what we do not know?"


The question of the origin of the world of beings and conditioned things

After receiving permission, Hemavata, who had the greater strength and wisdom of the two, asked questions about what was unknown to them, reciting the following verse:

 

Kismiṁ loko samuppanno, kismiṁ kubbati santhavaṁ,

kissa loko upādāya, kismiṁ loko vihaññati?

 

"Blessed Buddha, in the presence of the arising of what do the two worlds come into being: the world of living beings (satta-loka) and the world of conditioned things (saṅkhāra-loka)? In what do all beings, such as humans, devas, and brahmas, closely associate themselves with craving and wrong views (taṇhā-diṭṭhi), thinking 'I' and 'mine'? Thereupon, the world of living beings and the world of conditioned things are so called? In the arising of what do all beings, such as humans, devas, and brahmas, become miserable?"

Then the Buddha, intending to answer the question put by Hemavata, relying on the six internal sense organs: eye, ear, nose, tongue, body and mind (ajjhattik-āyatana) and the six external sense objects: form, sound, smell, taste, touch and ideas formed in the mind, replied by reciting this verse:

 

When the loko is samuppanno, when the kubbati is santhavaṁ,

channam-eva upādāya, chasu loko vihaññati.

 

"Hemavata, when the six internal organs and the six external objects arise, two worlds arise: the world of living beings and the world of conditioned things.

 

From the standpoint of absolute truth (paramattha-dhamma), the world of living beings, men, devas and brahmas, is simply a composite of these twelve spheres, namely, the six inner ones - eye, ear, nose, tongue, body and mind; and the six outer ones - form, sound, smell, taste, contact and the idea formed in the mind. Without these twelve spheres there can be no such thing as a being, whether man, deva or brahma. From the standpoint of absolute truth, the world of conditioned things, meaning such things as fields, land, gold, silver, rice and so on, consists of only the six outer spheres. Without these six there can be no world of inanimate conditioned things. Hence the Buddha's answer: Chasu loko samuppanno..., "when the six inner and six outer spheres arise, two worlds arise - the world of living beings and the world of inanimate conditioned things."

 

Hemavata, in the six internal and six external spheres, all beings, that is, humans, devas and brahmas, are closely associated with craving and wrong views through the concepts of 'I' and 'mine'.

 

All beings, that is, men, devas and brahmas, who in close friendship with craving and wrong views take 'I', 'other', 'man', 'woman', 'field', 'earth' and so on as 'I' and 'mine', are, from the standpoint of absolute truth, composites of the same six inner and six outer spheres. This is the truth. Taking the eye as 'I' and 'mine', beings are friends with craving and wrong views; taking the ear as 'I' and 'mine', they are friends with craving and wrong views; they do the same with the nose, the tongue, the body, the mind, and with forms, sounds, smells, tastes, touches such as hardness or softness, heat or cold, and the ideas that arise in the mind. Hence the Buddha's answer: Chasu kubbati santhavaṁ... "In the six inner and six outer realms, all beings, humans, devas and brahmas, closely associate themselves with craving and wrong views through the concepts of 'I' and 'mine.'"

 

Hemavata, the six inner and six outer spheres, are the so-called world of living beings and the world of conditioned things.

 

In relation to the above-mentioned twelve spheres, there arose such names as "Humans", "Devas", "Brahmas", that is, the world of living beings, and such names as "field", "earth", "rice", "paddy" and so on - the world of inanimate conditioned things.

Hemavata, when the six internal and six external spheres are clearly manifest, all living entities, namely human beings, devas and brahmas, become unhappy.

According to the Discourse on Burning (Āditta-pariyāya-sutta, SN 35.28), the twelve realms are ablaze with the eleven fires of greed, hatred, and delusion (rāga-dosa-moha) and others. From the standpoint of ultimate truth, the world of sentient beings, consisting of humans, devas, and brahmas, is also simply these twelve realms, the six inner and the six outer. These realms are also constantly and separately ablaze with the eleven fires. Because there are realms, there is burning; because there is burning, there is suffering. If there were no realms, there would be no burning; if there were no burning, there would be no suffering. That is why the Buddha replied thus: Chasu loko vihaññati... "when the six inner and six outer realms are clearly manifest, all beings, that is, humans, devas, and brahmas, become miserable."

 

After this, Hemavata, being unable to clearly remember the brief answer given by the Buddha to his question about the round of suffering, and wishing to know both the enumeration of the spheres and their opposites contained in the Buddha's answer, recited the following verse to ask about the round of suffering (vaṭṭa) and the cessation of the round of suffering (vivaṭṭa):

 

Katamaṁ taṁ upādānaṁ, yattha loko vihaññati,

niyyānaṁ pucchito brūhi, kathaṁ dukkhā pamuccati?

 

“Blessed Buddha, if, according to the answer: Chasu loko vihaññati... 'living beings, that is, humans, devas and brahmas, become unhappy due to the existence of the six realms,' how are these six the cause of suffering for beings?”

 

This directly asks about the truth of suffering (dukkha-sacca); by asking about the truth of suffering, one also asks about the truth of the cause of suffering (samudaya-sacca).

 

“What is the factor that brings about liberation from the cycle of suffering? How can liberation from the cycle of suffering be realized? May the Buddha, who has been thus approached with the question of liberation from the cycle of suffering, have the mercy to answer us.”

 

The second half of this verse directly asks about the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering (magga-sacca); and in asking about the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering, one also asks about the truth of the cessation of suffering, that is, Nibbana (nirodha-sacca).

 

When Hemavata asked about the four noble truths, mentioning the truth of suffering and the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering explicitly, and the truth of the origin of suffering and the truth of the cessation of suffering implicitly, the Buddha recited the following verse to answer the questions posed by Hemavata:

 

Pañca kāma-guṇā loke, mano-chaṭṭhā paveditā,

ettha chandaṁ virājetvā, evaṁ dukkhā pamuccati.

 

"Hemavata, I have clearly shown the five sense pleasures of form, sound, smell, taste and touch, with the mind as the sixth in the world (kāma-guṇa).

 

By the word 'mind' in the first half of this verse the mental sphere (manāyatana) is directly taught; and by teaching the mental sphere the object of the mind (dhammāyatana) is also taught. By the five sense pleasures (kāma-guṇa) of form, sound, odour, taste and touch the five objects (āyatana) are directly taught: the form object, the sound object, the odour object, the taste object and the touch object; and by teaching these five sense objects their five recipients are also taught: the eye, the ear, the nose, the tongue and the body. Thus by the first half of this verse the six internal spheres (ajjhittikāyatana) and the six external spheres (bāhirāyatana) are taught, twelve in all; these spheres may be called clinging (upādāna), which forms the suffering of the world of living beings.

 

Hemavata, the thirst and desire (taṇhā-chanda) for the totality of these twelve realms, the circle of suffering and the truth of suffering, must be completely eliminated and destroyed.

 

For their elimination and destruction, they must first be distinguished either as aggregates (pañcaskangha), or as spheres (ayatana), or as elements (dhatu), or briefly as mind and matter (nama-rupa). They must be meditated upon for insight (vipasanā), subjecting them to the test of the three characteristics (anica, anata, dukkha). Their elimination and destruction ultimately occurs through insight, which culminates in the path of the fruit of Arahant. By eliminating and destroying them, one is freed from the cycle of suffering.

 

The second half of the verse answers the question of cessation (vivaṭṭa), and also shows the truth of the path (magga-sacca). The truth of origination (samudaya-sacca) and cessation (nirodha-sacca) are considered to be answered, as they were briefly stated in the previous answer in the verse.

 

In other words, the first half of the verse shows the truth of suffering; by the term desire and passion (chanda-rāga) in the second half of the verse, the arising of suffering is shown. From the word 'having removed' (virājetvā) is derived dispassion (virāga), which is Nibbana, the cessation of craving, and also the truth of cessation (nirodha-sacca). By the word 'thus' (evaṁ) the truth of the path (magga-sacca) is shown, for it signifies the course of practice in the eightfold path leading to liberation from the suffering of Samsara. Thus the four truths are proclaimed by the Buddha in this verse.

 

The Buddha thus showed the excellent way of exit, which is the eightfold path as the means of escape from the round of births and deaths. And again, because he wished to conclude his answer on the way of exit in natural language, he recited the following verse:

 

Etaṁ lokassa niyyānaṁ, akkhātaṁ vo yathā-tathaṁ,

etaṁ vo aham-akkhāmi, evaṁ dukkhā pamuccati.

 

"Hemavata, I have taught you this means, the Eightfold Path, which can lead to liberation from the conditioned world of the three elements (tedhātuka-saṅkhāra-loka), such as the element of sensual pleasures (kāma-dhātu), the element of materiality (rūpa-dhātu), and the element of immateriality (arūpa-dhātu). Since there can be no liberation from the suffering of the round of birth and death except through this Eightfold Path, and since there is no other path of liberation, even if you were to ask 1000 times, I will tell you only this: this Eightfold Path is the only excellent Dhamma for liberation from the round of birth and death. Or, since there can be liberation from the suffering of the round of birth and death only through the Eightfold Path, and since there is no other path of liberation, I will speak only of the Eightfold Path as the only excellent The Dhamma that leads to liberation from the cycle of birth and death, to enable you, who have already realized the lower paths and fruits, to realize the higher ones."

 

Yakshas became “Stream Enterers”

 

Thus the Buddha perfectly completed the discourse of the Dhamma, which reached its culmination in the fruit of Arahant (Arahatta-phala). At the end of this discourse, the two yakṣas, Satagiri and Hemavata, were established in the fruit of stream entry, together with their retinue of 1,000 yakṣas.

 

After this, Hemavata, being one who by nature had due respect and devotion to the Dhamma, and now established as a noble Stream Enterer, was not fully satisfied with the wonderful Dhamma of the Buddha, which was perfect both in letter and in spirit. Therefore, desiring to know the two causes: the cause of becoming inferior noble ones (sekkha-bhūmi-dhamma) and the cause of becoming Arahants (asekkha-bhūmi-dhamma), he addressed the Buddha, reciting the following verse:

 

Ko sūdha tarati oghaṁ, kodha tarati aṇṇavaṁ,

appatiṭṭhe anālambe, ko gambhīre na sīdati?

 

"Blessed Buddha, who in the world, endowed with virtuous conduct, is able to cross the turbulent waters of the four streams? Who, endowed with virtuous conduct in this world, is able to cross the wide and deep ocean of Saṁsāra? Who can remain whole and unharmed without drowning in the bottomless ocean of Saṁsāra, where there is nothing below to stand on and nothing above to grab hold of?"

 

The first half of this verse asked about the reason for becoming noble from the lower classes, and the second half asked about the reason for becoming Arahats.

 

Then the Buddha recited the following verse, wishing to answer the reason for the lower becoming noble:

 

Sabbadā sīla-sampanno, paññavā susamāhito,

ajjhatta-cintī satimā, oghaṁ tarati duttaraṁ.

 

"Hemavata, the monk who is endowed with morality constantly, without transgressing the precepts, but with care for their observance even at the risk of life; who is also endowed with mundane and supramundane knowledge; who is also steadfast in access concentration (upacāra-samādhi) and full concentration (appanā-samādhi); who by means of insight knowledge (vipassanā-ñāṇa) constantly meditates on the mental and physical aggregates known internally as his own (niyakajjhatta), applying the three characteristics; who is also possessed of mindfulness (sati), enabling him to practice continually the threefold training (sikkhā), namely, virtue, concentration, and wisdom (sīla-samādhi-paññā); he, thus endowed with this threefold training, is able to cross the turbulent expanses of the four streams, which is difficult for ordinary people to do."

 

After the Buddha gave an answer about the cause of becoming lower noble, he recited the following verse to give an answer about the cause of becoming Arahants:

 

Virato kāma-saññāya, sabba-saṁyojanātigo,

nandī-bhava-parikkhīṇo, so gambhīre na sīdati .

 

"Hemavata, the monk who has attained complete abstinence from all perceptions accompanied by craving for the material objects of sense pleasures; who has also broken and cast off the long belt of the ten attachments by means of the four paths; in whom the three types of craving and the three states of existence, collectively called enjoyments (nandī), have completely dried up; who is an Arahant fully endowed with these qualities, he does not drown in the vast and bottomless ocean of Saṁsāra, where there is nothing below to stand on and nothing above to grasp."

 

He has now attained with the greatest ease Nibbana with remainder (sa-upādisesa-nibbāna), because his thirst is exhausted, and he is on the verge of Nibbana without remainder (anupādisesa-nibbāna) due to the cessation of existence. Thus his landing is with the greatest ease and comfort, and he is one who does not sink.

 

Two Yakshas sing praises

 

After this, Hemavata looked with satisfaction at his friend Satagiri and also at the retinue of 1000 Yakshas, and recited the following five verses in praise of the Buddha. Together with his friend Satagiri and the retinue of a thousand Yakshas, he paid homage to the Buddha with due respect and devotion and returned home. The five verses of praise and veneration were:

 

Gambhīra-paññaṁ nipuṇattha-dassiṁ,

akiñcanaṁ kāma-bhave asattaṁ,

taṁ passatha sabbadhi vippamuttaṁ,

dibbe pathe kamamānaṁ Mahesiṁ.

 

"O venerable fellow Devas, behold with your clear eyes the Buddha Gotama, who is endowed with analytical wisdom regarding such profound matters as the aggregates (khandha), sense spheres (āyatana), etc.; who thoroughly sees the meaning of the questions presented by those possessed of subtle intellect; who is devoid of the slightest particle of the sevenfold evils of greed, hatred, delusion, conceit, wrong view, bad conduct and defilements; who has no attachment to sensuality and the threefold existence; who is completely liberated from the bondage of desire and lust for all sense objects such as the aggregates, sense spheres, etc.; who is able to walk up and down the divine path of the eight attainments; and who has sought such noble qualities as the observance of the code of moral precepts (sīlakkhandha)."

 

Anoma-nāmaṁ nipuṇattha-dassiṁ,

paññā-dadaṁ kāmālaye asattaṁ,

taṁ passatha sabba-viduṁ sumedhaṁ,

ariye pathe kamamānaṁ Mahesiṁ.

 

"O venerable fellow Devas, behold with your clear eyes the Buddha Gotama, who has extraordinary epithets through undiminished attributes such as the Completely Self-Awakened One (Sammā-sambuddha); who thoroughly sees the meaning of questions presented by those possessed of subtle intellect; who disseminates extraordinary knowledge by giving instructions in a sweet and pleasant voice so that others may be moral and knowledgeable; who has no clinging with passion and wrong view such as 'I' and 'mine' to the various sense objects sought after through passion and wrong view; who analytically knows everything; who is endowed with knowledge of the perfections that form the basis of omniscience; who is able to walk up and down the divine road of the eight attainments; and who has sought noble qualities such as the observance of the code of moral precepts."

 

Sudiṭṭhaṁ vata no ajja, suppabhātaṁ suhuṭṭhitaṁ,

yaṁ addasāma Sambuddhaṁ, ogha-tiṇṇam-anāsavaṁ.

 

"O venerable fellow Devas, since we have had the good fortune to behold the Buddha with our eyes, who has culminated in the attainment of the paths and fruits; the Buddha who has crossed the turbulent waters of the fourfold stream and who has eliminated the fourfold defilements (āsava). Our vision of the Buddha, our timely contemplation of his person, has truly occurred today! Such an advent of dawn, such a dawn leaving behind all darkness and faults, has truly occurred! Awakening from deep sleep, fully awakening from sleep without greed, hatred and delusion, has truly now occurred!"

 

Ime dasa-satā Yakkhā, iddhimanto yasassino,

sabbe taṁ saraṇaṁ yanti, tvaṁ no Satthā anuttaro.

 

"Venerable Buddha of radiant glory, all these divine Yakshas, endowed with supernatural powers arising from their past good deeds; possessing many excellent acquisitions and retinues; numbering 1,000, with their bodies imbued with the spirit of the great supramundane refuge, approach with us to seek protection, believing that you are the refuge. You are our unsurpassed supreme teacher, who assists and guides us, giving instructions so that we can build the first exquisitely decorated palace of the noble ones."

 

Te mayaṁ vicarissāma, gāmā gāmaṁ nagā nagaṁ,

namassamānā Sambuddhaṁ, Dhammassa ca Sudhammataṁ.

 

"Venerable Buddha of radiant glory, from now on we will move from one divine village to another, from one divine mountain to another, proclaiming like town criers, calling for the path to Nibbana and singing praises to the respective glories of the Three Treasures; we will proclaim, paying homage to Buddhahood, with folded hands placed on our heads, and our joyful devotion destined for the true Buddha, the Lord of the Three Worlds, and full of immeasurable attributes, as well as the state of the Dhamma, the path of exiting the round of suffering, the teaching which is tenfold, its constituents being the four paths, the four fruits, Nibbana and the mass of parts of the Dhamma."
