How does consciousness, analyzed into eighty-nine types, become one hundred and twenty-one types?

 

1. Path consciousness of the first jhana of stream-entry, together with initial application, sustained application, rapture, happiness, and one-pointedness.

2. Path consciousness of the second jhana of stream-entry, together with sustained application, rapture, happiness, and one-pointedness.

3. Path consciousness of the third jhana of stream-entry, together with rapture, happiness, and one-pointedness.

4. Path consciousness of the fourth jhana of stream-entry, together with happiness and one-pointedness.

5. Path consciousness of the fifth jhana of stream-entry, together with equanimity and one-pointedness.

 

These are the five types of path consciousness of stream-entry. Similarly for the path consciousness of once-returning, non-returning, and Arahantship, which makes twenty types of path consciousness. In the same way, there are twenty types of fruit consciousness. Thus, there are forty types of supramundane consciousness.

 

Commentary

All meditators attain the supramundane paths and fruits through the development of wisdom (paññā) — the insight into the three characteristics: impermanence, suffering, and selflessness. However, they differ from each other in the degree of development of concentration (samādhi). Those who have developed insight without a basis in jhana are called practitioners of pure ["mere"] insight (sukkhavipassakā). When they attain the path and fruit, their path and fruit cittas occur at a level corresponding to the first jhana.

Those who have developed insight on the basis of jhana attain the path and fruit corresponding to the level of jhana they reached before attaining the path. Ancient teachers put forward different views on which factors determine the jhana level of the path and fruit. One school of thought holds that it is the foundation jhana (pādakajjhāna), that is, the jhana used as a foundation for concentrating the mind before developing insight, which reaches its highest degree in the attainment of the supramundane path. A second theory asserts that the jhana level of the path is determined by the jhana used as the object of investigation in insight meditation, which is called the attained or investigated jhana (sammasitajjhāna). And a third school of thought says that when a meditator has mastered a range of jhanas, they can control the jhana level of the path by their personal wish or inclination (ajjhāsayā). Despite this, whichever explanation is accepted, for both the pure insight meditator and the jhana-meditator, all path and fruit cittas are considered as types of jhana consciousness. This consideration is because these cittas, like the mundane jhanas, occur in the mode of close contemplation of the object, with full immersion in it, and also because they possess the jhana factors, the intensity of which corresponds to their counterparts in the mundane jhanas. The supramundane jhanas of the paths and fruits differ from the mundane jhanas in several important aspects.

First: while mundane jhanas take a concept, such as a kasina image, as their object, the supramundane jhanas take Nibbāna—the unconditioned reality—as their object.

Second: whereas mundane jhanas merely suppress defilements, leaving the underlying sources intact, the supramundane path jhanas eradicate defilements so that they can never arise again.Third: whereas mundane jhanas lead to rebirth in the fine-material world and thus sustain existence in the cycle of rebirths, the path jhanas cut off the fetters that bind a being to the cycle, and thus resolve into liberation from the round of birth and death. Finally, whereas the role of wisdom in mundane jhanas is subordinate to concentration, in the supramundane jhanas, wisdom and concentration are perfectly balanced, with concentration fixing the mind on the unconditioned element, and wisdom penetrating the profound significance of the Four Noble Truths.

According to their combination of jhana factors, the path and fruit cittas are distributed along the scale of the five jhanas. Thus, instead of a simple eightfold enumeration of supramundane consciousness by type of path and fruit, each path and fruit consciousness can be enumerated as consisting of five—according to the level of jhana at which it can occur.

Because of this, the eight supramundane cittas, each taken at all five levels of jhana, become equal to forty, as shown in Table 1.10.
