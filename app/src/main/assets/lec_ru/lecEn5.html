Dhatunanatta-sutta: Variety of the elements, SN 14.1

From today we will talk about Vipassana. We can practice it in terms of 6 ayatanas (spheres of perception), but there is also an explanation through 18 dhatus or elements.

1. Eye element (cakkhu-dhatu).
2. Element of color (rupa-dhatu).
3. Element of eye consciousness (cakkhu-vinnana-dhatu).

4. Ear element (sota-dhatu).
5. The element of sound (sadda-dhatu).
6. Element of ear consciousness (sota-vinnana-dhatu).

7. Element of the nose (ghana-dhatu).
8. Element of smell (gandha-dhatu).
9. Element of smell consciousness (ghana-vinnana-dhatu).

10. Element of language (jivha-dhatu).
11. Element of taste (rasa-dhatu).
12. Element of taste consciousness (jivha-vinnana-dhatu).

13. Body element (kaya-dhatu).
14. Element of tangible object (photthabba-dhatu).
15. Body consciousness element (kaya-vinnana-dhatu).

16. Element of mind (mano-dhatu).
17. Element of mental objects (dhamma-dhatu).
18. Element of mind consciousness (mano-vinnana-dhatu).

If we take this definition, then there are only these 18 elements in the world. There is nothing in the world except the elements. Vipassana will show us what paramatha is : the highest truth, absolute reality.
Usually, when we hear some sound, and after that the thought Уthere is some person thereФ appears in our mind (i.e. the thought that the world is there), then this means that we are already in the world.
Why is dhatu a sunnya ? That is, why are the elements УemptyФ? Because there is only paramattha (primary reality), there is nothing else there. There is no person there, there is no meaning, so we say it is sunnah .

The eye, Ananda, is empty from the "I" and that which belongs to the "I". Forms... eye consciousness... eye contact... any feeling that arises with eye contact as a cause - pleasant, painful or neither-pleasant-nor-painful - is also empty of "I" and that which belongs to the "I". Ear... nose... tongue... body... mind... mental phenomena... consciousness of the mind... contact of the mind... any feeling that arises, having as a cause the contact of the mind... is also empty of the "I". If we manage to stop the sound as a sound and do not follow it further, then this is already Vipassana. But if we donТt have time and follow him, then we are late and end up in samsara. For this we meditate, for this we must practice and develop our awareness.
Doing this right away is not easy, but we must know exactly how to practice, we need to understand where we are aiming. And if we know, then we can know our body precisely as elements, we can know ayatanas as elements, objects of ayatanas as elements. And we can continue to practice this.
The shortest instruction on Vipassana that the Buddha gave sounds like this: ditthe ditthamattan, sute sutamattan, muthe muthamattan, vinnyate vinnyamattan. In the visible there is only the visible, in the audible only the audible, in the sensed only the sensed, in the knowable only the knowable ( Bahiya Sutta: Bahiya, Udana 1.10 ). If we can understand what Vipassana is, then it is easier for us to practice.
When we sit and meditate, we observe our body sensations. But what is the body? This is already our daddy , mental growth. We can say that papancha are erroneous concepts, concepts with kilesas (mental defilements) that we believe exist.
The body can also be known as vinnana (consciousness), as kaya-vinnana-dhatu (element of body consciousness). If thoughts appear about this, we can recognize them as mano-vinnana-dhatu (element of mind consciousness). This is exactly what the Buddha shows us as Vipassana. This is what Vipassana leads to.
Here we have an ear and sounds, and because of the ear and sounds we have sanyojana (fetters). Whatever appears as thoughts from these sounds is all sanyojana . We have a thought that there is something there, and this thought is already sanyojana , these are fetters for us, these are our kilesas (impurities).
We must understand what Vipassana actually is. Vipassana means observing paramatha (reality), paramatha as they are, seeing the world as it is. Just like every ayatana, every dhatu (element) has its own task, we can understand and observe this. But at the beginning you need to know and think about this: the eye can only recognize colors, the objects of the eye can only be colors. You need to think this through carefully, understand it from your own experience, and let it pass through yourself. The ear can only hear, the objects of the ear can only be sounds. Nose - smells, tongue - tastes, body - tangible things, mind and its objects. If it seems to us that we already know this, then we need to understand that this is actually how our ignorance manifests itself. Our ignorance is so deep that we don't even see it.
You need to think about this very thoughtfully. Then we can watch the mind: when the mind begins to think about something, we can determine whether it is an object of the mind or, for example, an object of the ear. Only then does our ignorance decrease. Otherwise, we begin to think and the idea arises in our minds that what we are thinking about exists somewhere out there, and these are already erroneous views. We must consider this in reverse, and to do this we must constantly think this way.
How to apply this in practice: here we sit and watch, the mind begins to perceive, for example, the cry of a squirrel, and the idea immediately appears in the mind - Уthere is a squirrel there.Ф First of all, we need to remove this erroneous misunderstanding. To do this, you need to think in reverse: УNo, this is not so, the squirrel is not there, the squirrel is in the mind.Ф It means "squirrel" - it's a concept. The ear's perceptions are only sounds. There was a sound, and then a creature appeared in the mind. We need to think about this very carefully, itТs as if we are programming the mind in the opposite direction, reversely, not in the way the mind is accustomed to.
If we do this, what will happen? I heard a sound, then thoughts appeared that there was some kind of creature there, and I was thinking about it. Then awareness and wisdom appear, and we understand that there cannot be any being there, it is impossible. And why is there nothing there? Because what I think about are the objects of the mind. And awareness and wisdom will show that in the external world there were simply some elements - colors, sounds, smells, tastes, objects of touch - and they have already disappeared, and what exists now are objects of the mind, these are not beings. And this way it will be easy for us to overcome our attachment, greed, anger, because there will be no basis for holding on. They will have nothing to cling to.
Let us now see from a practical point of view which verses work. Let's take the ear as an example. A sound is heard and it disappears. The ear hears nothing except sounds. Then a squirrel, or a bird, or a person appears in the mind. Then it is immediately clear that for the УsquirrelФ we need two ayatana, or six dhatus: ear, sounds, consciousness of the ear, mind, objects of the mind, consciousness of the mind. Where exactly is the "squirrel"? The squirrel in the mind as an object of the mind. The consciousness of the mind appears and the idea of ​​УsquirrelФ arises. Because there is the mind, the object of the mind and the consciousness of the mind. The object УsquirrelФ has appeared in the mind and the consciousness of the mind understands: Уthe squirrel that exists there.Ф And after this whole process has taken place, the squirrel exists for us.
Or if we take the tongue: its task is to perceive tastes. Some tastes are pleasant to us, some are not. But when we eat, in fact, all the ayatans work for us, because we see, we smell, and we taste. Even one type of food is enough for us. For example, when people come to Sri Lanka, all food is unusual for them, and almost all food is very spicy for them. And it happens that people see some white food and think that because the food is white, it means it is not spicy, although in fact it can be very spicy. Or vice versa: they see red food and think that this food is spicy, although it is not necessarily spicy at all. But the mind says - this is spicy food. And the ayatana of the eye, the ayatana of the nose (smells), the ayatana of the tongue (tastes), the ayatana of the body (objects of touch), and the ayatana of the mind works. But if we take the ayatana of the tongue, then the tongue knows only taste. And the name of this food appears in the mind, and that this food is tasty, and so on.
And if you live here longer and get used to it, then you will already have asavs . Asawa is translated as inflows, leakages, or as streams of the mind: that this is this form, and this taste, this smell, whether I like it or not, it exists in Sri Lanka. And then the next time you come to Sri Lanka, you see this form, then your asavs are already triggered: УOh, I know what it is, give me two of these.Ф
If you look at what our attachment is to, what our thirst is for, then this УfoodФ is actually in our mind, for this УfoodФ we have some kind of story. If it were something familiar to you, some familiar dish that we have already seen many times (for example, buckwheat), then we may even forget the first time we ate it, how we got used to it. But at the same time, we already have the asavas themselves in our minds, so itТs enough for us to simply see the color and shape, and the object of consciousness of the mind immediately appears in our minds: Уthis is buckwheat.Ф
But we must understand that when we eat, we can observe how the tongue feels the taste, but in fact there are two ayatanas working in the mouth: the tongue feels the taste, and the body feels hardness, warmth and pressure. And some other thoughts appear in the mind. And if you observe what appears in the mind, then it is not at all connected with what is in the mouth. If we look at what is in our mouth, it will not be pleasant to us - everything is mixed up there. If we spit it out, we even feel disgust. But while we are eating, some other food appears in our minds: pleasant, with pleasant smells, and we may even forget about food altogether and think about something completely unrelated. These pleasant sensations are lobha anussaya : the hidden tendency towards greed.
Now we have many ayatans working: the eye sees, the ear hears, the body feels tangible things, thoughts appear in the mind - all this together gives us the idea that I am here: I am here, sitting in the hall and listening, the teacher speaks, it is raining. For this whole world to appear, different ayatans performed their tasks: the eye perceived colors, the ear perceived sounds. But every time after the eye comes the mind, after the ear comes the mind, after the body comes the mind. These ayatans work together and the mind connects them together.

The ayatans individually could not give us such an idea. But you need to know that this idea is also erroneous - it is a relative reality.
Remember how we visited a large monastery: we were sitting in a huge hall, there were a lot of people, the teacher was sitting in front and you couldnТt even see his face. There were speakers behind us, and we sat and listened. And every time we heard, we thought that we were listening to the teacher and that the sound was coming from him. But in fact, the sound came from behind, from the speakers, but the mind completely forgets about it. The mind thinks: there is a man in front, he is talking, sound is coming from there, I am listening to him. And some person appears in our mind, a teacher whom we listen to. Look how many ayatans work! But we can separate them, for this we first need this understanding. Then we must think a lot and carefully about this, ponder this knowledge, let it pass through ourselves. You need to take one ayatana and know it very well, so that the mind can see that in the external world there is nothing except the elements. There are only colors, sounds, smells, tastes, tangible things. Everything that arises after them arises in the mind: it is only the mind, it is not the external world. There are only ayatans here, except for the ayatans - the eye and its objects, the ear and its objects, and so on - there is nothing here except that. It all ends here. After that, whatever I see, whatever I think about, itТs all mind.
We can feel something behind us. But in fact, there shouldnТt be peace for us there, because the whole world that we feel there is not accessible to our five senses. And this whole world behind us is created by our mind - these are just ideas of the mind. At such moments we can separate the ayatanas and our mental concepts. Then we understand that this is a mental image and not reality. This is a relative reality, not an absolute one.
For example, when we see a person, we immediately have some idea about him, some assessment of what kind of person he is. For example, in Sri Lanka, if people see a white person, they usually think: this person definitely speaks English, which means that he is rich, that he is a tourist. People have such ideas, they just see someone - and immediately they have their own ideas. But this may not be so! We must understand that our mind works this way, these are the ideas of our mind, and this is not reality, the reality of what is visible. What I see is only colors, not a person, so what I think about is not visible. What I think is the mind, and what I see are colors, and they are not the same thing.
There is a very beautiful gatha in Pali: sankappa rago, purisassa kamo . Sankappa is concepts, raga is passion, purisassa is for a person. Kamo is a capacious term; it cannot be translated in just a few words. If you look at this word in the context of this gatha, then everything through which we want to receive sensual pleasures is all kamo for a person. We can say that kamo is sensual pleasures. The hall is kamo , tea, mugs, everything that is in this room, our relatives are all kamo . Even our religion can sometimes be kamo for us. Sometimes, for example, people come to worship the Bodhi tree in order to receive some kind of luck, to ask for something for themselves - then the tree has already become an object for a person to receive kamo . Everything we see in the world, everything we want in the world, everything we want is all kamo , so the Teaching always tells us that we need to move away from kamo .
When the Buddha explains what kamo is , he says that all kamo are concepts. This means that what we want, what we desire, is all in our minds: it is not in the outside world. What then is there for us in the outside world? Hardness, warmth, colors, sounds and so on. Elements.
This is a very deep gatha. We can think about it: everything I want, everything I desire, all this kamo , all these kamo objects - they are all in my mind. In the real world there are ayatan objects, and they are neither kusala (good) nor akusala (non-virtuous). The Buddha says: neither the eye nor the objects of the eye are good or not good, they are avyakata (neutral) - beyond this. So are the others: the ear and the objects of the ear, the tongue and the objects of language. All wholesome and unwholesome objects are concepts in the mind. If we can understand this at least at some level, thatТs already very good. And then we have to see it through one of the ayatanas. Here a person appears in the mind, and we see him, and we touch him. But touch is not a person. What we have in our minds and what we touch are two different things. If we can share this, then this will be the application of this knowledge in practice.

When we begin to observe ayatanas, then we can see, for example, that the eye only perceives colors, the mind does the rest. At first it seems that it is impossible to separate it, because it all seems to be happening at the same time. We see a person. And at first the mind does not believe it. But if we begin to practice, we can see this difference, see the eye, see the work of the eye, see the appearance of an object in the mind. And we can understand that the object in the mind is not the reality of what is seen, and we can separate them.
The Buddha says that if some sanyojana appears through the eye , then one should see the sanyojana as sanyojana . Sanyojana means impurities that appear along with something. For example, along with a vision some thoughts appear, and there is ignorance ( avijja ). Maybe there is disgust, maybe pride, envy - whatever. This has arisen and we must see: here were the objects of the eye and sanyojana appeared , and these are no longer the objects of the eye - these are already our defilements of the mind, our obscurations - this is already the mind. Defilements are always an object of the mind, so we must observe.
We practice by observing the sensations of the body. This has a double benefit. When we observe sensations there are pleasant and unpleasant sensations, and the Buddha says that we must overcome lobha (passion, greed) for pleasant sensations and overcome aversion to unpleasant sensations. And when we observe, we still have obscurations. For example, we observe a body, but in our mind there is an image of the body, that there are some parts of it. But in fact, we cannot see the body, Buddha says that the body is anidassan - it cannot be seen, just like the eye, ear, nose, tongue, mind - these are all anidassan . Then what do we see? Objects eyes, colors. And the mind perceives it as a body and we think that what I see is what I touch, and this is my body.
But we can simply see sensations that some kind of touch appears. And we treat them equally - without being attracted to them or rejecting them. And when some concepts appear in the mind, we go in reverse order: where it began, which ayatana worked, what was the object for it. It could be a sound, it could be some kind of touch, it could be a memory. Something arose in our minds, and we got hooked on it and started thinking about it. But in reality these are all delusions. If we see them as obscurations, then we understand that these are all objects of the mind. There was one memory, and after that I began to think. And these are all new kammas (karma) that I am now creating, these are new sankharas (willed formations of the mind). These forms that I am thinking about are not present in the outside world now, and this can be understood. And then we continue to observe the sensations of the body, practicing equanimity.
When we sit down to meditate and close our eyes, we experience the body. And we also have the world around us: people, the hall, Sri Lanka - an idea of ​​where we are. You can immediately think at the beginning that this is all Ц these are ideas of the mind, objects of the mind. With my eyes closed, I really feel only this hardness, the warmth that is here, sometimes I hear some sounds, subtle smells. This is if we look at what actually exists, in reality. And sometimes some thoughts appear and disappear - this is also normal. But if we begin to believe in them, that there are still other people sitting here meditating, and when some erroneous thoughts then appear in our minds, then we must understand that this is not in the external world - these are objects of the mind, but in the external world there are only elements. And by meditating, we can free ourselves from this. The Buddha says: УJust as the ocean has one taste: the taste of salt, so in the same way, this Teaching and Discipline has one taste: the taste of liberationФ (Uposatha, Udana 5.5).
How do we free ourselves? And how are we connected? What are we freeing ourselves from? I am connected to this mug, I am connected to this tea. I can close my eyes, but my mind still says that tea here is already connectedness. But we can free ourselves from everything. From the earth, from the sky, from people, birds, trees, from Buddha - from everyone. All of these are objects of the mind, so when we close our eyes, everything that comes to our minds is primarily the objects of the mind.
I saw a machine that runs old cars through, and it disassembles them into parts, into components. Vipassana is the same - through it we can disassemble the whole world into six components: the eye and the objects of the eye, the ear and the objects of the ear, the nose and the objects of the nose, the tongue and the objects of the tongue, the body and the objects of the body, the mind and the objects of the mind. Buddha called his Teaching vibhajavada : vibhajati is to disassemble, separate, analyze, and vada is teaching. Therefore, Vipassana teaches us to see the world differently. For example, everyone sees some bad person who has done something and is sent to prison. During the practice of Vipassana, a bad person also appears in our mind, but we immediately see that this is an object of the mind that causes defilements of the mind. And then we see there not an unwholesome person, but our own unwholesome mind. And therefore we can see the world differently, we can rely on reality - Vipassana teaches us this.
This is a very deep Dhamma, but still, when we start, we start from the very beginning - it's just observing. Observe our body, our sensations, what we hear, and so on. But if you work hard, you will definitely get results. Buddha gave an example with an egg: as if our world is an egg, and Buddha is the one who first punched a hole in the shell.

In the same way, Brahmana, in [this] generation, immersed in ignorance, becoming like a completely covered egg, I broke through the shell of ignorance. I am the only one in the world who has awakened to unsurpassed perfect enlightenment. That's why I'm the eldest, the best in the world.
