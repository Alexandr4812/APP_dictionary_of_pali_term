Dana, Sila, Bhavana Ц Three Kinds of Practice in Buddhism
Nyanasiha Bhikkhu

 
text revision: 14.05.2024

 

"Avoiding all evil, realizing good, purifying one's mind - this is the teaching of the Awakened Ones."

Buddhavagga: The Awakened One (Dhammapada 183)

Many people think that Buddhist practice is only meditation while sitting, but this is not true. Buddha distinguished three main types of practice: dana, sila, bhavana . Dana is giving, or generosity. Sila is morality, or correct behavior of body and speech. Bhavana is the development of the mind, which is divided in turn into two types: samadhi-bhavana - the development of concentration of the mind, and vipassana-bhavana - the development of wisdom. Usually the word "bhavana" is translated as meditation. In short, dana, sila, bhavana - this is Buddhist practice.
Another thing to say is that our life should be inseparable from Buddhist practice. It is wrong to think that we should only practice in the morning and evening for 20 minutes and then go about our daily life. Our life should become our practice. When we practice correctly, our lifestyle changes, and over time we will be able to look at ourselves and see the changes.
Buddha says that we should start with generosity, or giving (dana) . We help both ourselves and others with this practice. Giving is very widely explained in the Teaching. Buddha says that we received our human birth first of all thanks to giving: our human qualities are preserved by generosity. When a person succumbs to greed - there are no limits for greed, greed grows all the time. At first it is directed at some of one's things and possessions, at accumulation and preservation, and then it grows beyond them. At first it is lobha - greed, tanha - thirst, and then abhidjha arises - when we already begin to want someone else's. Such a person always lacks, he cannot be satisfied. He cannot see what he has, he sees only what he does not have. He is always directed at the world, he is always in search of something and this will actually only bring suffering, envy. Greed closes happiness and joy for us.
If it develops further, it leads to stealing, to wrong means of livelihood. Because of this, thieves, murderers appear in the world, because of this, wars take place. As greed develops, people will become either animals or hungry ghosts. Although they will be human during this life, they will live like animals (manussa-tiracchana) , or like hungry ghosts (manussa-peta) , or like demons (manussa-yakkha), always experiencing anger. What joy is there in such a life?
To live as humans, we first need generosity, giving. We must look at others, we must feel them, without this we will not be able to develop our human qualities.
There are also people in the world who live as deities (deva-manussa) : they have very few impurities of the mind, great kindness and compassion, they practice generosity, morality, they have a developed mind - in the world they are like devas, deities. We must look and choose for ourselves which path we want to go, who we want to become. If we were born human, then we must become human or deity, and generosity helps us with this.

"Endowed with these two things, a person is born in the heavenly world, as if he were drawn there by force. Which two? Virtuous conduct and virtuous views. Endowed with these two things, a person is born in the heavenly world, as if he were drawn there by force."

Dutiyasila Sutta: Conduct (II), Itivuttaka 2.6, Twos

We all understand that our human life is very short, we live here for a very short time. And all our property, all our loved ones Ц we will have to leave everything and go. And where we will go Ц we do not yet understand. But when we came to this human world, we actually did not come empty-handed, we brought with us our kammas that we committed in samsara. Buddha says that this is why people are different: some have health, some do not, some live a long time, some a short time, some have all their senses working, some only some, and so on. This is all because we brought our kammas here, these are their fruits. And when we leave Ц we will not leave empty-handed: we will take with us our kammas, what we did. Any kind of intentional action (no matter mental, verbal or physical) is kamma.

Monks, beings who are the owners of their own kamma, the heirs of their kamma, come from their kamma, kamma is their kin and refuge. Whatever kamma they do, good or bad, they will inherit it.

Samsappaniya Sutta: Sprawl, AN 10.216

"Kamma cannot be said to be stored up somewhere in this present consciousness or in any part of the body. However, dependent on the body and mind, it lies dormant, immediately manifesting itself when the opportunity arises, just as a mango that grows on a mango tree is not located somewhere inside the tree, but grows once a season under favorable circumstances."

(Milinda's Questions)

We must understand that we have a lot now, but what remains of it in the end? In the end, only what we have given to others remains. If you want to practice the Buddha's Teaching, you must always begin with generosity. Thanks to generosity, we receive good results in this life and in future lives.

"It is not because of perfection in kin, [not because of] perfection in wealth, or perfection in health that beings, with the disintegration of the body, after death, are reborn in good destinies, in the heavenly world. But because of perfection in moral conduct and [because of] perfection in views, beings, with the disintegration of the body, after death, are reborn in good destinies, in the heavenly world."

Sampada Sutta: Perfections, AN 5.130

The next practice is morality, sila . Morality is divided into two types: caritta-sila and viratta-sila . The latter is morality consisting of avoiding the unwholesome. First of all, this is the observance of the five vows (pancha-sila) :

1. A vow not to kill;
2. A vow not to take anything that is not given voluntarily;
3. A vow not to lie;
4. A vow not to commit sexual misconduct;
5. A vow not to consume alcohol or mind-numbing substances.

If a person observes these vows, then he develops morality. They establish the necessary basis for our spiritual development, without this we will not have enough peace of mind. And then we can expand our practice, including a more complex part: further work with speech, body and mind (not to speak rudely, not to gossip, work with unwholesome qualities of the mind, and so on).

"Monks, this Dhamma offers the means for avoiding. It is not that it has no means for avoiding. And how does this Dhamma offer the means for avoiding, and [why] is it not that it has no means for avoiding?
(1) For one who destroys life, there is abstinence from destroying life as a means for avoiding it. (2) For one who takes what has not been given, there is abstinence from taking what is not given, as a means for avoiding it. (3) For one who indulges in unwholesome sexual conduct, there is abstinence from unwholesome sexual conduct as a means for avoiding it. (4) For one who tells lies, there is abstinence from lying as a means for avoiding it. (5) For one who is divisive with his speech, there is abstinence from divisive speech as a means for avoiding it. (6) For one who speaks harshly, There is abstinence from harsh speech as a means to avoid it. (7) For one who chatters idle talk, there is abstinence from idle talk as a means to avoid it. (8) For one who is full of craving, there is non-craving as a means to avoid it. (9) For one who is full of ill will, there is good will as a means to avoid it. (10) For one who has wrong views, there is right view as a means to avoid it.
Thus, bhikkhus, this Dhamma provides a means for avoiding, and it is not that it has no means for avoiding."

Parikkamana Sutta: Avoiding, AN 10.175

Charitta-sila is the opposite of what we need to do to develop morality: these are our duties. For example, we need to clean the house, if there is work, we need to work honestly. We need to respect other people, we need to show respect and help our elders, parents, we need to take care of each other, take care of living beings, be sincere and compassionate, share and be generous. All this relates to human morality. This is explained in detail in the Sigalovada Sutta, DN 31 , one of the most important suttas of the canon, devoted to describing the criteria for the right life for lay people.
Human morality is necessary both for society and for ourselves. It is important to understand that we practice morality not out of fear of Buddha. Buddha is not a judge or a warden seeking to punish for wrongdoing. On the contrary, Buddha is our main friend and mentor. All the vows and precepts, all the teachings and instructions that he gave us - he gave us out of great compassion for us.
They are based on an understanding of the laws of the world, kamma, and are not intended to restrict or punish us, but to help us get rid of the suffering that arises from our ignorance and attachment, so that we do not harm ourselves because of our ignorance (avijja).
Thus, the practice of morality in the Buddha's teaching is not blind obedience, but a conscious choice aimed at personal development and understanding. The Buddha has provided us with tools for practice, vows, and teachings, and our following them is the path to liberation that we choose ourselves, guided by wisdom and compassion, and not by fear of external punishment.

[The Blessed One said:] (1) "Monks, abandon what is unwholesome! There is the possibility of abandoning what is unwholesome. If there were no possibility of abandoning what is unwholesome, I would not say, 'Monks, abandon what is unwholesome!' But precisely because there is the possibility of abandoning what is unwholesome, I say to you, 'Monks, abandon what is unwholesome!' If this abandoning of what is unwholesome were to lead to harm and suffering, I would not tell you to abandon it. But since the abandoning of what is unwholesome leads to well-being and happiness, I say to you, 'Monks, abandon what is unwholesome!'
(2) "Monks, develop what is wholesome! There is the possibility of developing what is wholesome. If there were no possibility of developing what is wholesome, I would not say, 'Monks, develop what is wholesome!' But precisely because there is the possibility of developing what is wholesome, I say to you, 'Monks, develop what is wholesome!' If this development of the good would lead to harm and suffering, I would not tell you to develop it. But since the development of the good leads to well-being and happiness, I say to you: "Monks, develop the good!"

Navama Sutta: Ninth, AN 2.19

If we observe the vows and develop morality, our mind will become calm, there will be no worries in the mind, regret will go away. It will be easy for us to practice meditation, bhavana , we will be able to develop our mind. Without morality, we will not be able to practice bhavana .

"Monks, just as all actions requiring effort are based on the earth, based on the earth, so in the same way, based on morality, based on morality, a monk develops the four foundations of mindfulness."

Bala Sutta: Effort, SN 47.73

"Monks, just as all actions requiring effort are based on the earth, based on the earth, so in the same way, based on morality, based on morality, a monk develops the seven factors of enlightenment."

Bala Sutta: Effort, SN 46.99

The next Buddhist practice is bhavana : samadhi-bhavana Ц the development of concentration and calmness of the mind, and vipassana-bhavana Ц the development of wisdom.
Samadhi-bhavana is the development of concentration, one-pointedness, and composure of the mind. There are various unwholesome qualities in the mind, and with the help of concentration and calming we suppress them and develop some good qualities over them. Then good states appear in the mind, but since the unwholesome qualities are not eradicated, they can arise again at any moment. This can be compared to grass hidden under a heavy stone: the stone hides the grass, but does not destroy it, and as soon as the stone is removed, the grass appears again. So samadhi-bhavana is a very important practice, but by itself it does not completely remove the impurities of the mind, does not eradicate them. But thanks to it, calmness and composure appear in the mind, and this in turn gives us the opportunity to practice vipassana, samadhi creates the basis for the practice of insight.
Samadhi-bhavana is not easy to practice in society, because we very easily and quickly lose the resulting calm and composure of mind, it is enough to remember some offense. It is described that various conditions are needed for the practice of samadhi: solitude, a suitable climate, and there must be a teacher. This is not just a recommendation, but a necessity, since practice without the guidance of a teacher can be not only ineffective, but also dangerous.
Vipassana bhavana is a meditation that helps us see the world as it is. To see our ignorance. It is the development of our wisdom. Passana means "vision", and the prefix "vi" gives the meaning that it is a special vision, based on a certain understanding, seeing as it is. This is what brings us to liberation.
The shortest description of vipassana given by the Buddha is: ditthe ditthamattan, sute suttamattan, mute muttamattan, vinyate vinyatetamattan Ц in the visible, only the visible, in the audible, only the audible, in the felt, only the felt, in the cognizable by the mind, only the cognizable by the mind (Bahiya Sutta: Bahiya, Ud 1.10 ). Bahiya, the man to whom the Buddha explained this, received enlightenment immediately after this instruction, so sharp was his mind, so wise was he. He is specifically mentioned in the canon as the man who received enlightenment the fastest. Our mind, our wisdom and virtue are not so well developed, so we need to practice hard, study a lot, listen to the Dhamma, reflect on it.
All Buddhist practices are closely connected and reinforce each other, forming a coordinated system aimed at achieving the ultimate goal of all our practice, namely, achieving liberation, complete purification of the mind, eradication of all unwholesome states of consciousness. Practice wisely, correctly, develop your good qualities, purify your mind. May the merits of studying this Dhamma help you meet good friends and free yourself from all suffering!

"In the same way, friend, the purification of morality [is necessary] for the sake of the purification of the mind. The purification of the mind is for the sake of the purification of the view. The purification of the view is for the sake of the purification by overcoming doubt. The purification by overcoming doubt is for the sake of the purification by knowledge and vision of what is the path and what is not the path. The purification by knowledge and vision of what is the path and what is not the path is for the sake of the purification by knowledge and vision of the path. The purification by knowledge and vision of the path is for the sake of the purification by knowledge and vision. The purification by knowledge and vision is for the sake of the attainment of final Nibbana without clinging. It is for the sake of final Nibbana without clinging that the holy life is led [under the teaching] of the Blessed One."

Rathavinita Sutta: The Transfiguration Chariots, MN 24
