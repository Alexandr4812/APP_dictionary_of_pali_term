Sankhara Loka - the world of mental formations
Nyanasiha Bhikkhu

 
translation revision: 02/01/2024

  “Where there is an eye, Samiddhi, where there are forms, the consciousness of the eye and things cognizable by the consciousness of the eye, there exists a world or a description of the world. Where there is an ear... nose... tongue... body... mind, where there are mental phenomena, consciousness of the mind and things cognizable by the consciousness of the mind - there is a world or a description of the world.

Where there is no eye, Samiddhi, no form, no consciousness of the eye, no things cognizable by the consciousness of the eye, there is neither the world nor any description of the world. Where there is no ear... nose... tongue... body... mind... things cognizable by the consciousness of the mind, there is neither the world nor any description of the world.”

Pathama Samiddhi Sutta: Samiddhi (IV), CH 35.68

Budda taught that the term "world" (loka) has three different meanings. First: okasa loka is the world of space, that is, the world of space-time reality, or, one might say, the astronomical world.
Second: satta-loka is the world of beings. This is the most clear thing for us: the three of us are sitting here now, discussing the Dhamma, we are in the same world. A dog is walking nearby, some insects are flying - they are all in another world. They don't know that Buddhists are sitting here, that they are talking with a monk, discussing the Dhamma. There are no temples, monks, or religions for them. All this that we have is not in their world. And there are a lot of such worlds. This is called satta-loka - the world of beings.
And third: sankhara-loka - the world of formations, formations of the mind. The Buddha explains in great detail exactly how the world comes into being, how the world exists, and how the world disappears. And even the reasons for the emergence of the world.
There is a sutta called Sammaditthi-sutta: Right View (MN 9), in which the Venerable Sariputta, one of the Buddha's two main disciples, explains in detail what samma-ditthi , right view, is. He says that if a person knows what the world is, the reasons for the emergence of the world, the cessation of the world and the Path leading to the cessation of the world, these are the correct views. If a person knows what a feeling (sensations) is, the causes of sensations, the cessation of sensations, the path leading to the cessation of sensations - these are correct views. And in this way he explains a lot: birth, existence, nutrition, aging and death, clinging, and not only that.
And there are suttas in which the Buddha explains the Four Noble Truths. The first Noble Truth is the truth about suffering, dukkha . Then - the truth about the cause of suffering, the truth about the possibility of ending suffering, and the last truth - the truth about the Path leading to the ending of suffering (Sanyutta Nikaya 45.8).
But in this Thera Sutta, Sariputta explains it differently: instead of “suffering,” he says “peace.” Thus, “the truth about suffering” becomes “the truth about the world,” “the cause of suffering” becomes “the cause of the world,” “the cessation of suffering” becomes “the cessation of the world,” and “the path leading to the cessation of suffering” becomes “the path leading to the end of the world." This means that the world is suffering.
Once the Buddha was asked what the world is. Buddha replied that the world is suffering. If a person understands what the world is, then he understands suffering. But he not only understands suffering, he must also understand the cessation of suffering.
Now let's see how Buddha explains the world. The world is 18 elements (dhatus) .
Five sense organs:

1. Eye element (cakkhu-dhatu).
2. Ear element (sota-dhatu).
3. Element of the nose (ghana-dhatu).
4. Element of language (jivha-dhatu).
5. Body element (kaya-dhatu).

Five objects (perceived through the five senses):

1. The element of color (rupa-dhatu).
2. The element of sound (sadda-dhatu).
3. Element of smell (ghana-dhatu).
4. Element of taste (rasa-dhatu).
5. The element of tangible objects (photthabba-dhatu).

Five elements of consciousness through the five senses:

1. The element of consciousness of the eye (cakkhu-vinnana-dhatu).
2. Element of ear consciousness (sota-vinnana-dhatu).
3. Element of smell consciousness (ghana-vinnana-dhatu).
4. The element of taste consciousness (jivha-vinnana-dhatu).
5. Body consciousness element (kaya-vinnana-dhatu).

And three additional elements:

1. The mind element (mano-dhatu).
2. The element of mental objects (dhamma-dhatu).
3. The element of mind consciousness (mano-vinnana-dhatu).

There are different classifications, which are also called elements, but now we will talk about these 18 elements.
In total, it turns out that the eye is one element, colors are another element, and the consciousness of the eye is another element. The task of the eye consciousness is to cognize colors and shapes, that’s all.
So it is with the ear, sounds, and the consciousness of the ear.
Language, taste, consciousness of language.
Nose, smells, consciousness of the nose.
Body, touch (or body objects), body consciousness.
Mind, objects of mind, consciousness of mind.
Total 18 elements. Let's now think, besides these 18 elements, does anything exist in the world?
There is also Nibbana, but Nibbana is not caused by causes. What are the reasons? There are 9 reasons.
The first three are: lobha - greed, dosa - ill will, moha - ignorance.
The following are: alobha - absence of greed (or generosity), adosa - absence of ill will (or kindness), amoha - absence of wrong views.
And there are three more, but they are also alobha , adosa and amoha . That is, we can have two types of generosity, kindness and correct views.
Let's see what the difference is using generosity as an example. Generosity is practiced in every religion, I don't know of any religion where generosity is not encouraged. But why should we practice generosity? In order to live well in this life, in the next life, or, as explained in other religions, in order to go to heaven. And this is the first type of generosity: it helps us in samsara, helps us get good fruits in samsara, in general, it helps us live better.
And the second type of generosity is when a person thinks: all defilements of the mind arise due to greed, due to thirst. Even when we die, no one says: “I had enough; enough is enough, I don’t want to see, hear, feel anymore.” Everyone wants to live, see, hear, feel, everyone wants to continue to perceive the world through the senses. And this is thirst, (tanha) , and because of this we are in samsara, in eternal wandering.
If a person understands: I need to overcome this thirst, otherwise samsara will win, and because of this he practices generosity, not with the goal of getting something later, but in order to defeat greed, if a person does dana this way , he will not have greed , there will be no pride. Why then does a person give? In order to achieve Nibbana, liberation from the eternal cycle of suffering and rebirth, this becomes his motivation in his practice of generosity.
These nine causes are not found in Nibbana. In Nibbana there is no generosity, no thirst, no greed. When there are no these reasons, then Nibbana. And then it is clear that Nibbana is not in the world, Nibbana is Supermundane. And our world is made up of 18 elements.
Let's now look at our world: we have 18 elements. Where then is Buddha, is he there, in these elements? We are now discussing your world, is there a Buddha in your world? If we answer that it is in the mind, then we need to understand that the mind is three elements: the mind itself, the objects of the mind and the consciousness of the mind. The task of the consciousness of the mind is to cognize; it cognizes the objects of the mind, thoughts. But we are not aware of the objects of the mind as objects of the mind, of the thoughts as thoughts. We accept them as reality. We perceive thoughts as Buddha, as Jesus, as “good”, as “bad”, as “monk”, as mine, as right and wrong, as reality. Then where is Buddha? In our thinking. And where is "I"? Also in our mind. This is all our thoughts.
Look how subtle and sublime the Teaching is. The Buddha says: see thinking as thinking - citte cittanupassi viharati , contemplate the mind as mind (Satipatthana Sutta: Foundations of Mindfulness, MN 10). If we see thinking as thinking, we do not see any permanent being there, no self, we do not see Buddha there.
For example, at night, if we hear loud sounds of drilling from our neighbors, then without knowledge of the Teaching we immediately have some kind of disgust in our mind, discontent, ill will: why so loud, why at such a time? The mind says something about this. How do we see these thinking? Like reality. We see “I” in these thinking, we see “other people”, and we do not see our ill will in the thinking. If we saw: this is anger, when it arises I feel bad, I can’t support it, this will make me feel bad too, if we saw this, we would immediately let go of this anger. And in fact, we don’t hear the person drilling - we hear sounds. Where is this person then? He's in the mind.
We can completely dismantle our world. You have your whole past life: you remember what school you had, what friends you had, what pleasant and unpleasant people you met, what enemies you had. And where are they all? They are all in the mind. But we believe they are real. After all, the mind actually cannot remember everything exactly 100%. And what does he do?
For example, today we went to the botanical garden. The mind cannot retain all the details of this day 100%, so it remembers what we think about this situation today, our attitude towards it. For example, someone may decide that today was a very bad day, we wanted to visit so many places, but it started to rain and everything changed, and then we didn’t go anywhere. But even if a person thinks so now, maybe a month will pass, the person will go to his homeland, and there, in another country, he will open photographs of how we went to the botanical garden and think: “It was so good, we all went to the botanical garden together.” garden, and then it started to rain, it was so beautiful, how good it was!” And then the memory of this situation changes, and this situation becomes good for him.
Look - the mind really knows nothing, and that means our past is just our current thoughts. We don't know what really happened! I can ask you: where were you on this day 10 years ago? Think about it now. We can try to remember and try to answer, but we cannot be sure. And in fact, this is all empty thinking.
Look, where is Russia? Usually we think that Russia exists, it is there, in such and such a direction. Or that she is on planet Earth. But if we break it down again as 18 elements, it's again in the mind. Now we claim that the Earth is round, and for us it is like that, this is our reality. And before that, people said and thought that it was flat, and all people had such an Earth in their minds! Just recently they began to say that it is not even round, but slightly flattened.
Or, for example, in Hinduism it is said that Brahma holds the Earth with his hand, and people in India imagined a huge Brahma. But now we think that the Earth is round, the Moon revolves around it. This is what our parents taught us, then at school and at university, and now we believe in it as if it were reality. But in reality we don’t know – it’s all our thoughts. Maybe it really is so, maybe not, it doesn’t really matter - these are all our thoughts.
For example, I think: “Max is here.” And this is actually my idea, this is what I think - Max is here. And what is Max like? He is the way I know him, the way I think about him. That's why people try to give other people a good idea of ​​them, because I don't only live in my mind, I live in your minds too. And I want me to be good in your minds. Therefore, every person wants to have a good impression of him. And even when a person dies, and understands that he is about to die, he still wants to exist. Where else could it exist? In other people's minds. That's why some people say: you'll die and no one will remember you. And it scares people - it’s as if I won’t exist at all.
Or I can ask: “Where is Sasha?” So you point him at me and touch him, but is this Sasha? These are the elements. Then where is Sasha? In the mind. And if Sasha is not here, then what can I see, what can I touch? What is this then? These are the elements.
As an example of another classification, there are primary elements, or great elements - mahabhutas , into which everything material is divided. Matter, or rupa , consists of four primary elements, or four "basic" physical phenomena: density (literally "earth"), cohesion (literally "water"), heat (literally "fire") and motion (literally "air").
The element of tangible objects or body objects (photthabba-dhatu) includes the following three mahabhutas :

1. The earth element or hardness (pathavi-dhatu) , manifesting as hardness or softness.
2. The element of fire, or temperature (tejo-dhatu) , manifesting itself as heat or cold.
3. The element of wind (vayo-dhatu) , manifested as movement or pressure.

The element of water, or cohesion (apo-dhatu) , we cannot feel physically - for us it will still be hardness, warmth and airiness, so it is not included in the objects of the body. It is available to us only as a result of analysis.
Even if we analyze one element, it will give great, great wisdom. Buddha says: one who knows hardness ( pathavi , earth element) can know that hardness is not the “I”, and can escape the element of hardness, and can escape the world. The Buddha explains this in great detail:

“Both the internal and external elements of the earth are only the element of the earth. And it should be seen with correct wisdom in accordance with reality: “This is not mine, I am not like this, this is not my self.” When one sees it in accordance with the reality of correct wisdom, then he loses his fascination with the earth element and makes the mind impartial towards the earth element.
And a time comes when the outer water element is disturbed, and at that time the outer earth element disappears. So, even in relation to such a vast outer. of the earth element one can see impermanence, destruction, disappearance, change, so what then in this short-lived body, clinging to thirst, can be “I”, “mine”, “what I am” Nothing?

(Mahahatthipadopama-sutta: Great example with elephant tracks, MN 28)

And we touch the elements, feel the hardness, and I think - this is Max. I see some colors and some image appears in my mind, and my mind says: “This is Max, this is Sasha, this is Pasha.” This is the mind speaking.
If we analyze our world this way, then in order for our world to exist, we need concepts and elements, nama and rupa (namarupa) .

"Feeling, perception, volition, contact, attention - this is called mind. The four great elements, as well as the form built from the four great elements - this is called form. Thus, this mind and this form is what is called mind -and-form."

(Sammaditthi Sutta: Right View, Majjhima Nikaya 9)

Without nama and rupa there will be no reality, no world. For our presentation to be real, we need some elements.
Think about your school. For this memory to become a reality for you, you think: the school is located in Russia, on a certain street, in a specific building, in a certain territory. And then your thinking becomes reality. But if we see it as elements, as thinking, then we see that this is not reality, but thoughts. And seeing this way, we are freed from defilements. We compare our ideas, our concepts that arise in our minds with some elements that we think exist in the external world, and we think: “Yes, this is real.” And what do we think is real? Our thoughts and concepts are what they exist in the world. But in the world there are only elements.
Let's say there is one fish. She has two legs. And two hands. And the head. And she can walk. And she can talk. And even drive! Look, when we say this, the mind does not perceive it as something real. And why doesn't he perceive? He doesn’t know this, he doesn’t know how to drive in the water, what kind of cars there are, there’s nothing like that in our experience. When we say “a fish and it has two legs,” the mind begins to imagine an ocean and some fish in it. When we get to the point that she can talk and drive, the mind already believes that this is unrealistic. And when it is unrealistic, we are no longer afraid, because we know that it is just imagination, an idea.
So we watch cartoons, and we know that they are unreal, and after watching we have no idea in our heads that something like this actually exists, unlike watching the news. But at the same time, the characters from these cartoons live in our minds. Remember, a person saw a drawing from a cartoon and immediately said: “Oooh, that’s Totoro!” Where's Totoro? Is it on the arm as a tattoo, or on paper? No, it's in someone's head! Someone invented it, drew it, and now these Totoros live in the head of every person who has seen this cartoon. They see and recognize - “oh, Totoro”! We do the same with other people: we see a person and say “he is my friend!” How is it? How is this a friend? How did the friend come about? Or another person is my enemy, and I feel unpleasant. How is it? We actually think that these thoughts of ours are out there in the world. I think: “Here he is, my Max, here he sits here.” This is called upadana , attachment.
When we say "attachment", we think it is a very easy concept. Attachment to the phone is when I can’t live without a phone, that’s attachment.
Can you imagine the attachment to ants? If a person has not achieved arhatship, then he has attachment to ants. To all the plants he knows, to his school. Why is that? Because we take our concepts that are in our minds as reality. When our concepts become reality for us, this is upadana , because we have some kind of attitude towards all this. And these are real objects for us that exist in the world for us, and they can change, break, die, and all this affects us, all this is significant for us. If we can understand at least a little what reality actually is, and what a deception of the mind is, this is already a kind of wisdom, this already gives us some kind of freedom.
When we compare our mental khandhas (formations or structures) with external elements, with some rupa , we say: “This is that person, and this is a tree, and these are ants,” then this is already upadana , attachment. Or when we see a cat, it appears in our mind and we think that it is out there somewhere in the world. And we tie it to the outside world. And we begin to perceive some elements as a cat. If I say that it is bad, then a bad cat appears, if I say that it is good, a good cat appears. But we do not see the emergence and disappearance of the world. Existence (bhava) appears for us . In dependent arising, after attachment, existence arises next: upadanapachaya bhavo - from clinging, existence arises as a necessary condition.
Here's a good example with a tattoo on the arm. Look, we look at a hand with a tattoo, and we see a face there, or any other picture. But when we see a picture, we do not see a hand, when we see a hand, we do not see a picture. Or when we look at a wall with a beautiful design, for example, a pond with lotuses: we either see the wall itself, and then we do not see the lotuses and the pond, or we see the pond, and then we do not see the wall.
Imagine if someone made a statue in the shape of a body, and very carefully applied paints to it so that it looked exactly like the body. Without knowing that it is a statue, we may look at it and mistake it for a person. But it doesn't matter whether we look at a statue or at a person - in fact, we do not see a body in either one or there: we see "drawings" of different colors. But in reality, we only see drawings all the time, and we think that there are people there, that they are doing something. But in reality we see elements, colors. Vision occurs in the eye: the eye perceives color, and through the consciousness of the eye, cognition of this color occurs. But when we think that what we see with the eye is somewhere in front of us, then the idea arises in the mind that these colors are wood, people, statues, ants, and so on. And then trees, people and the whole world instantly appear in our minds. Although in fact the eye only perceives colors.
First of all, we need to purify our views. If the views become correct, we will feel much better, much easier, because a huge amount of our suffering (one might say almost all of it) is due to ignorance. We invent it for ourselves, we create it ourselves, and we ourselves live in this prison. Even if our ignorance decreases even a little, our life will become much better. And we don't need to look for happiness somewhere in the world. If we have no suffering, this is happiness, this is peace. Such happiness is always available to us; we do not need to look for it in the outside world. Then, even when there is physical pain, our mind may not suffer, because physical pain and mental suffering are different things. We want to find some other happiness, and this is our mistake. While we are looking for it, even the body will feel tense, and if there is pain, it will increase. When we have thirst, we never have enough, and we cannot be calm, we cannot stop, we cannot relax.
Try to observe the elements as elements, this will give you some understanding. If at least once we see elements as elements, our world will change.
Buddha called his Teaching vibhajavada: vibhajati is to disassemble, separate, analyze, and vada is teaching. And that's what we do - we take everything apart. To gain understanding, you can divide your world into 18 elements. If you practice this way, then you can sort out any of your suffering, any problem. And you can see and understand where the problem actually is: it is that the problems that exist in our mind, we see as problems in the external world, and we want to solve them in the external world, we want to do something about them . But if we see thinking as thinking, they disappear. And of course: almost all problems are in our minds. Why do we solve them in the outside world?
Here's an example of what elements are and how to see elements as elements. Close your eyes. How are you feeling now? You feel that other people are here, we are all sitting here, there are other people around, nature. Where is all this really? If you see it in your mind, then you can let it go, because it is all just a mental image. Apart from the sensations of the body, we do not feel anything, and we actually do not know anything.
Imagine that you are now in space. And under our feet there is a spaceship, around us is space. And all these sounds are actually a recording being played from the speakers. Try to imagine yourself on this spaceship.
How did you feel when you imagined that? If you manage to imagine a spaceship and that we are sitting in it, the Earth immediately disappears and space appears around us. And if we imagine that all these sounds are actually from speakers and are played on a computer, then all these people and animals - they immediately disappear for us, they are not there. Or if we turn on an audio file with the sounds of rain, we immediately imagine rain. If we turn on an audio file with frogs croaking, we hear every sound and immediately imagine a frog. But in reality there is no rain or frogs near us.
You need to assimilate this knowledge, remember it, reflect on it. You need to form your own understanding. We must try to see the world this way. This practice provides refuge: a person will always have a way out, even in the most insoluble life situation. If you see the elements, there are no problems in them. One sees the mind, sees the objects of the mind, colors, sounds - just elements, and there is no suffering in them. These are just our ideas about reality, these are just our thoughts. We imagine this ourselves and suffer from it ourselves.
Tell me, without remembering any past, what would you like in the future? This is impossible. So we can understand that memories are our thoughts. Yesterday is about memories and how we feel about it. These are thoughts arising right now. We can see thoughts as thoughts, as objects of the mind.
Think about it, try to parse your world into 18 elements. And you will either have a lot of questions, or you will not have any more questions - all questions will disappear. With this knowledge, we understand our world through these 18 elements. And we see how they arise and how they disappear.
May the wisdom you gain from reading this lecture help you free yourself from all suffering!

He is incapable of committing any action , due to which he could be reborn in hell, in the animal world or in the world of suffering spirits. He cannot die without realizing the fruit of stream entry.
He who, having thought sufficiently through wisdom, has agreed with these teachings is called. "walking-by-the-Dhamma." He has entered the solid path of correctness, entered the world of superior people, left the world of ordinary people. He is incapable of committing any act that could cause him to be reborn in hell, in the world. animals or in the world of suffering spirits. He cannot die without realizing the fruit of entering the stream.
He who knows and sees these teachings in this way is called “one who has entered the stream,” he is adamant, he will never again be able to find himself in the lower worlds, he is heading towards. enlightenment."

(Dhatu Sutta: Elements, Sanyutta Nikaya 25.9)
