Vajira Sutta: Vajira , SN 5.10

From today on I want to explain to you what suffering is. As Buddha explains it:

Birth is suffering, aging is suffering, illness is suffering, death is suffering, association with the unpleasant is suffering, separation from the pleasant is suffering, not getting what one wants is suffering.

Dhammacakka Pavattana Sutta: The Turning of the Wheel of Dhamma, Samyutta Nikaya 56.11


And then Buddha says: in short, the five aggregates subject to clinging are suffering. That is, suffering is attachment to the five khandhas. Understanding the five khandhas is very important for Buddhist practice.


The five khandhas , or panchakhandhas, are:

1. Rupa khandha , the aggregate of form.
2. Vedana khandha , the aggregate of sensations.
3. Sannya khandha , the aggregate of mental perceptions.
4. Sankhara khandha , the aggregate of mental formations.
5. Vinnana khandha , the aggregate of consciousness.

We can roughly divide almost all people in the world into two groups. Some people believe that we were born in a world where everything exists: people, animals, sky, earth, countries, nations and everything-everything-everything. It existed before us, we were born here, and without us it will continue to exist. Other people believe that all this happens in the mind: as if everything we are aware of and perceive is an illusion, and without us nothing exists; there is nothing except the mind, all this is only in the mind, in consciousness ( vinnanavada ).
Buddha rejected both of these views. Buddha explained absolute reality differently, and to understand these explanations we need knowledge of the five khandhas . Let us examine what the five aggregates are.


Rupa Khandha

The first is rupa khandha , the aggregate of form. What is rupa , form: it is matter, or material elements. Buddha explained that there are four material elements, or four great elements - satara maha bhuta :

1. The earth element, or hardness ( pathavi-dhatu ), manifesting as hardness or softness.
2. The fire element, or temperature ( tejo-dhatu ), manifesting as heat or cold, temperature.
3. The wind element ( vayo-dhatu ), manifesting as movement or pressure.
4. The water element, or cohesion, fluidity ( apo-dhatu ).

These elements should not be understood literally as earth, fire, wind and water. This is not the earth we walk on: such earth contains all four elements.
Buddha says that there is no creator of the great elements, and that they are beginningless. They are constantly changing - they do not have any one form. For example, hardness decreases, fluidity increases, then airiness increases, and so on. For example, when ice melts, the fire element increases (i.e., the temperature increases), the earth element decreases (hardness decreases), and the water element increases (fluidity). These elements are like energy - they are constantly changing, and they are present everywhere. Also, these elements are never separate - all four elements always coexist together, and it is impossible to separate them. If we take any smallest particle of matter, it will always contain all four great primary elements.
The Buddha describes that where there are four great primary elements, there will be four more dependent elements, the falling rupa :

1. Varna , color.
2. Ghanda , smell.
3. Race , taste.
4. Oja , nutritional value.

These four dependent elements are always there when there are four great primary elements, they are always together. This is all the aggregate of materiality, or the aggregate of form - rupa khadha .
Many people think that the totality of form is our body, but this is not entirely correct. The totality of form is also the objects of our sense organs. These are the objects of the eye, ear, nose, tongue and body.
To explain it differently, the totality of the form is:

1. Eye and eye objects (colors).
2. Ear and ear objects (sounds).
3. Nose and nose objects (smells).
4. Tongue and tongue objects (tastes).
5. Body and body objects (touch).

This is all - this is rupa .
When we think "materiality", "matter", we immediately think that all objects in the world are matter: buildings, trees, earth, rivers, oceans and so on. But in the Buddha's Teaching this is not so and to understand this we need to look at where it all arises. This is exactly what is explained through panchakhandha .
When we say, "I see a person," we think, there is a person there, his body consists of four elements, and he has a mind. But in reality, when I see a person, the eye sees only colors. Buddha teaches that the eye perceives varna rupa , colors. The eye perceives nothing except colors, so in reality panchakhandha arises here. But in panchakhandha, form is the eye and the objects of the eye. The objects of the eye are not the person, the objects of the eye are colors, and colors are materiality in the Buddha's Teaching. Everything else is the mind, it is my idea: that there is a person there, his body consists of elements, he is good or bad, what religion he follows, where he is from - these are all my ideas, this is nama (mind). And materiality, rupa , is the eye and the objects of the eye.
Let's look at the ear and the objects of the ear. The objects of the ear are only sounds. But we say, for example, "I hear a car." Then I can think that the car is not alive, the car is something material. But when I think like that, then for me there is an external world and in the external world there is a car. If we want to see the real world, to see reality, then we must understand: the ear and the objects of the ear are materiality ( rupa ), and everything else is mind ( nama ).
Buddha even gave us a meditation to see the elements as elements. He teaches: we should see the inner elements and the outer elements simply as elements. There is inner hardness, such as hair, nails, teeth, bones and so on, and there is outer hardness, but there is no difference between them - hardness is hardness. There is inner fluidity, or water, such as blood, mucus, sweat, saliva and so on, and there is outer water. But the quality of the water is the same, there is no difference. You cannot say that the inner water is me and the outer water is you, this view arises from ignorance, in reality there are simply elements.


Vedana Khadha

The next aggregate is vedana khandha . Vedana is sensation, khandha is a mountain, a heap, an aggregate. And what is vedana , sensation? Buddha teaches that there are three kinds of sensations: pleasant, unpleasant and neutral. They arise through contact ( phassa ). When I see, hear, smell, taste, touch - through contact there is sensation. Even when we think, thoughts create sensations. In the mind there is sadness, joy - these are also mental sensations.


Sannya Khandha

The third aggregate is sannya khandha , the aggregate of perception. When we hear something, we perceive these sounds, when we see, we perceive colors, and so on. The mind constantly perceives objects through the senses. This is the most basic recognition of objects that we perceive, without judgment and reflection. These perceptions arise one after another very quickly, at any moment there are many of them, so they are called khandha : heap, mountain, aggregates.


Sankhara khandha

The fourth aggregate is sankhara khandha , the formations of the mind, or the volitional formations of the mind. Sankharas are our thoughts. Every time we perceive something, some formations appear in the mind. Somehow the mind perceives it all, and then creates some thoughts about it, gives some assessment, thinks about it. These thoughts are sankhara , or we can say it differently: these are our greed, anger, generosity, kindness, compassion, wisdom, stupidity - these are all sankhara .


Vinnana Khandha

The fifth aggregate is vinnana khandha , the aggregate of consciousness. Usually when we say "consciousness" and "mind" - these words are synonymous for us. In Pali there are also two words for them, two separate concepts, vinnana is consciousness, mano is mind, but they are not synonymous, they are two different concepts.
And what is consciousness, viññana ? The task of consciousness is to cognize, to make something accessible to our mind. And what does consciousness cognize? Whatever arises in the mind, it cognizes. For example, walking at night, we can see a rope lying on the path and take it for a snake. The mind object "snake" immediately arises in the mind and consciousness cognizes the snake. Then we can shine a flashlight there and see that it was a rope: the mind object "rope" arises and consciousness cognizes the rope. The mind, mano , can think logically and understand that we were mistaken: that in fact there was no snake and all this time there was a rope. But these are objects of the mind, this is how thinking works. Consciousness, viññana , does not work this way - consciousness only cognizes what has arisen. This is called the aggregate of consciousness.
Many people ask where consciousness is, but from the point of view of the Teaching, this is the wrong question, because it arises and disappears. It arises on the basis of the eye, ear, nose, tongue, body and mind, so we say eye consciousness, ear consciousness, nose consciousness, tongue consciousness, body consciousness and mind consciousness. Look at yourself carefully, how many consciousnesses arise in us now. We simultaneously understand where we are sitting now, and we see, hear, we feel the body. But the body is not one consciousness: in order to feel the body, myriads of consciousnesses arise. If we observe carefully and consciously, we can see that different sensations arise in the body all the time, and thanks to these sensations I feel the body, I perceive the body.
The same with vision: in fact, we perceive a constant flow of pictures, which means that the eye consciousness constantly arises and disappears. If we look at a tree and see the leaves on it separately, then separate consciousnesses arise so that we can perceive the leaves separately - the mind works so quickly. And the same with the other senses. Imagine how many consciousnesses are arising now. That is why we say that this is a mountain of consciousnesses. Khandha is a mountain, a heap, a pile.

And what, monks, is the aggregate of form? The four great elements (maha-bhuta) and the body based on these elements are called the aggregate of form. And what, monks, is the aggregate of feeling? There are six categories of feeling: eye-contact, ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, consciousness-contact... And what, monks, is the aggregate of perception? There are six categories of perception: perception of forms, perception of sounds, perception of tastes, perception of odors, perception of bodily sensations, perception of objects of consciousness. And what, monks, is the aggregate of mental formations? There are six volitions (cetana): volition for forms, volition for sounds, volition for odors, volition for tastes, volition for bodily sensations, volition for objects of consciousness. And what is the aggregate of consciousness? There are six categories of consciousness: eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, mind-consciousness.

Upādana Paripavatta Sutta: The Phases of the Aggregates Subject to Clinging, SN 22.56

All these five aggregates, panchakhandhas , they are just like the four great primary elements - they always arise together, they are inseparable. We cannot perceive them in any other way, for example only form, or only feeling. They arise simultaneously and immediately disappear, then the next panchakhandhas immediately arise , and also disappear. If a person can see how panchakhandhas arise and how panchakhandhas disappear , then he has vipassana nyanana , the knowledge of insight. He begins to see the world differently.

Feeling, perception, consciousness, friend: these qualities are united, not separated. It is not possible to separate one from the other and make a distinction between them. What one feels, one perceives. What one perceives, one knows. Thus these qualities are united, not separated, and it is not possible to separate one from the other and make a distinction between them.

Mahavedalla Sutta: The Great Collection of Questions and Answers, MN 43

If one practices vipassana, one's goal should be to see the arising and disappearance of khandhas . This is the vision of impermanence, the real vision of impermanence. Even without vipassana we can say that the world is impermanent: someday none of this will exist, everything will collapse, the trees will dry up and disappear, but this is not the vision of impermanence, not the vision of reality as it is - these are just our thoughts. To see reality as it is, to understand the world, one must observe panchakhandhas .
How do we use this knowledge, what do we do with all this? How do we put it into practice? The purpose of the Teaching is to be free from suffering, so we need to use this information for liberation. As I said, the five aggregates arise simultaneously, let's see how they arise.
Let's take the ear as an example and see how the five aggregates arise through the ear. Sound is materiality, rupa . Some sound enters the ear, ear consciousness arises, it perceives the sound. Apart from this, ear consciousness cannot do anything, it only perceives the sound and then disappears. Then the consciousness of the mind arises in order to understand what kind of sound it is. Recognition of this sound occurs through memory, through one's ideas, or asavas - knowledge with contamination of the mind. In this way, recognition of sound occurs, and for us immediately something good, bad or neutral appears in the external world. A person, a dog, a cat and so on appear in the mind - this is how the mind perceives. A sensation and perception ("cat", "dog") appear in the mind. And in order to somehow evaluate and perceive this, sankhara appears in the mind - some subtle formation, and vinnana appears - consciousness. Here consciousness is the consciousness of the mind, which is aware of a dog or a cat. If the formation was "cat", then consciousness is aware of the cat. This is how panchakhandhas appeared .
But the panchakhandhas themselves are not suffering. Buddha says: the five aggregates subject to clinging are suffering. There must be upadana , attachment, clinging, for suffering to arise.
How do we understand attachment correctly? What do we attach it to? Usually when we say "attachment", we mean that we want something very much, we hold on to it. But there is more than just this kind of attachment.
So, let's say, when a cat appears in our mind, then we think about this cat that it is there, in the external world. And we "attach" it to the external world, we begin to think and we begin to perceive some external elements as a dog, as a cat: we compare our ideas with external elements. And what happens after that? For us, a cat appears there. If I say that it is good, then a good cat appears. If I say that it is bad, then a bad cat appears. And we do not see arising and passing away. So, existence appears for us. Why? If we take paticca-samuppada , interdependent arising, then there is upadana (attachment). If there is attachment, then existence will be next: with clinging as a condition, existence [arises]. This means existence appears.
Suppose now you can think about Thailand, Sri Lanka, America. And we have perception, we have some thoughts. And for us these thoughts are not just something that appears in the mind. We begin to see it in the outside world as reality. This means that everything that is in our mind, we begin to see in the outside world. This is how our world appears, and this is how our suffering appeared for us.
This is a very difficult topic, but you can observe yourself, study yourself and think about this knowledge. We study ourselves in order to free ourselves. Free ourselves from what? From everything. From a cat, from a dog, from people, from countries, from the earth and the sky - we can free ourselves from everything. And why? Because all this arises in our mind. And why does all this arise in our mind? Because of ignorance. We do not see what is actually happening in reality.
Every time we hear something, we have panchakhandhis in our mind : some perceptions, formations, feelings, etc., and we start seeing it in the external world. And what happens in reality? In reality, all the time, either the eye is working, or the ear is working, or the tongue is working, or the nose is working, or the body is working. Or the mind is working. The mind connects all these organs and creates the real world for us.
The eye perceives colors, there is nothing else for the eye except colors. The body perceives only touches and nothing else. If a blind person touches something, he also feels something. But he cannot touch the beauty that we see with our eyes. Even if we show him something very beautiful for us, he will not be able to touch it, because when he touches it, he will feel only hardness, airiness, temperature. But if we give him a precious stone and explain to him that it is a jewel, then in his sensations for him there will be not only hardness, pressure and temperature - for him it will be a touch of a jewel. And what is a jewel? It is his perception, it is his formation of the mind, it is his consciousness. But in reality there is hardness, temperature, pressure - this is reality.
So we begin to see how it happens. We see colors, we touch temperature, hardness, airiness, and the mind says, "You saw a cat, you touched a cat." Do you see where delusion arises? In the mind. We can see where delusion arises, greed, anger. Anger, greed - they arise in my mind, they do not belong to an external object, that is what we must understand. If a person understands this, then he has some level of awareness and he can observe himself, because without this he does not see his thoughts. He does not see his thoughts - he sees other people in them, good and bad, and he does not see his anger and disgust, greed.
If we begin to see: this is anger, these are thoughts, these are sankharas , then this is already some level of awareness. To observe like this, we must somehow see the real world, for this we can develop awareness. For example, during meditation you can be aware, alert. There was a sound and after that a person appeared in the mind, then some disgust appeared, and we can think in reverse order what actually happened. A sound was heard, it disappeared long ago, and a person appeared in the mind. Who does it belong to then, where does it exist? It arose in my mind, this is my formation of the mind, and in the real world elements arise and disappear. They arise when we listen, see, touch, smell, taste, think. This is how we can observe.
Sounds never appear to us as just sounds. It is as if we already have a program, and it is always working for us. We cannot help but hear words when we hear the sounds of speech, because we already know these sounds, and as soon as we hear - together with these sounds we begin to perceive speech. Buddha taught that first of all we must see our clinging, upadana . If a person can understand at least a little what upadana is , then this is already the development of the mind. Upadana first of all creates sakkaya ditthi , false views, a false understanding of the "I". Therefore, we can develop awareness and wisdom, and practice using this knowledge, forming the right views in ourselves.
I wish you awareness and wisdom so that you can see the world as it is. I wish you good practice, and may the merit of listening to this Dhamma help you to be liberated!
