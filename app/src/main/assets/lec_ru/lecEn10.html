  “Impermanent are compounded things,
Now appearing, now disappearing,
Having arisen, they soon cease:
But their pacification is blissful.”

Parinibbana Sutta: Final Nibbana, SN 6.15

Impermanent are all conditioned things when they are seen with wisdom

Maggavagga: The Path, Dhammapada 277

C ankhara is one of the main terms in the Buddha's Teaching, which has three meanings depending on the context.
All sankharas are anicca , impermanent: " anicca vata sankhara " - compound things are impermanent, sabbe sankhara anicca ti - all conditioned things are impermanent. In this sense, sankharas are what has arisen from causes. What arises from causes? Everything except nibbana arises from causes. Or, we can say, everything that is in samsara arises from causes. The body and the mind, everything that we see, hear, smell, touch, taste, know - these are all sankharas, they all arise from causes, they are all impermanent. All things, all mental factors - these are all sankharas.
Sabbe sankhara anicca ti - all sankharas are impermanent, yada pannaya passati - if one knows this through wisdom, as it is, then this is the path to liberation. This is the first meaning for sankharas - that which arises from causes. Everything that is in samsara is sankharas.
The next meaning is the meaning in the context of pancakkhandhas . The five khandhas , or pancakkhandhas , are:

1. Rupakkhandha , the aggregate of form.
2. Vedanakkhandha , the aggregate of sensations.
3. Sannyakkhandha , the aggregate of perceptions of the mind.
4. Sankharakkhandha , the aggregate of formations of the mind.
5. Viññanakhandha , the aggregate of consciousness.

In this context, sankharas are volitional formations of the mind. In order to understand information from our sense organs, some formations arise in the mind (or we can also translate sankharas as fabrications, definitions).
Sankharas are part of the experience of every being, including Buddhas and Arahants. But for us there is good and bad, and for the enlightened there is no difference - there are just sankharas.
The following definition in the context of paticca-samuppada , dependent origination: avijjapaccaya sankhara ; sankharapaccaya vinñanan . Avijja is ignorance. Avijjapaccaya sankhara – due to ignorance, sankharas arise. Sankharapaccaya vinñanan : due to sankharas, consciousness arises.
In this context, there are three types of sankhara: punnabhisankhara, apunnabhisankhara, anenjabhisankhara . Punnabhisankhara is wholesome kamma or wholesome thoughts of the mind, apunnabhisankhara is unwholesome. Anenjabhisankhara is kamma for high levels of samadhi (concentration), not for our world of kama-loka (the world of the senses). Whatever we think about in the world, it will all be kusala or akusala abhisankharan .
Today I want to talk about sankharas in the context of dependent origination. My goal is to give you some knowledge, some understanding that you can see through your experience, that you can use in your meditation, and that will help you to understand yourself deeply.
How do you think we can see the sankharas that arise from ignorance? It is our internal conversation, or our thinking. Sometimes sankharas appear as some pictures in the mind, as ideas: for example, we are angry and we imagine something. Or if we have been criticized, we begin to hear these words in our mind, as if it is happening right now. All these pictures, ideas, words in our mind – these are all our sankharas.
Usually sankharas look like an internal conversation. If a person wants to see himself, he must see his internal conversation, his mind, his thoughts. There is no other system for seeing yourself. Let's say I start observing myself, and the mind starts saying: "Look, how you are sitting, look what you have done, this is who you are, you are good, you are bad." The mind speaks and we observe. And these thoughts that occur in the mind are "I", atta. This is where "I" appears. But the Teaching says that there is no atta , anatta . In order to begin to understand this at least a little, we need to see ourselves.
If we want to see ourselves, we need to go back and see what the mind says. But while we are talking, while we are constantly busy with things, we have no time to look at ourselves. To look at ourselves, we need to leave all other things. While we are reading, listening, looking at the outside world, we will not have vision of ourselves. When we are silent and observe, in this silence we begin to see ourselves.
For those who meditate, who participate in retreat, the most difficult thing for them is often to remain silent. They think that the most difficult thing is to sit, but then they realize that it is even more difficult to remain silent. When people are silent, their minds become audible to them, and the mind becomes very loud, so they see their suffering. When we see our suffering, we want to stop it. Everyone wants to stop their inner suffering at least a little, and we all have our own methods for this.
How do we usually cope with them? We distract ourselves. We either look at something, or listen, or talk. In the past, before gadgets and the Internet, people bought newspapers and books and read them constantly. Even earlier, people just walked outside and talked - these conversations distracted them from suffering. Therefore, people constantly need to do something, if people do nothing - their suffering increases. Let's say a grandmother is sitting at home, she has nothing to do. But what does she do? She sews something, knits, occupies herself with something. When she does this, she may not listen to her inner conversation. It is still audible, but this way she is at least a little distracted. This inner voice is suffering.

The Buddha's Teaching explains three types of suffering:

1. Dukkha dukkha ;
2. Viparinama dukkha ;
3. Sankhara dukkha .

Dukkha dukkha is painful sensations in the body: headache, stomach ache, pain in the legs and back when sitting, and so on. In fact, we almost always have this suffering, we are always a little cold or hot, a little uncomfortable, and so on.
Viparinama dukkha is the suffering of change. For example, we cannot just eat once, we then want to eat again. Why do we constantly have to eat, wash, brush our teeth, get treatment? Because everything is constantly changing. The body is constantly changing, we need to constantly maintain it - this is suffering. Imagine how much food we have eaten in this life alone. And we have to work endlessly, clean endlessly, cook endlessly, repair our things endlessly, treat our body endlessly. Because of the changes, we are constantly busy and this is suffering.
These first two types of suffering seem great to us, it seems to us that there are many of them, but in reality their ratio is insignificant in relation to the third type of suffering. Most of all people suffer not from the body and not from changes, most of all people suffer from the mind - this is sankhara dukkha .
For example, some medical procedures: let's say a tooth needs to be removed. A person can suffer from the very moment he finds out that he is going to have to have a tooth removed. The tooth extraction itself and the pain during the process are one thing, but people can think about it for a very long time, be very afraid and suffer. These thoughts, these sankharas affect the body, and physical pain increases from this, and illnesses can increase from this. This is sankhara dukkha , the main type of suffering for us.
For example, aging is viparinama dukkha : the body becomes weak, there are more diseases, hair falls out, the sense organs become weak. But much greater suffering will be in the mind from thinking about this. "I am becoming ugly, I am weak, how can I prevent my hair from turning grey, from falling out" - these sankharas arising in the mind bring great suffering. And what will happen next, according to dependent arising: Sankharapaccaya vinñanan - due to sankharas consciousness arises. Whatever the mind thinks, whatever sankharas arise in the mind, such will be the consciousness, vinñan .
For example, it was cold in the morning, the mind learns about it. And it is not just cold, the thinking begins: "Oh, how cold, how bad." After that, the mind realizes that everything is bad. As a result, the whole body changes, begins to correspond to the mind, we begin to feel the world differently. And the mind also changes: all mental factors change.
Vinññapaccaya namarupan : due to consciousness, mind and body, or mentality-materiality (namarupa), arise. Namarupapaccaya salayatanan : due to mentality-materiality, the six spheres of perception, or the six sense organs (salayatana), arise. After the mind, the six sense organs change and we become ready to see the bad world, the cold world.
For example, at the end of autumn someone says: "The first snow is coming soon." People usually perceive this joyfully: that soon there will be no mud, everything will be white and beautiful, the holidays are coming soon. But for a poor person who has no heating at home, or for a person who is allergic to cold - for him it will be suffering. His internal sankharas will be different, and what sankharas are - such will be the consciousness (vinnana) , such will be the mind (namarupa). As the mind, such will be the sense organs, or the six spheres of perception (salayatana).
In reality, almost all of a person's suffering is his mental suffering, sankhara dukkha . Because of our inner suffering, we do not see the happiness that we already have now. For example, now we are comfortable and it is pleasant, but usually people cannot feel it. When we open the window and feel the fresh breeze - it is pleasant. When we pour tea - its smell and taste are pleasant. But we do not feel this pleasantness because of our inner suffering: there is an endless internal conversation in the mind. What is this conversation about, if you look at it carefully, what is it? It is our ignorance, it is our thirst, it is our aversion.
In the Buddha's teaching it is explained that we are sugati : gati is birth, su is good. We are beings who have received a good birth. We have happiness here, but we do not see it. With our minds we constantly suffer. If a person is truly aware of his suffering, then he can get rid of it. We must see suffering in ourselves.
Let's say we see a bad person, we start to judge and criticize him. And now for us there is a bad person, whom we look at and whom we judge. And while we look at him, we do not see our suffering. Our suffering is our condemnation, our anger, our disgust, our ill will. If a person really sees these sankharas of his, he wants to stop them. But in order to see them and want to stop them, wisdom is needed. And he must understand at least a little what rest from sankharas is.
When there are few thoughts in the mind, the mind rests. When there are too many thoughts, memories, plans in the mind, when the mind starts discussing and criticizing everyone, it is very difficult. The mind is always discussing something, or planning, or remembering something – all these sankharas that arise from ignorance are suffering. And all these thoughts require energy, that is why we are so tired.
But at the same time we need to understand that states of mind in which thinking is completely absent are not the goal of our practice. Buddha never encouraged such states of mind, he warned against attachment to them. We need to use thinking, but we need to use it correctly, to think wisely (yoniso manasikara) .
Without these sankharas, arising from ignorance, greed, ill will, a person can rest: he can simply drink tea, without chasing old thoughts in circles. Feeling the warmth and hardness of the cup, feeling the smell and taste of tea - this is also awareness. But if a person begins to compare this tea with some other tea, or to plan where and what tea to buy, to think how much it will cost - these thoughts require energy and we do not feel the taste of tea, we thus lose our time.
You can just sit, observe your breath, observe the sensations of your body, observe your posture, your body position. You can walk consciously, observe how your foot rises and falls, how it touches the ground. Observe sensations as sensations, thoughts as thoughts. This will all be meditation. When we see thoughts as thoughts, there is no suffering.
The First Noble Truth is suffering, dukkha , and we must be aware of our suffering. To the extent that we are aware of our suffering, to that extent will we have peace. We can be free from suffering, but as long as we look for the causes of our suffering in the external world, we do not really see it. We must look at ourselves, at our sankharas.
There are different types of sankharas. There are good sankharas (kusala) , for example: kindness, generosity, sympathetic joy. They give us merits that bring good fruits. There are bad, or unwholesome (akusala) sankharas: anger, greed, envy, pride. There are many of them, and they will bring us unwholesome fruits. Sometimes we can consider some sankharas to be good, but if we look at them carefully, there is envy, greed, pride. But to us they may seem like justice or protection for us. We have many obscurations of the mind and they are all suffering.
If you look very deeply, even good thoughts are suffering. But here you have to be very careful and cautious. It happens that people get acquainted with the Teaching a little and think that since Buddha said that you have to leave everything, both good and bad, then you don’t have to practice generosity, kindness, you don’t need morality, and so on. But you will never be free from suffering. Morality (sila) and generosity (dana) are the foundation of our entire practice, we will not be able to build anything if we do not have this foundation, we will not be able to develop our mind without it.
First of all, we must get rid of the unwholesome: greed, anger, hatred, pride, envy and the like. To get rid of this, we must develop good thoughts, good qualities - this is like a medicine for a disease, like an antidote to unwholesome states of mind. When morality, generosity and other good qualities develop, awareness and wisdom will develop with them. And only after this can a person develop supra-mundane wisdom, and only then will a person understand that both good and unwholesome are suffering, and one can free oneself from all this, but this is a very high level.

"Here, bhikkhus, when this man had crossed over and arrived at the far shore, he would think: 'This raft has been of great use to me, for by means of it I have reached the far shore safely, exerting myself with my hands and feet. Why should I not pull it up onto dry land or leave it floating in the water, and then go wherever I please?' Having done this, bhikkhus, he would have done as one should do with a raft. In the same way, I have shown you how the Dhamma is like a raft, the purpose of which is to float across, not to cling to.
Bhikkhus, when you know how the Dhamma is like a raft, you should [then] abandon even the Dhammas, what to speak of things contrary to the Dhammas."

Alagaddupama Sutta: The Simile of the Snake, MN 22

But at the initial level we must see our sankharas, our suffering. Practice helps us to protect our mind. For example, generosity and morality help us to develop good thoughts, good qualities of mind.
Meditation helps us to develop the mind, and to keep the mind on a good object, for example, on kindness. Kindness can also be an object for meditation, and we can develop our mind in this way. When we think about kindness, the five hindrances decrease, and harmony, composure, joy, concentration, one-pointedness of mind appear – good qualities appear. And then a person really feels good. It is as if the house was dirty, untidy, cluttered, and we cleaned it well, the house became clean, tidy, spacious, light, fresh, and you want to live there. A good mind will be the same.
This is samadhi – composure of mind, concentration of mind, harmony of mind, calm of mind. But samadhi can be lost at any moment, because we still have ignorance (avijja) . When samadhi develops, our ignorance simply hides from us, and when ignorance appears again – we can instantly lose our samadhi . Therefore, if we have developed some level of samadhi , we must immediately practice vipassana to see ourselves, our suffering, the causes of suffering, how it arises, how we ourselves create our suffering. Then wisdom appears: understanding how suffering arises, the cause of suffering, deliverance from suffering and the Path leading to deliverance from suffering.
What we do, how we practice must correspond to the Teaching, only in this way can we understand that we are on the right path. And this wisdom will not be lost. Due to ignorance, some unwholesome thoughts may arise, due to which we will lose peace of mind, but thanks to wisdom we will be able to see our unwholesome thoughts as our suffering, and we will want to get rid of it, and we will be able to work with it further. We will not think that the external world is wrong, that the people around us are wrong, society is wrong, and that we need to do something about it.
We always want to get happiness by changing the world. But with wisdom we understand that we have to change ourselves. Suffering arises in me, not somewhere in the world, suffering disappears in me. We have to see this difference and for this we have to realize what sankharas are and how they arise. We have to see it in ourselves. If at least at some level the sankharas that arise due to ignorance subside in us, then we will have happiness. If the sankharas that arise due to ignorance disappear in us altogether, then this will be nibbana: sabba sankhara samatho – the pacification of all sankharas, this is one of the descriptions of nibbana.
During meditation, sankharas are our mental obscurations, our thoughts, our inner sorrows, our sufferings. Or we can say it another way: these are our kammas . We constantly create new kammas , and they constantly influence us. If a person needs to be aware of himself, needs to observe himself, then it is imperative to watch his thoughts, to be aware of sankharas.
May you develop your wisdom, mindfulness and good qualities of mind, may the merits of listening to this Dhamma help you to be free from suffering!

"And what, monks, is old kamma? The eye is old kamma, to be seen as born of volition and as something that is sensed. The ear… the nose… the tongue… the body… the mind—these are old kamma, to be seen as born of volition and as something that is sensed. This is called old kamma.
And what, monks, is new kamma? It is any action that someone now performs through body, speech, or mind. This is called new kamma.
And what, monks, is the cessation of kamma? When anyone attains liberation through the cessation of action by body, action by speech, action by mind, that is called the cessation of kamma.
And what, monks, is the way leading to the cessation of kamma? It is this very Noble Eightfold Path."

Kamma Sutta: Kamma, SN 35.146
