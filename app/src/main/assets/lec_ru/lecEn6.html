  УHe dwells in the contemplation of feelings as feelings, being determined, alert, aware, having eliminated greed and dissatisfaction with the world.Ф

Mahasatipatthana Sutta: The Great Sutta on the Fundamentals of Mindfulness (DN 22 )

This is the gatha from the Satipathana Sutta, let's look at what vedanupassana is . Vedana is sensation. The Buddha says that there are three types of sensations: pleasant, unpleasant and neutral. The Buddha also mentions vedana in Paticca-samuppada ( SN 12.2 ). We can say that paticca-samuppada is cause and effect, or the chain of dependent arising. In the paticca-sammupada, the Buddha explains that vedana is the cause for the emergence of tanha .
Let's take a little look at what vedana is, what sensation is. What are the reasons for the feeling? How does tanha arise from sensation? The word "tanha" in Russian is usually translated as thirst or passionate desire. However, Buddhism has its own terminology, and for many concepts there are no exact translations into other languages, so it is best to formulate your understanding for the Pali word "tanha".
Buddha says there are three types of tanha. The first is kama-tanha , sensual pleasures. Kama-tanha is when a person desires something that will bring him sensory pleasure. For example, we love to eat delicious food, listen to beautiful music, see beautiful things - all this is kama-tanha.
The second type is bhava-tanha . Bhava is existence. For example, when we feel happiness and want this happiness to continue, for it to exist, this is bhava-tanha, the thirst for existence, the desire to exist. For example, every person wants to continue his life. Or when we feel some pleasant sensation, we want it to continue.
The third type of thirst is vibhava-tanha . The prefix УviФ here gives us negation, that is, it is a thirst for non-existence, a desire for something not to exist, or for something to cease. The most obvious example is pain: we want there to be no pain. Or, for example, some loud noise - we donТt want to hear it. Also meeting an unpleasant person Ц we donТt want to see him. This desire not to see this, not to hear, not to feel, not to smell, etc., so that this does not happen - this is vibhava-tanha.
Today our topic is vedana and vipassana. The Buddha says that the cause of tanha is vedana, or sensation: vedana-paccaya tanha . Let us see how vedana becomes the cause of tanha.
Just imagine that there are different types of dishes, we look at them and we want to try some dish - as if we see that it is tasty. Then, when the moment of choice comes, we take this dish, try it, and realize that it is very salty. And after that our desire to eat this dish disappears. Why is this happening? Because when we tried this food and a touch of the tongue appeared, an unpleasant sensation appeared in the body, an unpleasant vedana, and after it we felt disgust. And the reason for this disgust was a sensation. In the end, we don't love food, but sensations. We say: I love pineapple, I love mango, I love durian, but in the end we don't love pineapple, mango and durian - we love the taste. If we look further at why we love this taste, then in fact we donТt even need the taste, but this pleasant sensation that we feel. Therefore the Buddha says that the reason for tanha is vedana, sensation.
There is another interesting point: tanha becomes the reason for upadana . Upadana is usually translated as attachment, clinging. We have many attachments: we are attached to food, to housing, to the country - a variety of attachments. These attachments arose due to tanha, thirst. Because of the desire to receive something, or the desire for something not to be, or because of the desire to preserve and continue this feeling: kama-tanha, bhava-tanha, vibhava-tanha. Because of this, upadna, attachment, appears.
Look: we love taste because we need this feeling, this pleasant sensation. Therefore desire arose and because of this tanha appeared. Tanha came to this taste, and because of tanha came upadana, attachment. And we become attached to the mango, not to the sensation.
The Buddha says that there are five types of attachment: rupa upadana - attachment to form (or materiality); vedana upadana Ц attachment to sensation; sannya upadana - attachment to perception; sankhara upadana - attachment to mental formations; vinnana upadana - attachment to consciousness.
Rupa upadana means attachment to form. For example, if we like mango, then even among many other fruits, we immediately pay attention to mango because of its attractive shape. This happens because we like the feeling. Because of sensation, thirst arises, because of thirst, clinging to form arises: vedana-paccaya tanha, tanha-paccaya upadanan . There are many different types of sensations, but we like certain ones, and we become attached to them and then constantly think: I like this, I love this. For example, I like this kind of music, but I donТt like this kind of music. This means that there are already certain attachments to sensations.
The same is true with perceptions and with everything else. Even when we hear a name, we immediately think: УOh, I like this, I know this, this is good.Ф Even if we just hear a name, attachment immediately appears.
Formations ( sankhara ) follow . We like to think in a certain system. We talk about clinging to mental constructs, sankhara upadana, but it seems to me that it goes even deeper, because there is also ditthi upadana - clinging to views. There are some other false views there. A person can say: УThis is good, this is how it should be done, this is correct.Ф And why does he say that - because he thinks so, he likes it, he is attached to it. The reason for his statements is his own beliefs, his own preferences and attachments.
There is also vinnyana upadana . "Vinnana" is translated as consciousness. Or you can say, this is what we are aware of the world through, through which we are aware of the world. And there is vinnana upadana , attachment to consciousness. Even in our mango example, the end result is vinnana - that which we have realized. And we become attached to this consciousness.
These are the five types of attachment. If we go in the opposite direction, where is the cause? The reason was sensation. This is the most immediate cause for tanha - sensation, vedana. If we go even deeper, to the original cause, then it is avijja , ignorance. It is because of ignorance that all this begins. Therefore, there are two causes for tanha: the proximate cause is sensation, and the distal (or original) cause is ignorance.
In fact, without defeating avija we cannot defeat tanha. A person cannot simply defeat tanha by itself. Let's take the example of food again. Someone likes chocolate, how can he conquer his thirst? After all, tanha is the cause of dukkha , suffering.
There are four Noble Truths. The First Noble Truth is the truth about suffering. The Second Noble Truth is about the cause of suffering; the cause of suffering is tanha. The Third Noble Truth is about the possibility of liberation from suffering: this is nibbana . The Fourth Noble Truth is about the path to nibbana.
Tanha is also mentioned in the Four Noble Truths. Tanha is the cause of suffering. If we consider the tanha in the context of paticca-samuppada, then the tanha has a cause - vedana. Therefore, our goal is to conquer tanha in order to conquer suffering. How to do this?
For example, we have a thirst for chocolate, how can we overcome it? Typically people think there are two options. The first is to satisfy a desire, that is, when a desire for chocolate appears, we take it and eat it: if there is a desire, we must satisfy it. But then the feeling of satisfaction comes at the moment of consumption, and after some time the desire arises again. The desire does not stop: today we feel good, and tomorrow everything will be all over again. ItТs as if our life, or our body, is a fire, and we need to constantly throw wood into it. We cannot stop, and this process never stops.
The second option, as people usually think, is to give up chocolate. It seems to them that they can overcome this thirst with their willpower, simply by refusing to satisfy this desire.
These are the two ends: one must eat or one must not eat. Do you think by giving up chocolate, can we overcome our craving for chocolate? This wonТt work either, because even if we donТt eat chocolate, we still remember it, we see it, we see others eating it, and so on. Even when we just see it or remember it, we have a desire to eat it.
What people donТt do to conquer themselves, their clouds of mind, to calm themselves down, to be satisfied with themselves. For this, people work, earn money, strive for power. Buddha says that even if there was a rain of gold, peopleТs thirst for gold would still not stop. No matter how much we receive, our thirst still remains with us. This region will not take us where we need to go.
And the other side is to move away from sensual pleasures. What people donТt do for this. People practice various asceticism, for example, they isolate themselves from society and go into caves, practice silence there, do not see anyone, do not communicate with anyone. During the time of the Buddha, some people even took a vow to live like a dog: sleep like a dog, eat like a dog. Why did they do this? Because they saw that all human habits are rooted in thirst.
Why do I like this one out of all the clothes? Because the desire is there. Why, of all types of tea, do I like this particular tea? Because there is thirst there. Therefore, people thought that in order to completely overcome thirst, they needed to completely give up these habits. Therefore, people began to live by imitating animals. Or other people thought that they needed to torture themselves: for example, by swimming when it was cold, or sitting by the fire when it was hot. But they did not conquer thirst.
These are the two extremes. Buddha says that both these extremes are wrong. In order to overcome thirst, you need to see its causes - both immediate and original. The proximate cause is vedana, sensation. The original cause, where everything begins, is avijja, ignorance - everything begins from there.
To explain dependent arising (paticca-samuppada), two methods are generally used. One is an explanation through three lives: past, present and future. But dependent arising can also be seen right here and now, in the present moment. This is the explanation I use now.
Avijja-paccaya sankhara : due to ignorance, sankharas arise (can be translated as volitional formations of the mind.
Sankhara-paccaya vinnana : due to the formations of the mind, consciousness arises. What are we aware of? What appears in the mind. What thoughts appear in our minds, such will be our consciousness, then we will know. For example, the thought arises in the mind: УI am a good person,Ф then the mind knows: УI am good.Ф The result is vinnana, the mind knows that УI am a good person.Ф
Vinnana-paccaya namarupa . Nama-rupa means mind and form, or mental and material, or name and form. Because of consciousness, mind and form arise.
Namarupa-paccaya salayatana . Salayatana is the six sense organs. Because of mind and form, the six senses arise.
Salayatana-pacchaya phasso . Phassa is contact. Because of the six senses, contact occurs.
Phassa-paccaya vedana : due to contact, sensation arises.
What does contact mean? For example, sound enters the ear. If there is contact, then a sensation appears. If there is an eye and colors, then contact occurs and then sensation appears. And so with all senses.
The Buddha says that there are three types of sensations: pleasant, unpleasant and neutral. And also the Buddha says that sensation is an object for meditation, we can observe it and develop our awareness.
In the Satipatthana Sutta there are 4 objects for developing awareness, the four foundations of awareness. The first basis is the body ( kaya ), the second basis is sensations.

Vedanasu vedananupassi viharati atapi sampajano satima, vineya loke abhijha-domanassan.

He dwells in the contemplation of feelings as feelings, being determined, alert, aware, eliminating greed and sadness for the world.

Vedanasu vedananupassi viharati

Recognize sensations as sensations.

The word "atapi" means a strong desire to overcome one's mental defilements, "sampajano satima" - wisdom and awareness. A strong desire to overcome one's mental defilements with awareness, without getting carried away by the world and without rejecting the world.
This gatha can be translated as follows: УOne abides, observing sensations as sensations, with the desire to overcome oneТs mental defilements with wisdom and awareness, without being carried away by the world or rejecting it.Ф
We observe sensations in the body, and it seems to us, for example, that this is the head. But the head is actually not just one part; there are myriads of sensations. If a person, observing the sensations of the body, develops awareness, then he will see that a huge number of subtle sensations arise and disappear. They can be pleasant, unpleasant or simply neutral. When a person observes a sensation, he can see that when a pleasant sensation arises, the mind gets carried away with it, wants it to continue. When an unpleasant sensation arises, the mind rejects it and wants it not to exist. But the mind does not notice a neutral sensation as a sensation. It is there, but it is invisible to us. We see the neutral sensation with ignorance, and usually we perceive the neutral sensation as УhandФ, as УheadФ, as УIФ. Therefore, usually a neutral sensation is not visible as clearly as a pleasant or unpleasant sensation, it is a very subtle sensation.
These three types of sensations can be further divided into two categories: samissa vedana and niramisa vedana. Samisa means sensation with defilement of the mind, and niramisa means sensation without defilement of the mind.
These explanations are clearer to those who meditate on sensations, who practice using sensations. When a person observes his body sensations, it becomes clear to him: somewhere there is an unpleasant sensation, but at the same time there is also disgust. And we must see both these unpleasant sensations and the aversion of the mind - this is samsa vedana. LetТs say that when we observe the body and see pleasant sensations somewhere, then we can see not only pleasant sensations, but also the desire in the mind to feel them, the desire for these sensations to continue. These desires are the defilements of the mind.
This way we can constantly see our mind and see how impurities arise in it. And when we observe our body sensations, we can see: there are pleasant sensations, there are unpleasant sensations, but they are accompanied by defilements of the mind.
And now we need УatapiФ: the desire to conquer our defilements, conquer our disgust and our thirst. For this we observe. If we have wisdom and awareness, then we can see that these sensations are impermanent. So when we have wisdom and awareness, then there is no personality in what we observe - it is all impermanent. And then the mind learns to observe sensations as sensations, without the defilements of the mind. The mind sees that there is simply pain. If disgust arises with pain, then suffering of the mind immediately arises.
When physical pain occurs, it is just a sensation in the body. But the moment disgust appears in the mind, suffering of the mind already arises. And then, because of disgust, different, different thoughts appear - this is also suffering. And then, because of these thoughts, our bodily pain increases. And what we have as a result: our pain increases and not only the body suffers, but also our mind.
Therefore, if a person has wisdom and awareness (sampajano satim) he can see: this is not a permanent sensation, it will not always be like this, sensations constantly arise and disappear, arise and disappear, there is no permanence. And it cannot be said that these sensations are УmeФ. There are reasons for the sensation Ц itТs contact. A sensation arose from the contact. Therefore, it cannot be said that the sensation is УIФ. A person sees that sensation in itself is suffering. But if, out of ignorance, you begin to react to a sensation, this increases suffering. With wisdom and awareness one sees sensation as suffering and ignorance as suffering. And he has the desire to overcome his mental defilements. And with this he observes sensations as sensations.
Then he sees that there are simply sensations. And he can observe them simply as sensations, and there is no defilement of the mind in this. This means that there is bodily pain, but there is no mental aversion to it. There is bodily lightness, joy, some pleasant sensations, but there is no desire to continue this, he can simply observe the sensation as it is. And he does not see the sensation as "I".
There is ignorance in sensations, so usually we perceive sensations as УIФ: Уthis is my sensation.Ф Therefore, usually in sensations we see: Уthis is the head, this is the arm, this is the back,Ф but in fact the sensation is just a sensation. If we can see a sensation as a sensation, then the "I" will go away. Parts of the body will disappear - some kind of sensation will simply arise.
Then what is a "head"? "Head" is perception. When I close my eyes, how do I know that I have a leg? How do I know this? Because I feel, because of sensations I know, through sensations I know. And I perceive these sensations as a leg, as a head. And I perceive painful sensations as Уmy head hurtsФ, Уmy leg hurtsФ. If I have even more ignorance, then I begin to think: УHow will I walk later, since my leg hurts so much now, I probably wonТt be able to walkФ - and thatТs where personality arises. This feeling is combined with ignorance.
If we simply observe a sensation as a sensation, then we can observe niramisa vedana - sensations without defilement of the mind. To see that there are simply sensations. Therefore, at the beginning we simply observe sensations of the body: pleasant, unpleasant, neutral sensations. As the Buddha teaches: vedanasu vedananupassi viharati , to remain observing sensations in sensations.
When unpleasant sensations arise ( dukkha-vedana ), observe these unpleasant sensations as unpleasant sensations. When pleasant sensations arise ( sukha-vedana ), observe these pleasant sensations as pleasant sensations. When neutral sensations arise ( adukkham-asukha vedana) , observe these neutral sensations as neutral sensations.
When we begin to observe this way, we begin to see that there are unpleasant sensations, but along with them there are also mental defilements, aversion.
The Buddha says: if you see samsa vedana, sensations with mental defilements, observe sensations with mental defilements as sensations with mental defilements. Buddha does not say that we need to somehow change them, try to react somehow to them - we donТt need to do anything. We should initially have the desire to overcome our mental defilements, but that is different. If we begin to react to sensations, it means that we create new kamma, new suffering. Therefore, the Buddha says that if there is already a sensation with mental defilement, then observe it as a sensation with mental defilement, and you donТt have to do anything. And further, when there is a pleasant sensation with defilement of the mind, or a neutral sensation with defilement of the mind, observe them as sensations with defilement of the mind.
Then the Buddha says how to observe niramisa sensations, sensations without mental defilements. If there are pleasant or unpleasant sensations without mental defilements, and we see them, then there is no need to be happy or upset, there is no need to react to them. Just observe these pleasant sensations, or unpleasant sensations, or neutral sensations as sensations without mental defilements.
This is what we do during vedananupassana . We develop our awareness, our wisdom. When a person observes like this, he begins to notice and see how everything arises. Then he begins to see not only the sensations, but also how the five aggregates ( pancha khandha ) arise.
For example, when we observe the sensations of the body and some sound was heard. We see: there was some sound and disappeared, and the sensation changed and disappeared. Then there was some kind of perception (for example, a dog), and it also disappeared. There were some subtle sankharas, for example: Уthere is a dog there,Ф or Уsomewhere a dog is barking,Ф and they also disappeared. It appeared and disappeared. And a person observes the emergence and disappearance, then he does not see the outside world, does not see a dog, a person somewhere out there, and so on. He sees how all this arises and disappears in his own mind. This is pure Vipassana, seeing as it is. And in order to practice pure Vipassana, a person must first know how our senses work. This knowledge is the foundation, the basis for practice. If he does not know, as the Buddha explains it, then he will not have enough knowledge for the pure practice of Vipassana and will get lost somewhere along the way. Because you can say: УBut there is a dog there, if I go there, I will see it, I can pet it.Ф
But if a person knows the Dhamma well, and at the same time practices the Dhamma, then he can see: the dog is a concept of the mind, and it arose in the mind. When I look, I can only see colors, the eye does not perceive anything else. The eye is limited to the perception of colors; it cannot see dogs, people, animals. The ear cannot hear people, animals, music: the ear perceives only sounds. When touched, we can only feel hardness, softness and temperature. And a dog is something that arises in the mind. If there is ignorance, then the dog remains, although the senses are constantly changing, arising and disappearing, but at the same time the same constant dog exists in the head. This is bhava, existence.
Therefore, if a person meditates deeply, observes his body sensations, then when he sees only sensations, without the pollution of the mind, or just sensations, then he can still see how the world arises and how the world disappears. This is real Vipassana, very deep Vipassana.
Therefore, observing sensations gives us good results. We develop our mind, educate it. For example, in order to develop vedananupassana , a person sits and observes his body. Sure, sitting in one position for a long time can be painful, but he doesn't run away from it, doesn't let disgust take over, doesn't change his position, practices patience as much as he can. Patience is parami , a virtue or perfection, so the practitioner develops his parami. Parami are good qualities that help us free ourselves from samsara. There are 10 of them in total, all of them develop during Buddhist practice.
Do we always need patience? Now we are sitting and can change our position whenever we want. But if an illness occurs and hospitalization is required, it will be impossible to move. They give us some injections and perform procedures, but we canТt do anything. What do we have left? We can only endure, nothing more. If at such a moment we lack patience and lack wisdom, then what will happen in our mind? There will be disgust, anger, fear, hatred. And what will we have as a result? Bad rebirth. And before that we will suffer and torment ourselves in the hospital, and after death we will be reborn in a bad place.
Through Dhamma study and practice we can prepare ourselves. By observing painful sensations and not reacting to them, we teach our mind to perceive unpleasant sensations simply as unpleasant sensations, without suffering. We usually think that unpleasant sensations are suffering, but this is not so. In fact, these are two different aspects: the sensations themselves and the suffering of the mind.
Of course, at the deepest level, the sensations themselves are also suffering, because sensations are part of samsara and they change all the time, but we canТt do anything about it now. If you look at the paticca samuppada, these sensations arise due to past kamma. We are not creating these sensations now - it is because of some past kamma that these sensations arise, and we cannot change them. But at the same time, if we now begin, out of ignorance, to think Уwhy is this happening to meФ, Уwhy does my leg hurt so much nowФ, Уwhat should I do about itФ, Уhow can I stop thisФ, etc., then we create new kamma. If new kamma appears, new suffering will appear. Because of the past kamma, these painful sensations appeared, and because of the new kamma, new painful sensations will appear in the future, we cannot stop the kamma.
We observe the sensations of the body and learn proper patience. Patience also comes in two types: right and wrong. It is not always good to endure; sometimes it is important to respond adequately to your feelings. If a person is in a lot of pain and wants to cry, then sometimes it is important to express the pain and allow yourself to cry. We understand that if this pain is endured inside, it can come out as a disease of the body, or as some other problem.
What does correct patience mean: correct patience is when there are unpleasant sensations, but we do not suffer from it. The mind does not suffer, does not react to it. For this, a person needs knowledge, he needs skill, he needs awareness. This takes practice. We can get this practice by observing the sensations of the body. This is how we develop patience and equanimity of mind. Equanimity means a calm mind. When a pleasant sensation arises, joyful anxiety does not arise. When an unpleasant sensation or pain occurs, grief and disgust do not arise. There is equanimity, peace of mind, and that is what we learn.
By still observing the sensations of the body, we develop our wisdom. The wisdom to see sensations as sensations, not as УmeФ, not as УmineФ, not as something that belongs to me. A sensation arises because of a cause: as long as there are causes, sensations exist; with the cessation of causes, sensations cease. This is how wisdom develops. Therefore, the Buddha says: this awareness and wisdom that we have gained from observing the sensations of the body helps us further develop awareness and wisdom. Therefore, we must further develop and deepen our awareness and wisdom, using the awareness and wisdom that we already have. And this awareness with wisdom helps us to be completely liberated. To be completely free means to be free from all suffering. Therefore, vedanupassana is useful to any person; any person can practice it and get good results.
I wish you a good practice so that you get the right experience of meditation, and that this experience helps you free yourself from all suffering!
