In Sri Lanka, before each Dhamma teaching, monks often recite this gatha:

 

**Sabbapāpassa akaraṇaṃ,**

Refrain from all evil

 

**Kusalassa upasampadā;**

Cultivate the good

 

**Sacitta pariyo dapanaṃ,**

Purify one’s own mind

 

**Etaṃ buddhāna sāsanaṃ**

This is the teaching of the Buddhas.

 

This gatha is found in the Dhammapada. The commentary provides the following explanation for this verse:

 

While residing at the Jetavana Monastery, the Buddha recited the verses (183, 184, and 185) in connection with a question raised by the Venerable Ananda regarding the core teachings for monks given by previous Buddhas.

One day, the Venerable Ananda asked the Buddha whether the Fundamental Teachings for monks given by previous Buddhas differed from the current ones. The Buddha replied that the teachings of all Buddhas are expressed in the following verses:

Then the Buddha recited these verses:

 

**Sabbapāpassa akaraṇaṃ,**

**Kusalassa upasampadā;**

**Sacitta pariyo dapanaṃ,**

**Etaṃ buddhāna sāsanaṃ**

Not doing evil, cultivating virtue,

purifying the mind — this is the Teaching of the Buddhas.

 

**Khantī paramaṁ tapo titikkhā,**

**Nibbānaṁ paramaṁ vadanti buddhā;**

**Na hi pabbajito parūpaghātī,**

**Na samaṇo hoti paraṁ viheṭhayanto.**

 

The best moral practice is patience and forbearance;

"Nibbāna is supreme," say the Buddhas.

A monk never harms another.

One who harms others is not a monk.

 

**Anūpavādo anūpaghāto,**

**Pātimokkhe ca saṁvaro;**

**Mattaññutā ca bhattasmiṁ,**

**Pantañca sayanāsanaṁ;**

**Adhicitte ca āyogo,**

**Etaṁ buddhāna sāsanaṁ.**

 

Not reviling, not harming, practicing restraint

according to the Fundamental Teachings for monks,

being moderate in eating, dwelling in secluded places,

and dedicating oneself to higher concentration — this is the Teaching of the Buddhas.
