If any bhikkhu, spoiled, angry, dissatisfied, unjustifiably accuses another bhikkhu of violating the parajika class, thinking, “Perhaps I can cause him to fall out of chaste life,” then, regardless of whether he is later cross-examined, if the accusation is unfounded and the monk confesses his anger, this entails the initial and subsequent meetings of the Community.

 This is only the beginning of the origin story of this rule, which is one of the longest and most controversial stories of the Vinaya.
 After Mettiya Bhikkhuni made her accusation, the Buddha called a meeting of the Sangha to resolve the issue with Venerable Dabba Mallaputta.
 He, being one who attained arahantship at the age of seven, answered truthfully that he could not remember ever having indulged in sexual intercourse even in his sleep, much less while awake.
 The Buddha then told the Sangha to expel Mettiya Bhikkhuni and interrogate her instigators, after which he returned to his abode.
 When the monks expelled her, the followers of Mettia and Bhummaji told them: “Friends, do not expel Mettiya bhikkhuni.
 It was provoked by us, we were upset, unhappy and wanted him to fall.”  
 “You mean that you wrongly accused the Venerable Dabba Mallaputta of a parajika class offense?”
"Yes friends". 
“Then the monks indignantly, and complained, and murmured: “How can the followers of Mettiya and Bhummajī unjustifiably accuse the Venerable Dabba Mallaputta of the offense of the parajika class?”
 However, for centuries after the canon was compiled, many people became indignant and complained more about the Buddha's attitude towards Mettiya bhikkhunis.
 According to the commentary, her expulsion was one of the controversial issues that separated the Abhayagiri Vihara monks from the Mahavihara monks located in the former capital of Sri Lanka, Anuradhapura.
 Even modern scholars have objected to the Buddha's treatment of Mittiya bhikkhunis in this way and have interpreted this passage as a "monastic interpretation", as if the Buddha himself was not a monk and the entire Canon was not the work of monks and nuns.
 The commentary states that the Buddha did this because he knew that if he treated her less harshly, the followers of Mettiya and Bhummaji would never reveal that they had incited her to make an accusation, and the truth would never come out.
 This would lead some people to secretly believe that Venerable Dabba Mallaputta was guilty and, due to his being an arahant, it would cause them long-term harm and harm.  
 In any case, what interests us is that at some point after this rule was formulated, the Buddha instructed the Sangha to deal with such accusations, and provided a certain pattern of action so that its judgments would be as fair as possible.
 Since the Vibhanga and the Commentaries to this rule are based on this model, we will first discuss the model itself before considering the special case of unfounded accusations covered by this rule.  

 Comment.
 As the Buddha states in Sg 12, one of the ways by which a monk can hope to advance in his training is through mutual exhortation and mutual restoration.
 If a monk commits an offense, he is responsible for informing his fellow monks so that they can assist him with any procedures that the offense may entail.
 Human nature is such that there are monks who neglect this responsibility, in which case it falls on other monks who are aware of the offense, who can admonish the offending monk in private, if possible, or, if he is stubborn, at a formal meeting of the Community. .  
 Before admonishing a monk, one must first ensure that the other monk is qualified for the role.
 According to Cv. IX. 5.1-2, this means knowing that he:
 1) is pure in bodily behavior,  
 2) is pure in verbal behavior,  
 3) acts out of good intentions and not out of revenge, 
 4) trained in the Dhamma,  
 5) knows in detail both Patimokkhas (for monks and nuns).  
 In addition, this monk decides:  
1) I will speak at the right time and not at the wrong time,  
2) I will speak about what is factual and not about what is not factual,  
3) I will speak softly and not rude.  
4) I will talk about what is related to the goal (attha), and not about what is not related to the goal (this can also mean: about what is related to the task and not about what is not related to the goal) .  
5) I will speak out of good intentions, and not out of internal rejection.  
 Cv. IX. 5.7 and Pv. XV. 5.3 adds that this monk must have five qualities of mind: compassion, concern for the welfare of another, empathy, the desire that the other monk be restored to his rights, and respect for the Vinaya.
 If a monk feels unqualified by these criteria, but he believes that another monk has committed an offense for which he was not punished, he must find a monk who is qualified to bring charges and inform him.
 Not to inform anyone in such a case is to commit pacittiya or derivative offense under Pc 64, except in cases of mitigating circumstances considered under the said rule.  
 The next step, if the monk is qualified to make an accusation, is to select an appropriate time and place to speak with the opponent, for example, when he is not likely to be embarrassed or upset by it, and then the monk should ask permission to speak with him: “Let the Venerable will give me permission.
“I want to talk to you (Karotu āyasmā okāsaṃ. Ahan-taṃ vattukāmo).”
 Accusing him of an offense without asking permission entails duccata (Mv.II.16.1).  
 As for the opponent, he may grant permission or not, depending on his assessment of the person asking, since it is possible that someone may ask for permission without any real reason, just to abuse it.
(This interpretation is followed by the Burmese edition in the corresponding fragment, Mv.II.16.3.
 In other editions in the same fragment it is said that a monk can give permission to another monk after evaluating him.
 However, as permissible, some of the group of six monks ask permission from monks, knowing that they are pure; there is apparently no need to allow a monk to consider whether the person he is planning to accuse is clean or not.
 This is one of the duties of the accuser, as provided in this rule, along with the following rule, Pc 76, and also more. with one fragment in Mv.II.16.3. 
 As for the case when permission is asked by someone who can abuse it, this is already described in Mv.II.16.2, which says that even after another monk has given permission, one must evaluate him before making an accusation against him.
 Thus, in context, the Burmese reading makes more sense: the one who is asked for permission is allowed to evaluate the one who asks before giving him permission to speak.
 If we had not followed the Burmese reading here, neither Vibhanga nor Khandhaka would have given any opportunity to refuse permission to a malicious accuser).
 A monk who asks for permission without any reason, that is, he has not seen his opponent commit an offense, has not heard a reliable account of the matter, and has no reason to suspect anything about it, takes upon himself the duccata (Mv. II.16.3).
 Pv. XV. 4.7 provides further justification for the Burmese reading here, suggesting that there is no need to grant permission to a monk who is:
 1) unscrupulous, 
 2) ignorant,  
 3) not in an ordinary state (for example, he is undergoing atonement for a sanghadise class offense or is under disciplinary sanction ),  
 4) indicates an intention to provoke unrest, or  
 5) does not intend to restore the rights of the monk whom he accuses.  
 Pv. XV. 5.4 also suggests that permission should not be given to a monk who:
 1) is not pure in bodily conduct,  
 2) is not pure in verbal conduct,  
 3) is not pure in livelihood,  
 4) is incompetent and inexperienced, or  
 5 ) is unable to maintain a consistent line of reasoning when asked.  
 If a monk is not unqualified in any of these aspects, he should be willingly given permission to speak.
 Cv. IX. 5.7 says that a monk who is admonished or accused must maintain two qualities of mind: truthfulness and non-irritability.
 The Pahimokha also contains a number of rules providing for punishment for the misbehavior of a monk when he is admonished formally or informally: Sg 12 if he is generally uncompromising, Pc 12 if during formal interrogation he is evasive or refuses to answer (see below), Pc 54 if he acts disrespectfully towards the accuser or the rule he is accused of breaking, and Pc 71 if he makes an excuse for not observing a certain rule of teaching.  
 If both parties act in good faith and without prejudice, these types of accusations can easily be resolved informally.
 If the charge cannot be resolved informally, it should be taken up at a Community meeting so that a decision can be made by the entire group.
 The procedures for this type of formal meeting will be discussed in the sections on the rules of Aniyata and Adhikarana Samatha.
 If a matter is to be raised at a Community meeting during Uposatha, there are additional procedures to be followed as discussed in BMC Volume 2, Chapter 15.
 If a matter is to be raised at a meeting during Pavarana, procedures to be followed are discussed in BMC Volume 2, chapter 16.

 Abuse of the system.
 As shown in the history of the origin of this rule, a monk making an accusation against another monk may act out of resentment and simply fabricate the accusation.
 This rule and the one that follows cover cases where a monk fabricates an accusation against another monk for violating the class of parajika.
 Pc 76 covers cases where a charge of violation of a lesser rule is fabricated.  
 A complete violation, according to this rule, includes four factors.  
 1) Object: another monk, considered a full monk.  
 2) Understanding: the monk perceives him as innocent of the violation of which he is accused.  
 3) Intention: a monk wants to see another monk expelled from the Sangha.  
 4) Effort: The monk makes an unfounded accusation in his presence that he is guilty of violating the parajika class.  

 An object.
 The definition of this factor - "another monk considered to be a full-fledged monk" may sound strange, but it comes from the Kankha Vitarani, which apparently expands on the principle described for the perception factor, explained below, that if one perceives a monk as innocently accused, the fact whether he is truly innocent is irrelevant to the violation of this rule.
 Similarly, Kankha Vitarani seems to argue that if one perceives a monk to be a monk, the fact of whether one is actually a monk is irrelevant to this violation.
 Kankha Vitarani does this for the following reason: in ordinary cases, the object of this rule will be an innocent monk, but there may be cases where a monk has actually committed a parajika class offense that no one knows about; instead of leaving monasticism, he acts as if he is still a monk, and everyone else continues to think the same.
 But even such a “monk” will meet this factor in relation to this rule.  
 For example, Monk X steals something from the monastery's supplies, but no one knows about it, and he continues to act as if he were a monk.
 Later, Monk Y harbors a grudge against him and makes an unfounded accusation that he had sexual intercourse with one of the parishioners of the monastery.
 Even if X is not actually a monk, the fact that people generally consider him to be one will mean that the factor is satisfied. 

 Understanding.
 If one perceives the monk whom he accuses of parajika as innocent of a given offense, this is sufficient for this factor to be satisfied, regardless of whether the accused is actually innocent or not.
 It is not an offense to bring an accusation based on the belief or suspicion that the accused is not innocent.  

 Intention.
 The formulation of the rule of training suggests that this factor must be fulfilled in a fit of antipathy, together with the motive - the desire to expel another monk, but Vibhanga consistently combines these two sub-factors into one motive.
 Thus, all that is needed to fulfill this factor is the desire to see the other monk expelled.
 If a monk wishes simply to insult another monk, Vibhanga says that such actions will fall under Pc 2. If one wishes to see him expelled and insult at the same time, then he takes upon himself both sanghadisesa and pacittiya.
 The texts do not explicitly mention this point, but it appears that if someone has such a strange sense of humor and makes a false accusation as a joke, without intending to offend or be taken seriously, then his actions would fall under Pc 1.  
 According to Vibhanga , admitting one's antipathy simply means admitting that the accusation was empty or false.
 Thus, the level of anger that motivates one to want to see another monk expelled does not have to be serious: if one wants to see him expelled simply for his own pleasure, this will fulfill the factor of intention.  

 An effort.
 The act covered by this rule is to make an unfounded charge of parajika in the presence of the accused.
 Whether someone does it themselves or entrusts it to someone else, the punishment will be the same.
 If this "someone" is also a monk and knows that the accusation is unfounded, he too takes full responsibility.  
 Vibhanga defines an unfounded accusation as one that is not based on anything seen, heard or suspected.
 In other words, the prosecutor did not see the accused at the time of the commission of the offense in question, and did not hear anything reliable about it, and there is nothing in the behavior of the accused that could give rise to any suspicion.  
 The concepts of "seeing" and "hearing", according to the commentary, also include the use of clairvoyance and clairaudience, which may have been developed through meditation.
 Thus, if one accused X of committing a parajika offense based on what he saw in visions, this would not be considered an unfounded accusation, although one should be careful to clearly understand from the outset what kind of vision the accusation is based on.  
 If there are actually grounds, but someone changed the status of the evidence, the punishment is the same.
 Substitution of status means that one claims, for example, that he saw something when in fact he only heard about it or suspected it, or claims that he saw it clearly when in fact he saw it unclearly.
 Example from the Commentary: Monk X goes to the grove to relieve himself.
 Mrs. Y goes for some reason to the same grove.
 Someone sees them leaving the grove at about the same time, which may be considered grounds for suspicion, but someone accuses Monk X, claiming that he actually saw him having sex with Lady Y.
 This would be considered an unfounded accusation.
 Another example: at night in the dark, someone sees a man stealing something from a monastery warehouse. He looks vague, similar to Monk Z, but one can't be sure. However, someone backs up his accusation by saying that he definitely saw Monk Z steal the item.
 Again, this will be considered an unfounded accusation.  
 The commentary states that for a false charge to be made under this rule, it is necessary either (a) to clearly state the precise act allegedly committed by the accused (for example, having sexual intercourse that caused a woman to have an abortion) or (b) to state that the accused is guilty of parajika, or (c) declare that the accused is no longer a true monk.
 If someone simply says or does something that might indicate that the accused is no longer a monk, such as refusing to show him respect according to his seniority, this is not considered an accusation.
 The commentary adds that charging a monk with an offense equivalent to or derived from parajika, as stated in the conclusion to the previous chapter, would also fulfill this factor.
 For example, if someone unreasonably accuses Monk A of killing his father before he was ordained as a monk, this would be considered a complete violation.
The Vibhanga does not mention these parajika equivalents in this rule, but the Great Standards can be used to justify their inclusion here.  
 All the accusations exemplified in the Vibhanga are made directly to the accused: “I saw that you committed a parajika violation,” “I heard that you committed a parajika violation,” and the Commentary, from this, concludes that full violation occurs only when someone makes an accusation in the presence of the accused, in accordance with the model of admonition discussed above.
 He also argues that making an unfounded accusation behind the back of the accused attracts duccata.  
 There is nothing in the Vibhanga to indicate that the Commentary is wrong in this case, except that since the accusation is unfounded, it may attract pacittiya for deliberate falsehood.
 Some, however, object to the Commentary's position, saying that dukkata or even pacittiya is a very mild punishment for covert character assassination.
 However, we must remember that proper charging procedures require that a serious charge be made in the presence of the accused.
 If a monk spreads gossip about another monk, accusing him of having committed parajika, he should be asked whether he has already discussed the matter with the accused.
 If not, he should be told to talk to the accused before telling anyone else.
 If he says that he does not feel qualified or that he is afraid that the accused will retaliate, he should be advised to take the matter up with the monk who will be responsible for calling a meeting of the Community.
 If he refuses to do this, he should not be listened to.  
 For some reason, the Commentary states that an accusation made in writing does not count, although an accusation made with a gesture, such as someone pointing at the accused when asked who committed the parajika, does.
 Perhaps in those days written accusations were considered too cowardly to be taken seriously.  
 The rule seems to require the accuser to admit that he acted from evil motives, although Vibhanga argues that this simply means he admits that the accusation was false.
 The Commentary goes on to say that the rule here refers to the point at which the rest of the Community knows that the monk making the accusation is guilty of sanghadises: he has actually committed an offense by making the accusation.  
 Kankha Vitarani adds "result" as an additional factor of the offense under this rule, saying that the accused must immediately understand the charge, but Vibhanga does not in any way support this additional factor.  
 Whether anyone actually believes the accusation is not a factor here.  

 Actions that are not violations.
 If one believes the accused to be guilty of parajika and sincerely accuses him based on what he saw, heard or suspects, then, regardless of whether he is guilty or not, one does not commit a violation.
 However, even in such a case, if one makes an accusation without asking the permission of the accused, he takes upon himself dukkata, and pacittiya, if he makes an accusation, to insult him.   Summary: Making an unfounded charge of violating the parajika class on a monk in the hope that he will leave the monkhood is a violation of the sanghadise class.
