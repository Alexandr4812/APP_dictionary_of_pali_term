3. Third rule of training

versions of Bikku Brahmali

by - Kheminda Samanera

One day the Buddha stayed at Vesali in a hall with a pointed roof in the Great Forest. At that time, the teacher spoke to the monks about unattractiveness in many ways - he praised unattractiveness, praised the development of the mind in unattractiveness, and praised the achievement of unattractiveness.

Then the Teacher turned to the monks: “Monks, I want to retire for six months. No one should approach me except those who bring me food to offer.

“Yes, venerable one,” they answered, and no one approached him except the one who brought him offerings.

The monks thought, “The Master has spoken a lot about unattractiveness,” and they devoted themselves to developing the mind in unattractiveness, in its many different aspects. As a result, they began to be concerned about their own bodies, ashamed of them, and disgusted with them. Just as a young woman or man - someone who loves jewelry, who has just washed her hair - would be ashamed, base and disgusting if the corpse of a snake, dog or man were hung around her neck, this is exactly how the monks were concerned about their own bodies. They committed suicide, took each other's lives and approached Migalandika, who looked like an ascetic, and said, “Please kill us. This cup and robe will be yours." And hired for a cup and clothes, Migalandika killed many monks.

He took [and carried] his bloody knife to the Waggumuda River, and while washing it, he became agitated and repentant: “This is really bad for me; I have done many evil deeds, killing virtuous monks. »

Then, walking on the water, the god from the kingdom of the lord of death (mara) said to Migalandika: “Well done, sir, you are really lucky. You have done a lot of merit because you have transferred (to the other shore) those who have not yet crossed over.”

And Migalandika thought, “So, apparently, I am lucky that I have done (accumulated) a lot of merit.” Then he went from dwelling to dwelling, from plot to plot and said: “Who has not yet crossed over? Who should I transport? "The monks, who still had sensual desire, became frightened and frightened, their hair standing on end. Only those who were free of desires were not affected.

Then in one day Migalandika killed one monk, two monks, three, four, five, ten, twenty, thirty, forty, fifty and even sixty monks.

At the end of this half of the month, the teacher came out of seclusion and asked Venerable Ananda: “Ananda, why has the order of monks diminished so much?”

Ananda told him what had happened and added: “Venerable One, please give another teaching to establish the order in final knowledge.”

“Well, Ananda, gather all the monks who live near Vesali in the meeting hall.” "Fine". When he had done this, he approached the teacher and said: “Venerable, the order of monks has been assembled. Please do as you see fit."

Then the teacher went to the meeting hall, sat down on the prepared place and addressed the monks:

“Monks, when calmness is developed and cultivated through mindfulness of the breath, it becomes a peaceful and sublime, satisfying state of bliss. And it stops and calms down bad, unwholesome qualities whenever they arise. Just as a strong, unseasonal storm in the last month of the hot season stops and settles into dust and dirt in the air, so when calm through mindfulness of breathing is developed and cultivated, it becomes peaceful and sublime, and it stops and calms in place bad, unwholesome qualities whenever they arise.

And how is calm developed and cultivated through mindfulness of breathing?

The monk sits down in the wilderness, at the foot of a tree or in an empty hut." He crosses his legs, straightens his body, and sets mindfulness in front of him. Completely attentive, he inhales; completely attentive, he exhales.

Taking a long breath, he knows it; taking a long breath, he knows it. Taking a short breath, he knows it; taking a short breath, he knows it. As he inhales, he trains himself to become fully aware of the breath; As he exhales, he trains himself to become fully aware of the breath. By inhaling, he trains himself to calm the activities of the body; by exhaling, he trains himself to calm down the activities of the body.

Inhaling, he trains, experiencing joy; exhaling, he trains, experiencing joy. By inhaling, he trains himself in the experience of bliss; by exhaling, he trains himself in the experience of bliss. By inhaling, he trains himself to experience the activity of the mind; exhaling, he trains himself by experiencing the activity of the mind. By inhaling, he trains himself in calming the activity of the mind; exhaling, he trains himself in calming the activity of the mind.

By inhaling, he trains himself in experiencing the mind; exhaling, he trains himself in experiencing the mind. By inhaling, he trains himself to please the mind; by exhaling, he trains to please the mind. By inhaling, he trains himself to calm the mind; exhaling, he trains himself to calm his mind. By inhaling, he trains himself to free the mind; exhaling, he trains himself to free the mind.

By inhaling, he trains himself in the contemplation of impermanence; by exhaling, he trains himself in the contemplation of impermanence. Inhaling, he trains himself in the contemplation of extinction; exhaling, he trains himself in contemplation of extinction. By inhaling, he trains himself in the contemplation of cessation; by exhaling, he trains himself in the contemplation of cessation. Inhaling, he trains himself in the contemplation of abandonment; by exhaling, he trains himself in the contemplation of abandonment.

Monks, when tranquility through mindfulness of the breath is developed and cultivated in this way, it becomes a peaceful and sublime, satisfying state of bliss, and it stops and calms in place bad, harmful qualities whenever they arise.”

The teacher then assembled the Order of monks and asked them: “Is it true, monks, that some monks committed suicide, killed each other and said to Migalandika: “Please kill us; will this cup and robe be yours?

"It's true, teacher."

The Buddha criticized them: “Monks, this is not suitable for these monks, this is not right, this is not worthy of an ascetic, this is unacceptable, this should not be done. How could these monks commit suicide, kill each other and say to Migalandika: “Please kill us; will this cup and robe be yours? This will not inspire confidence in those who do not have kyo... And, monks, this rule of training should be stated as follows:

Preliminary ruling

“If a monk deliberately kills a person or seeks an instrument of death for him, he is also excluded, and not in general.”

Thus the teacher established this training rule for monks.

Second sub-story

One day a layman was sick. He had a beautiful and pleasant wife, and the monks in the group of six fell in love with her. They said to each other: “As long as this layman is alive, we will not get this woman. Let us praise death before him."

Then they went to this follower and said: “You have done what is good and useful; you have created a refuge from fear. You didn't do anything wrong: you weren't greedy or immoral. What is the meaning of this miserable and difficult life? Death is better for you than life. When you leave, you will be reborn in a happy place, heaven. There you will be able to enjoy the five kinds of heavenly sense pleasures.”

The worldly devotee thought: “The Venerable Ones have spoken the truth regarding the fact that I have done what is good and avoided what is bad, and that after death I will be reborn in a happy place.”

After this, he ate various types of junk food and drank junk drinks, and as a result, he became seriously ill and died.

But his wife complained and criticized these monks: “These Saki ascetics are shameless and immoral liars. They claim to live according to the truth, are celibate and well behaved, truthful, moral and have good character. But they do not have the qualities of an ascetic or a Brahmin; they are out of their minds. They praised my husband's death, and because of them my husband died."

And other people complained and criticized them in the same way.

The monks heard the criticism of these people. Those monks who had few desires and [had] a sense of conscience, who were content, fearful of wrongdoing, and willing to learn, complained and criticized these monks: “How could they praise death to this worldly follower?”

After they criticized these monks in various ways, they informed the teacher...

After they criticized these monks in various ways, they informed the Master...

“Is it true, monks, that you praised the death of this worldly follower?”

"It's true, teacher."

The Buddha reproached them: “Foolish people, this is not suitable, this is not right, this is not worthy of an ascetic, this is unacceptable, this should not be done. How could you praise death to this worldly follower? This will not create confidence in those who do not have it... So, monks, this rule of training should be stated as follows:

Final ruling

'If a monk deliberately kills a person, or seeks an instrument of death for him, or praises death, or encourages someone to die, saying: 'Good man, what is the meaning of this miserable and difficult life?' Death is better for you than life! “- thinking and intending in this way, if he praises death in various ways or encourages someone to die, he is also excluded and not in community.”

Definitions

Any : whoever, ... Monk: ... A monk who has been given full ordination by a full order through a formal procedure consisting of one movement and three declarations of certainty and fitness for ordination - this type of monk is implied in this case.

Intentionally: knowing, perceiving, intending, deciding, he violates.

Man: from the first appearance of the mind in the womb, from the first manifestation of consciousness until the moment of death: in between - this is called “man”.

Kills: ends a life ability, ends it, interrupts its continuation.

Or he is looking for an instrument of death for him: a sword, dagger, arrow, club, stone, knife, poison or rope.

Or he praises death: he shows the shortcomings in life and speaks in praise of death.

Or encourages someone to die: he says: “Kill yourself with a knife / eat poison / die by hanging yourself with a rope.”

Good person: This is a form of address.

What is the meaning of this miserable and difficult life: miserable life: the life of the poor compared to the life of the rich; the lives of the poor are miserable compared to those of the rich; The life of people is miserable compared to the life of the gods.

A difficult life: the life of one whose hands are cut off, whose legs are cut off, whose arms and legs are cut off, whose ears are cut off, whose nose is cut off, whose ears and nose are cut off. Because of this kind of misfortune and because of this kind of difficult life, someone says, “Death is better for you than life.”

Thinking: Reason and thought are equivalent.

Intention: perceiving death, intention of death, desire for death.

In different ways: in different ways.

He praises death: he shows the flaws in life and speaks praises of death, saying: “When you pass away, with the disintegration of the body after death, you will be reborn in a happy place, in the heavenly world. There you will have access to and enjoy the five types of heavenly sensual pleasures.”

Or encourages someone to die: he says: "Kill yourself with a knife / eat poison / die by hanging yourself from a rope / jump into the abyss / jump into the hole / jump off the cliff."

He too: this is said with reference to previous violations that led to expulsion.

Excluded: Just as an ordinary stone that has been broken in half cannot be put back together again, so a monk who deliberately kills a person is not an ascetic, not a son of the Sakyans, therefore it is said: “he is excluded.”

Not in commonality: commonality: common formal procedures, the same recitations, the same training - this is called “community.” He does not take part in this - that is why it is called “not in community.”
