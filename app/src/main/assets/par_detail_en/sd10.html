If a monk agitates in favor of a split in a single community, or if he is stubborn in raising the issue leading to a split, the monks should make the following remark to him:: "Venerable Sir, do not agitate for a split in a single community or be stubborn in raising the issue leading to a split.
Let the venerable one make peace with the community, because a single community, whose members are on good terms, without disagreements, with a common recitation, lives peacefully. "
And if this monk, who has listened to such a remark from the monks, continues to do his work, the monks should reprimand him no more than three times, so that he stops.
If, after listening to no more than three comments, he stops, all is well.
If it does not stop, it requires initial and subsequent meetings of the monastic community.

Split.
Schism is a serious division of the community, so serious that, if achieved through dishonest means, it is among the five most serious crimes, which also include the murder of a mother, the murder of a father, the murder of an arahant, and the malicious infliction of a bleeding wound on the Buddha (AN 5.129).
To be considered a split, a split must meet five conditions::
1) The community initially remains in unity, that is, it consists of monks of the same affiliation living in the same territory.

2) There are at least 9 monks in this community.
3) A dispute arises in the community about any of the 18 grounds for a split.
other words, one of the parties agitates in favor of any of the following positions, consisting in an explanation:

  • The teachings of the Buddha as something that is not a Teaching
  • that which is not the Teaching of the Buddha, as that which is the Teaching of
  • monastic discipline as something that is not a monastic discipline
  • that which is not a monastic discipline, as that which is
  • not said by the Buddha as said by him said by the Buddha as not said by him
  • not performed regularly by him as performed regularly by him
  • performed regularly by them as not performed regularly by them
  • unformulated by him as formulated
  • formulated by him as unformulated
  • violations as what is not a violation
  • not a violation as a violation
  • serious violation as a minor violation
  • minor violation as a serious violation
  • violations with a remainder (non-parajika) as violations that do not leave a remainder
  • violations that do not leave a remainder as violations with a remainder
  • serious violations as non-serious violations
  • a minor violation is considered a serious violation.

4) There are at least 4 monks on each side.
5) Disagreements reach the point where two disputing parties conduct separate Patimokkha recitations, Pavarana ceremonies, or other community procedures in the same territory.
The canon records two schisms during the time of the Buddha, one involving monks from Kosambi (see Mv. 10), and the other initiated by Devadatta (CV 7).
two splits started from different motives. Two groups of Kosambi monks thought they were following the Teaching and Discipline, and Devadatta knew he wasn't.
And these two schisms were also committed in different ways — in the case of the Kosambi monks, it was done unilaterally, and in the case of Devadatta, it was done bilaterally. The resolution of these schisms was also different — in the case of the Kosambi monks, there was a complete reunion, in the case of Devadatta — only partial.
As we will see later, the different principles followed in these two splits led to different principles in the rules relating to the topic of the split as a whole.
split is the result of a dispute, but not all disputes, even long ones, will lead to a split.
example here is the dispute that led to the Second Council (CV 12).
Despite heated discussions, there was never a moment when one of the parties began to think about separating and performing community affairs separately in the same territory.
At the same time, even small disagreements can potentially lead to a split.
At the same time, as will be seen later, it is possible to act in favor of a split even before disagreements arise, while at the same time not raising issues around which disagreements may arise.
This and the following rule are designed to nip both of these behaviors in the bud before they can cause a split.
When disagreements have become a serious issue, these rules cannot be used, because at that point the procedures in Cv. IV. 14.16-26 explained in chapter 11 must already be followed.
Questions about what behaviors to follow when a split has already occurred and how it can be stopped are described in Volume 2, chapter 21.

The roots of the split.
According to Cv. IV. 14.4, the act of taking a stand in a dispute can be rooted in unwholesome mental states (greedy, vicious, or deluded) or in beneficial ones (not greedy, not vicious, or deluded).
Given the false nature of the motives for schism, the state of mind of a monk agitating for schism must be unwholesome.
However, it is critically important to identify the way in which his impulses and motivations are unwholesome, because this issue determines his own fate and prospects for successful resolution of the split.
In Cv. VII. 5.3 and Cv. VII. 5.5-6 explain that a monk who successfully performs a schism is destined to remain in hell during the cycle of the universe.
In the commentary to Mv. I. 67, it is added that once the schism has taken place, he is no longer a monk and is subject to exclusion from the monastic order.
1) A monastic community of the same affiliation and living in the same territory is united in the correct understanding of the teaching and monastic discipline.
2) The monk agitates for a schism, speaking in favor of the 18 grounds that give rise to a schism.
3) He considers his explanations or act of schism as not being a teaching, that is, he knows that what he is doing contradicts the teaching, or he has doubts about [this] subject.
4) Nevertheless, he distorts his views and actions, claiming that they correspond to the teaching.
However, if a monk speaks in favor of any of the 18 grounds for creating a schism with the understanding that he is in favor of the teaching and that the schism will take place according to the teaching, then even if he manages to create a schism, he is still a monk, he is not destined for hell and there is a possibility of his reunification with the community and

Strategies for splitting up.
In Chulavagga, two schemes are given, according to which a split can occur.
first scheme, derived from the Devadatta schism and described in Cv. VII. 5.1, is that a schism occurs when differences of doctrine, discipline, or instruction of a teacher are put to a vote in a community of at least nine monks, with at least four monks on each side.
It is added that all participating monks must be non-excommunicated and belong to the same group as a whole (i.e. they do not belong to separate groups in advance and are not excommunicated from the community) and live in the same territory (see volume 2, chapter 13).
If any of these conditions are not met — the issue is put to a vote in a community of less than 9 monks, one of the parties has less than 4 adherents, or the monks are excommunicated, do not have a common affiliation, or do not live in the same territory-a split effort is considered a crack in the community, but not a complete split.

The second scheme, describing the split in Kosambi, but given in Cv. VII. 5.2 (and also in AN 10.35 and AN 10.37), describes the two steps by which a group becomes schismatic.
1) Members of the group favor one or more of the 18 split grounds.
2) On the basis of any of these 18 points, they separate themselves into a separate group by performing a separate Patimokkha recitation, a separate pavarana ceremony, or a separate community procedure.
(XV.10.9) attempts to combine the two schemes into one. It lists five ways in which a split can occur: discussion, announcement, voting, procedure, and recitation.
In the comment, the five ways are explained as 4 steps of the same process (when the last 2 of the five are considered alternative forms of one step).
1) Discussion. A monk who wants to create a schism is in favor of any of the 18 positions listed above.
2) The ad. He announces that he is separating from the community and asks the other monks to decide on their position.
3) Voting. The question is put to a vote in a community of at least 9 monks, with a minimum of 4 on each side.
4) Procedure or recitation.
Monks who choose the side of the schismatic are separated from the others and recite Patimokkha or perform another community procedure separately from the rest.
According to the commentary, a real split is not considered to have occurred until step 4, when a schismatic group conducts community affairs separately in the same territory as the group from which it broke away.
This is consistent with Cv. VII. 5.2, but contradicts Cv. VII. 5.1, so the commentary explains that if voting takes place in a separate meeting of the splinter community, steps 3 and 4 are performed simultaneously and the split is considered complete.
Otherwise, if voting takes place outside the territory, the split is not completed until the seceded part conducts community procedures separately in the same territory as the other part.
However, it is possible that the compilers of Chulawaggi intentionally specified two schemes for the split, because it can occur in two ways: two-way and one-way.
In a two-way split, the schismatic group meets with the group from which it splits and asks everyone to take a position.
This is the scheme given in Cv. VII. 5.1.
For a one-sided schism, the schismatic group gathers itself, announces that it is separating from the rest of the monks in this territory, and conducts the procedures of the community separately from them.
This is the scheme given in Cv. VII. 5.2.

According to this text, this is not an instruction that a learned teacher can give.
However, this argument misses the basic idea of descriptions.
Their goal is to provide conscientious monks with a clear pattern so that they can recognize an attempted schism when they see it.
 Factors of violation.
In the Kankha Vitharani, which analyzes the factors of violation in this rule, only one factor is given — effort, divided into several sub-factors.
However, this rule is also classified there as requiring the participation of understanding or intent for a violation to occur.
Since the understanding factor is explicitly excluded in Vibhanga, this leaves only intention.
The sub-commentary says that intention here means the intention of the offending monk not to recant his behavior after the community has made a comment.
However, in the definition of Vibhanga given to the first sub — factor of effort — agitation for secession-intention is included as an integral part of the effort.
Since the alternative sub-factor-persistence in raising the issue leading to a split-does not include intent in its definition, this rule is best explained as covering two separate but related violations with different factors.
(For other similar cases, see Sanghadises 2, Nissaggia Pacchitia 18 and 24.)
In the first type of violation, there are two factors.
1) Intention: action with the thought: "How to lead them to division, how to make them separate, how to make them a separate group?"
2) Effort:
a) a monk agitates for a split in a single community, i.e. monks of the same affiliation in the same territory
b) [continues do this] even when he has been censured three times through a properly executed community procedure.
In the second type of violation, there is only one factor, which is divided into two subfactors.
1) Effort:
a) The monk persists in raising the issue leading to a split in a single community, i.e. in a community of the same affiliation in the same territory
b) [continues to do so] even when he has been censured three times through a properly executed community procedure.

Effort.
According to Vibhanga, agitation for schism is the search for followers-supporters or an attempt to connect a group of monks [with a common idea] with the above intention.
Perseverance in raising the issue leading to a split is taking a position on one of the 18 points listed above.
two types of effort may overlap — a monk seeking to split off a schismatic group may do so on the basis of any of the 18 points, but this is not necessary.
monk may also try to create a schismatic group in other ways, such as by organizing separate eating events only for his friends (see Paccitia 31).

A stubborn monk may refuse to give up a divisive position even when he is not yet seeking to create a schism.
In fact, the use of this rule is most effective before the two activities have overlapped. In fact, applying this rule is most effective until the two activities overlap.
When a monk has managed to link together a group of monks on one of the 18 grounds for schism, the community will have difficulty reaching a unanimous decision to comment on him, because a potentially divisive group has the right to object to the community's procedure.
Note that, unlike the definition of a single community in Cv. VII. 5.3, the definition given for a single community in Vibhanga does not require that it be unified around a precise understanding of teaching and discipline.
This means that in the case of the first type of violation, when a monk tries to create a schismatic group by explaining the discipline in accordance with the discipline in the community whose members have degraded in behavior, the community can still legally reprimand him.
It follows that if a monk wants to organize a return to true teaching and discipline in such a community, he should aim to convert all the members of the community, and not a separate group in it.
If a community recognizes a monk's efforts as contributing to separation, that monk can seek help from other communities, as explained in Chapter 11 and exemplified in the history of the second council, or simply leave that community in search of a place that is better conducive to practice.
If other monks in the community, supporting the views of that monk, come to the new place themselves, then all is well.
However, this rule states that the goal of a monk in explaining the teaching and discipline should never be to create a separate group (=split).
Instead, the goal should be to persuade all sincere monks to join in following the correct practice.
Therefore, when leaving the original community, a monk should leave as amicably as possible, so as not to alienate those who should be chosen to appeal to their views.

The procedure.
The Vibhanga says that if monks see or hear about a monk who has started agitating for a split or persistently raises an issue that could lead to a split in a single community, it is their duty to reprimand him three times.
Otherwise, if he / she has not received any comments, he / she is entitled to continue his / her efforts and the violation is not counted against him / her.
If these bhikkhus ignore their duty, a dukkata is awarded to each of them.
The commentary adds that this dukkata counts for any monk half a yojana away who learns of the monk's attempts [to split the community].
addition, it says that you can fulfill your duty under this rule only by coming to this monk personally, and not by sending him a letter or messenger.
(According to the sub-commentary, any monk within a half-yojana radius who is unable to make a comment due to illness or other [valid] reason is exempt from dukkata.)
As for any monk outside the half-yojana radius, even if he is not credited with dukkata, the commentary says that he should still consider it his duty to make a comment if he is able to go and make it to the initiator of the schism.
If an attempt [to split a community] occurs during the rainy season, the rest of the monks are allowed to interrupt their stay elsewhere in order to help prevent the attempt [to split] (Mv. III. 6-9).
However, as soon as a monk has fulfilled his duty [=made three comments] and at the same time sees that a split is still likely, he is allowed to leave the community even during the rainy season, if he does not wish to be present during the commotion that may follow (Mv.III.11.5).
If, after listening to the remark three times, the initiator abandons his attempts, i.e. stops campaigning for a split or refuses to take a position on the 18 points leading to a split, the penalty is not counted and no other action is required.
However, if he is still stubborn, dukkata is counted.
next step is to bring him to an official community meeting (the comment says that if necessary [to deliver], you need to grab his hands and feet) and give him an official remark three more times.
If he gives up his efforts until the end of the third remark, all is well.
If not, he is awarded a dukkata.
The next step is to read him a formal comment on behalf of the community, using the formula of one petition and three announcements given in Vibhanga.
If he persists, he is awarded another dukkata at the end of the petition, a thullaccaya at the end of each of the first two announcements, and a sanghadisesa at the end of the third.
When a complete violation is committed, the penalties awarded in the previous stages are canceled.
 

Understanding.
The Vibhanga states that if the remonstrance procedure is carried out correctly, i.e. a monk really seeks to split off a group or raises an issue that contributes to a split, and if all possible other formal requirements for a valid procedure are met, then if he does not give up his efforts, he is counted as a full-fledged sanghadisesa, regardless of whether he considers the procedure performed is proper, not proper, or questionable.
If the procedure is performed improperly, then regardless of how he understands its reality, he is credited with dukkata for not abandoning his efforts.
The fact that a monk is not free from violation in the latter case is important: There are several other similar passages in monastic discipline, such as the Buddha's advice to a teaching specialist in the dispute that occurred in Kosambi (Mv. X. 1. 8). There, for the sake of harmony in the community in cases that threaten to split, the Buddha advises monks to abandon controversial behavior and submit to the will of the community, even if it seems unfair.

 Actions that do not constitute violations.
In addition to the traditional exceptions, the list of actions that do not constitute violations includes those when the monk has not been made comments or he refuses his efforts (until the third comment is completed).

 
Next steps.
If a monk is so stubborn that he refuses to give up his attempts at schism even after the third remark, he will most likely not recognize that the community has acted appropriately, in which case he will not recognize the sanghadisesa credited to him or the need to undergo a redemptive procedure for it.
If the community deems it necessary, it has a clear reason to excommunicate him in the very place (volume 2, chapter 20).
fact, this may have been the original intention behind the procedures described in this and subsequent rules of the sanghadisesa: to give the community a clear opportunity to check how stubborn a monk is who seeks to make a schism or is stubborn, and to annul his membership in the community if his stubbornness is confirmed.
For this reason, a community that plans to apply any of these rules to a member should be prepared to read out a procedural excommunication statement for that member as well.
As soon as the violator's membership in the community is revoked, he has no right to contact any member of the community at all.
The fact that a monk does not belong to the community suggests that he can cause at most a rift in the community, and not a full-fledged split.
course, this may not put an end to his efforts to split, but the very fact that the congregation has gathered to deal with this situation should be enough to warn conscientious monks that he is following a bad path and this should help unite the community against his efforts.
If they consider it necessary to deter the laity from leaning in favor of the schismatic's arguments, they can authorize the act of informing one or more monks, [informing the laity] that the schismatic has committed this violation (see pachittia 9) and explain the reasons.
If they are unrepentant and decide to go somewhere else, they can send a message to the community they are trying to join.
course, if it turns out that the schismatic was actually right in his explanation of the doctrine and discipline, the efforts of the original community will draw unsightly attention to its own behavior.
This means that the community should reflect on its own behavior before implementing this rule.
All this shows why the split is treated so seriously: As the Buddha says in the second lesson on future dangers (AN 5.78), it is difficult to find time to practice when the community is caught up in this kind of discord.
Summary: To be stubborn after the third announcement of an official remark at a community meeting in an attempt to form a schismatic group or raise an issue that could lead to a split is a violation of the sanghadisesa class.
