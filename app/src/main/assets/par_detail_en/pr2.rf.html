Original story

First sub-story

 

One day Buddha stopped at Rajagaha on the top of Vulture Peak Mountain. At that time, several monks who were friends built huts on the slope of Mount Isigili and stayed there during the wassu (monsoon season). Among them was the venerable Dhaniya, the son of a potter. Three months later, when the rainy season ended, the monks demolished their grass huts, removed the grass and wood, and set out to wander the country. But the venerable Dhaniya also spent winter and summer there.

Then, one day, when the venerable Dhaniya was in the village collecting food from going for offerings, some women who were collecting grass and wood demolished the grass hut and took away the grass and wood. The second time Dhaniya collected grass and wood and created a grass hut, and again the hut was destroyed in the same way. The same thing happened a third time.

Dhaniya thought, “This happened three times. But I am well trained and experienced in my own pottery making. Why don’t I mix up the mud myself and make a hut out of just clay?”

Then Dhania did just that. He collected grass, wood and cow dung and burned his hut. It was a beautiful and charming little hut, and it was red like a scarlet rain mite. And when struck against it, it sounded like a bell.

It was then that the teacher, descending from Vulture Peak with several monks, saw this hut. He said to the monks: “Monks, what is this beautiful and charming thing, red like a scarlet rain mite?” The monks informed the teacher, and the Buddha criticized Dhaniya:

“This is not suitable, monks, for this fool, this is not proper, this is not worthy of an ascetic, this is unacceptable, this should not be done. How could he make a hut out of just clay? Of course, this stupid person has no respect, compassion or mercy for living beings. Go, monks, and destroy this hut. Don't let future generations engage in the destruction of living beings. And, monks, you should not make a hut consisting only of clay. If you do, you will have committed a violation of misconduct."

“Yes, Venerable One,” answered the monks, and they went to that hut and destroyed it.

Dhaniya asked them, “Why are you destroying my hut?”

"The teacher asked us."

“Destroy it, if the Blessed One says so.”

Then Dhaniya thought: “Three times when I was in the village collecting offerings, women collecting grass and wood demolished my hut and took away the grass and wood. And now my mud hut was destroyed at the request of the teacher. But the forest keeper is my friend. Why don't I ask him for wood and make a hut out of it?

Dhaniya then approached the forest guard and told him what had happened, adding, “Please give me some wood, I want to make a wooden hut.”
“There is no tree, venerable one, that I could give you. This wood is in the possession of the king and is intended for the repair of the city and is set aside for emergencies. You can only take it if the king gives it up.
"She was given away by the king."
The forest guard thought: “These Saki ascetics live according to the truth. They are celibate and have good behavior and are truthful, moral and have good character. Even the king believes in them. These honorable people would not say that something was given if it were not so.” And he said to Dhaniya:
"You can take it, Venerable One." Dhaniya then broke this tree into pieces, moved it with the help of carts and made a wooden hut.
Soon after this, the Brahmin Vassakara, the chief minister of Magadha, while inspecting the work at Rajagaha, approached the keeper of the forest depot and said to him: “What is happening? Where is the wood that the king owns, intended for repairing the city and set aside for emergencies? »
“Sir, this wood was given by the king to the Venerable Dhaniya.”
The Brahmin Vassakara was upset and thought, “How could the king give this wood to Dhaniya, the son of a potter?”
Then the Brahmin Vassakara approached King Seniya Bimbisara of Magadha and said: “Is it true, sir, that the wood in the possession of the king, intended for the repair of the city and kept aside for unforeseen circumstances, was given by the king of Dhaniya?” the potter's son?
"Who said that?"
"Lumber keeper, sir."
“Well, Brahmin, send for the forest guard.” And Vassakara tied up and took by force the caretaker of the forest storage 
Dhania saw the forest keeper tied up and taken by force and said to him, “Why did this happen to you?”
"Because of this timber business, venerable one."
“Go, and I will come too.”
“Please come before I am finished.”
Then Dhaniya went to the residence of King Bimbisara and sat down at the appointed place. The king approached Dhaniya, bowed to him and sat on one side. Then he said: “Is it true, venerable one, that I gave you the wood that the king owns - intended for the repair of the city, and set aside for unforeseen circumstances?”
"Yes, great king"
“We kings are very busy - we can give and not remember. Please remind me, Hon.
Do you remember, great king, when you were first anointed as king, you uttered these words: “Let ascetics and Brahmins enjoy the gifts of grass, sticks and water?” »
“I remember, hon. There are ascetics and brahmins who have a sense of conscience, who are afraid of committing a crime and want to train. They have a sense of conscience even regarding small matters. What I said was intended for them, and it concerned something that was ownerless and found in the wilderness. And yet, venerable sir, do you imagine that you can take wood not given to you by this trick? But how can I afford to imprison or banish an ascetic or a Brahmin living in my kingdom? Go, you are free because of your status, but don't do it again."
But people grumbled and complained: “These Saki ascetics are shameless and immoral liars. They claim to live according to the truth, are celibate and well behaved, truthful, moral and have good character. But they do not have the qualities of an ascetic or a Brahmin; they lost them. They even deceive the king - what to say about other people?
The monks heard the complaints of these people. Monks with few desires, who had a sense of conscience, who were content [with little], were afraid of committing offenses and wanted to train, complained and criticized Venerable Dhaniya: “How could he take wood that belonged to the king, which was not given to him by him? »
After criticizing Dhaniya in many ways, they reported it to the teacher. Then the Master called the Order of Monks and asked the Venerable Dhaniya: “Is it true, Dhaniya, that you took wood that belonged to the king, which was not given to you?”
"It's true, teacher."
The Buddha reproached him: “Foolish man, this is not suitable, this is not right, this is not worthy of an ascetic, this is unacceptable, this should not be done. How could you take wood that belonged to the king and was not given to you? It won't build trust in those who don't have it, and it won't increase trust in those who have it, but it will undermine trust in those who don't have it, and cause some with trust to change their minds."
At that time, sitting next to the teacher was a former judge who took monastic vows along with the monks. The teacher said to him: “For what amount of theft does King Bimbisara beat, imprison or banish a thief?”
“For a pada coin, teacher, for the value of a pada or more than for a pada.” At that time in Rajagaha, a pada coin was equivalent to five masaka coins.
After criticizing Venerable Dhaniya in various ways for being difficult to maintain... “And, monks, this rule of teaching should be stated as follows:
Preliminary ruling
If a monk, intending to steal, takes what was not given to him - such theft for which the kings, having caught the thief, beat him, put him in prison or expel him, saying: “You are a bandit,” you are a fool, you have lost your way, you thief,” he is also excluded from the community
Thus, Master established this training rule for monks.
Second sub-story
One day, the monks from a group of six went to the cloth dyers, took the dyers' things to the monastery and divided them. The other monks said: “You [apparently have accumulated] great merit, since you have received so much cloth for robes.”
“What merit do we have? We just went to the fabric dyers and took their things.”
“Didn’t the teacher set the rules for learning? Why then did you take the dyers' things?
“It is true that the teacher has laid down a teaching rule, but it concerns the village, not the wilderness.”
“But it's the same thing. This is not suitable, this is not correct, this is not worthy of an ascetic, this is unacceptable, this should not be done. How could you take the dyers' things? This will not create confidence in those who do not have it, and will not increase confidence in those who have it, but it will undermine the confidence of those who do not have it, and cause some with confidence to change their minds.”
After they criticized these monks in various ways, they reported this to the teacher.
The teacher called an order of monks and asked those monks: “Is it true, monks, that you went to the cloth dyers and took the dyers’ things?”
"It's true, teacher."
The Buddha reproached them: “This is not proper, stupid people, this is not right, this is not worthy of an ascetic, this is unacceptable, one should not do this. How could you go to the fabric dyers and take their stuff? It won't build trust in those who don't have it, and it won't increase trust in those who have it, but it will undermine the trust in those who don't have it, and cause some with trust to change their minds." Then, after criticizing these monks from the group of six in various ways, the teacher spoke condemningly, the difficulty of maintaining... but he spoke with praise... about putting in effort. Having given the teaching on what is right and proper, he addressed the monks... “... So, monks, this rule of teaching should be recited like this:
Final ruling
“If a monk, intending to steal, takes from a village or from the wilderness something that was not given to him, that is the theft for which kings, having caught the thief, beat, imprison or banish him, saying: You are a bandit, you are a fool, you have gone astray, you are a thief,” he is also excluded from the community.”
Definitions
Any : whoever, ... Monk: ... A monk who has been given full ordination by a full order through a formal procedure consisting of one movement and three declarations of certainty and fitness for ordination - this type of monk is implied in this case.
Village: a village with one hut, a village with two huts, a village with three huts, a village with four huts, an inhabited village, an uninhabited village, a closed village, an open village, a disorganized village and even a caravan settled for more than four months is called a "village". "
Village surroundings: closed village: throwing a stone outside by a man of average height standing at the threshold of the village gate; unenclosed village: outdoor stone throwing by a person of average height standing in close proximity to a house.
Wilderness: With the exception of the village and the surrounding area of ​​the village, the rest is called "wilderness".
What was not given: what was not given, what was not released, what was not abandoned; what is guarded, what is protected, what is considered “one’s own”, what belongs to someone else. This is called "what was not given."
Intending to steal: thinking about stealing, thinking about taking.
Takes: takes, carries away, takes away, interrupts movement, removes from its base, does not fulfill the agreement.
Type: pada coin, value of pada or more than pada.
Kings: Kings of the earth, kings of a region, rulers of islands, rulers of border areas, judges, ministers or anyone else who inflicts physical punishment are called "kings".
Thief: one who intends to steal, takes something worth five masaka coins or more than five masaka coins, not given - he is called a "thief".
They will beat you: with your hand, foot, whip, cane, club or mutilation.
Will […] be imprisoned: will be imprisoned by being roped, shackled, chained, confined to a house, confined to a city, confined to a village, confined to a district, confined to custody.
Will […] be expelled: expelled from a village, from a region, from a city, from a country or from a district.
You are a bandit, you are a fool, you have lost your way, you are a thief: this is a reproach.
He too: this is said with reference to a previous violation that entails expulsion.
Excluded: just as a fallen withered leaf is unable to become green again, so a monk who, intending to steal, takes a padu coin, [an object] worth pada or more than pada, not given to him, is no longer an ascetic, no longer a son of a Sakya , so it is said to be excluded.
Not in a community: the same formal procedures, the same recitations, the same training - this is called “Community”. He does not participate in this, which is why it is said “not in the community.”

If he makes them tremble, he commits a serious offense. If he moves them from his base, he commits an offense resulting in expulsion.

If he thinks, "I'll take them on foot," and he makes them move, he commits a serious violation for every foot that moves. When the last leg moves, he commits an infraction that results in expulsion.

Spy: tracking down the goods. If he describes them and says, “Steal such and such goods,” he commits a violation of wrongful conduct. If he makes them tremble, he commits a serious offense.

Protector of Things: A monk who guards goods brought to him. If, intending to steal, he touches something that is worth five masaka coins or more than five masaka coins, he commits an offense of wrongful conduct. If he makes them tremble, he commits a serious offense. If he moves them from his base, he commits an offense resulting in expulsion.

Mutually Agreed Theft: Many agreed together. If only one of them steals items, a violation occurs for all of them, resulting in expulsion.

Organized action: a person agrees to steal before or after a meal, at night or during the day. If he says, “Take these items in accordance with this agreement,” he commits the offense of misbehavior. If the other takes these items in accordance with this agreement, a violation occurs for both, resulting in expulsion. If he takes these items before or after the term of the agreement, there is no violation for the instigator, but there is a violation resulting in expulsion for the thief.

Making a sign: He makes a sign. If he says, “When I wink/raise an eyebrow/nod, steal the item at that sign,” he is committing a misconduct violation. If, on this sign, the other steals the object, the violation entails the expulsion of both. If he steals items before or after the sign, there is no violation for the instigator, but the violation results in an exception for the thief.
